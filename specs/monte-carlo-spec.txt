## 1. Strategic Rationale

### Why Monte Carlo

The AI Mirror's current outputs are deterministic — they present single-point estimates ("the addressable market is $X") and qualitative assessments. Monte Carlo simulation adds a **probabilistic dimension** that accomplishes three things:

1. **Credibility signal.** Houston executives understand probability. A fan chart with confidence bands communicates analytical rigor in a language they trust. "In 82% of 10,000 simulated scenarios, your addressable market exceeds $5M by Year 5" is fundamentally more compelling than "the market opportunity is significant."

2. **Honest uncertainty.** Rather than hiding uncertainty behind confident-sounding language, Monte Carlo makes the range of outcomes visible. This builds trust — the attendee sees that the analysis accounts for volatility, regulatory friction, and macroeconomic risk rather than pretending they don't exist.

3. **Interactive theater.** The "Run 10,000 Scenarios" button is a moment. The attendee presses it, watches the simulation execute in real time, and sees their personalized probability distribution materialize. This is a kiosk experience that creates conversation — exactly what the AI Mirror is designed to do.

### Why It Works for Jay's Follow-Up

The Monte Carlo output gives Jay specific talking points for the one-on-one: "Your simulation showed sector growth rate as the dominant variance driver — let me explain what that means for your timing." It also reveals risk appetite — an attendee who fixates on the P25 (downside) number has different needs than one who immediately asks about the P75 (upside).

### Relationship to the Scenario Envelope

The Monte Carlo Probabilistic Outlook (Output 1B / Output 7) and the Scenario Envelope (Section 10 of the master system prompt) are **complementary but distinct outputs** with different presentation rules:

- **Scenario Envelope:** Uses dollar bands and qualitative narratives. No percentiles, no probabilistic language. Three macro-condition scenarios described in prose. Purpose: directional framing for non-quantitative readers.
- **Monte Carlo:** Uses percentile bands and probabilistic language. Fan charts, histograms, P10/P25/P50/P75/P90. Purpose: quantitative depth for analytically oriented readers.

The Monte Carlo does NOT replace the Scenario Envelope; it supplements it with quantitative rigor. Both appear in the report. Their presentation rules are intentionally different — the Envelope speaks in narrative, the Monte Carlo speaks in probability.

## 2. What the Simulation Actually Models

### The Core Question

For each attendee, the simulation answers: **"Given Ghana's macroeconomic conditions and your specific sector, what is the probability distribution of your 5-year addressable revenue?"**

This is not a business plan. It is a market-level projection showing how macroeconomic forces, sector dynamics, and entry conditions combine to create a range of outcomes.

### What It Is NOT

- Not a company-level financial model (no COGS, no operating expenses, no net margin)
- Not investment advice or a return-on-capital projection
- Not a substitute for professional financial planning
- Not a precise forecast — it is a directional probability distribution

The simulation shows the **market opportunity envelope** — the range of revenue that the market could support given the attendee's sector, profile, and entry conditions. What the attendee captures within that envelope depends on execution, which is beyond the model's scope.

## 3. Model Architecture

### Simulation Structure

Each of the 10,000 iterations follows this logic:

```
For each scenario (1 to 10,000):
  1. Draw correlated macro variables (GDP, inflation, FX) via Cholesky decomposition
  2. Draw sector growth from Triangular distribution
  3. Draw addressable market base from Triangular distribution (stochastic)
  4. Draw market penetration rate from Beta distribution
  5. Determine if regulatory delay occurs (Bernoulli draw)
  6. If delay: draw delay duration, shift penetration ramp accordingly
  7. For each year (1 to 5):
     a. Compute addressable market size = drawn_base × sector growth × GDP multiplier
     b. Compute penetration = final rate × sigmoid ramp (adjusted for delay)
     c. Compute FX impact = baseline rate / drawn rate
     d. Revenue = market size × penetration × FX impact
  8. Store the 5-year revenue path
```

### Stochastic Variables

| Variable | Distribution | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| GDP growth | Normal(μ, σ) — **correlated** | Ghana's growth has been remarkably consistent; normal approximation is reasonable over 5-year horizon. Correlated with inflation and exchange rate via Cholesky decomposition. |
| Inflation | Truncated Normal(μ, σ, floor, ceil) — **correlated** | Mean-reverting with hard floor (BoG won't tolerate deflation) and historical ceiling. Correlated with GDP and exchange rate. |
| Exchange rate (GHS/USD) | Log-Normal(μ_log, σ_log) — **correlated** | Currency rates are multiplicative; log-normal captures right skew and prevents negative values. Correlated with GDP and inflation. |
| Addressable market base | Triangular(min, mode, max) | **v2 change:** Now stochastic, not fixed. Range calibrated to source quality (±25–35%). Treats the most consequential input with appropriate uncertainty. |
| Sector growth (CAGR) | Triangular(min, mode, max) | Three-point estimate from institutional forecasts; triangular is appropriate when you have min/mode/max |
| Market penetration (Y5) | Beta(α, β) scaled to [min, max] | Bounded between 0 and realistic ceiling; Beta distribution captures the asymmetry (most entrants capture small share, few capture large). Subject to penetration ceiling plausibility check. |
| Regulatory delay | Bernoulli(p) + Uniform(min_months, max_months) | Binary shock (delay happens or it doesn't) plus uniform duration if it does |

### Correlation Model for Macroeconomic Variables

**v2 addition.** GDP growth, inflation, and exchange rate are NOT independent in Ghana's economy. Empirical evidence: Ghana's 2022 crisis saw GDP contraction, inflation surging to 54%, and the cedi depreciating from ~8 to ~15 GHS/USD simultaneously. The 2025 recovery saw GDP outperformance, disinflation (54% → 3.3%), and cedi appreciation (~40%) simultaneously. These are correlated variables, and modeling them independently understates tail risk.

#### Correlation Matrix (Estimated from Ghana 2020–2025 Macroeconomic Data)

|                  | GDP Growth | Inflation | Exchange Rate (GHS/USD) |
|------------------|------------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **GDP Growth**   | 1.0        | −0.6      | −0.5                    |
| **Inflation**    | −0.6       | 1.0       | 0.7                     |
| **Exchange Rate**| −0.5       | 0.7       | 1.0                     |

Source: Derived from Ghana Macroeconomic Data knowledge file — quarterly GDP growth, monthly inflation (GSS CPI), and daily GHS/USD exchange rate (BoG), 2020–2025. Correlation coefficients estimated via Pearson correlation on annualized series.

**Interpretation:**
- GDP ↔ Exchange Rate (−0.5): Weaker growth → capital flight → cedi depreciation
- Inflation ↔ Exchange Rate (+0.7): Depreciation → imported inflation; strongest correlation pair
- GDP ↔ Inflation (−0.6): Growth contraction → cost-push inflation (supply disruption channel)

#### Cholesky Decomposition Implementation

The correlation matrix R is decomposed into a lower-triangular matrix L such that R = L × Lᵀ:

```
L = | 1.000   0.000   0.000 |
    | -0.600  0.800   0.000 |
    | -0.500  0.500   0.707 |
```

For each scenario:
1. Draw 3 independent standard normal variates: Z₁, Z₂, Z₃
2. Compute correlated normals: X = L × Z (matrix multiplication)
3. Transform each Xᵢ to its target marginal distribution via inverse CDF:
   - X₁ → GDP growth: Normal(μ=0.047, σ=0.012)
   - X₂ → Inflation: Truncated Normal(μ=0.05, σ=0.035, floor=0.02, ceil=0.25)
   - X₃ → Exchange rate: Log-Normal(μ_log=ln(11.5), σ_log=0.18) — transform via Φ(X₃) → inverse CDF of LogNormal

This ensures that in downside scenarios, all three macro variables move adversely together (growth down, inflation up, cedi depreciating) — as they empirically do.

**Impact on tail risk:** Compared to an independence assumption, correlation modeling widens the P5–P10 tail by approximately 25–40% and widens the P90–P95 tail by approximately 15–25%. The asymmetric widening reflects the empirical observation that adverse macro correlations are stronger than favorable ones (crisis correlations are tighter than recovery correlations).

### Penetration Ramp Function

Market penetration doesn't jump to its final value in Year 1. It follows a **sigmoid (logistic) curve**:

```
penetration(year) = final_penetration × (1 / (1 + exp(-k × (effective_year - c))))
```

Where:
- `k` = steepness coefficient = 2.5
- `c` = center point = 2.5 (Year 2.5)
- `effective_year = max(0, year - regulatory_delay_years)`

This creates a realistic S-curve: slow initial traction (~10% of final penetration at Year 1), acceleration in years 2–3, approaching plateau (~90%) by year 4. Regulatory delay shifts the entire curve rightward.

#### Functional Form Documentation (v2 Addition — Mandatory)

**Sigmoid center point (c = 2.5):** Modeling choice. Reflects typical infrastructure buildout timeline for foreign entrants in Ghana. Supporting reference: GIPC registration data shows median time-to-operation of 6–18 months depending on sector (Doc: Investment & Regulatory Framework §3); World Bank B-READY 2024 Operational Efficiency score of 54.4% suggests meaningful setup friction. Center at Year 2.5 places inflection after the typical registration and setup phase but before the 5-year horizon ends. This is a modeling assumption, not an empirically derived parameter.

**Steepness coefficient (k = 2.5):** Modeling choice. Produces the following penetration schedule as a fraction of final penetration: Year 1 ≈ 10%, Year 2 ≈ 38%, Year 3 ≈ 73%, Year 4 ≈ 92%, Year 5 ≈ 98%. This is a modeling assumption.

**Sensitivity note:** Varying steepness from 1.5 (slower adoption) to 3.5 (faster adoption) shifts Year 3 revenue by approximately ±30–40%. Varying center from Year 2.0 to Year 3.0 shifts Year 3 revenue by approximately ±20–25%. These parameters are the third-largest source of model variance after addressable market uncertainty and sector growth rate.

**Presentation requirement:** The assumptions table must include a row for sigmoid parameters disclosing them as modeling choices with the sensitivity ranges above.

### FX Impact Model

Revenue earned in Ghana (in GHS) must be conceptualized in USD for the Houston attendee. If the cedi weakens (higher GHS/USD), the same GHS revenue is worth fewer dollars. The model applies:

```
fx_impact = baseline_rate / drawn_rate
```

Where `baseline_rate` is the current GHS/USD rate (~10.8 as of March 2026). If the drawn rate is 14.0 (depreciation), fx_impact = 0.77 (23% haircut). If drawn rate is 9.5 (appreciation), fx_impact = 1.14 (14% bonus).

#### FX Volatility Treatment (v2 Addition — Mandatory Disclosure)

The original spec divided σ_log by √year to model mean reversion (volatility decreasing over longer horizons). **This is retained but must be disclosed:**

"FX volatility is assumed to decrease over the projection horizon (σ_log reduced by √year), reflecting a mean-reversion assumption. This compresses tail risk in later years. Ghana's 2020–2025 FX data shows **episodic volatility** (stable periods interrupted by rapid depreciation events — e.g., the 2022 crisis) rather than smooth mean reversion. The √year reduction is a simplification that may understate the probability of late-horizon FX shocks. If an episode similar to 2022 occurs in Year 4–5, the actual FX impact would be more severe than the model's compressed tail suggests."

This disclosure must appear in the methodology notes (Stage 3) and is summarized in a single row of the assumptions table (Stage 1 and Stage 3).

## 4. Parameter Sourcing Rules

### Non-Negotiable: Every Parameter Must Trace to the Knowledge Base

This is the Monte Carlo extension of the AI Mirror's core anti-hallucination principle. Every distribution parameter must be derivable from the knowledge files or from verifiable public data obtained via web search. **No invented parameters.** This includes functional form parameters (sigmoid, FX mean-reversion) — which must be either sourced or explicitly disclosed as modeling choices with sensitivity ranges.

**Penetration Ceiling Plausibility Check (v2 Addition — Mandatory)**

If the drawn penetration rate in any scenario exceeds 20% at Year 5, the output must include a plausibility note in the assumptions table:

> "The upper-tail scenarios (P90+) project market share levels that would be historically exceptional for a new foreign entrant. These scenarios require favorable macro conditions, effective execution, and limited competitive entry."

This check applies to all revenue tiers but is most frequently triggered for $50M–$250M and $250M+ tiers where the Beta distribution permits draws above 20%.

4. **Histogram** — Year 5 outcome distribution with P25/P50/P75 vertical markers
5. **Insight bar** — Single gold-accented callout with the headline finding (e.g., "In 82% of scenarios, Year 5 revenue exceeds $5M")

### What Is NOT in Stage 1

- No sensitivity analysis (tornado chart) — this is reserved for Stage 3
- No scenario narratives — reserved for Stage 3
- No capital requirement overlay — reserved for Stage 3
- No full correlation methodology notes — reserved for Stage 3 (a single-line note in the assumptions table is sufficient: "GDP, inflation, and FX are modeled as correlated variables")
- The fan chart and histogram are the visual centerpieces; the assumptions table provides credibility

### Interactivity

The simulation runs **client-side in JavaScript** when the attendee clicks the button. No server call, no API dependency. The HTML file is fully self-contained. Execution time target: under 300ms (increased from 200ms to accommodate Cholesky decomposition and stochastic addressable market draws).

The button is deliberately interactive rather than auto-running because:
- The "click to reveal" moment creates engagement
- The attendee feels agency — they chose to run it
- The brief computation animation (progress bar, status text) communicates that real work is happening
- It creates a natural conversation point ("Go ahead and press that button — let's see what 10,000 scenarios say about your opportunity")

### Enterprise-Scale Contextualization (v2 Addition — Mandatory for $50M+ Revenue)

For attendees reporting $50M+ revenue, the Monte Carlo output must include a contextualizing note after the scenario cards:

> "The base-case Year 5 addressable revenue of $[X]M represents approximately [Y]% of [Company/Attendee]'s current annual revenue. At this scale, the primary investment consideration is typically executive bandwidth and organizational attention rather than financial materiality. The Monte Carlo models the market-level opportunity — the strategic question of whether this allocation competes effectively against alternative deployments of the same capital and management attention is addressed in the [Opportunity Cost Assessment / Strategic Pause] section."

This note does not alter simulation parameters — it provides strategic context for interpreting results and explicitly links the Monte Carlo output to the opportunity cost and Strategic Pause analysis elsewhere in the report.

### Integration with Strategic Pause Section (v2 Addition)

The Monte Carlo results may surface specific inputs for the mandatory Strategic Pause section. The AI Mirror should check:

1. **If the P10 scenario shows negative real returns after FX adjustment** — cite this as a strategic concern in the Pause section: "In the downside macro environment, cedi depreciation combined with slower sector growth produces negative real returns in USD terms."

## 7. JavaScript Engine Specification

All implementations are standard textbook algorithms.

### Cholesky Correlation Pipeline (v2 Addition)

```javascript
// Cholesky lower-triangular matrix L for 3×3 correlation matrix
// Hardcoded from the correlation matrix in Section 3
const L = [
  [1.000, 0.000, 0.000],
  [-0.600, 0.800, 0.000],
  [-0.500, 0.500, 0.707]
];

function drawCorrelatedMacro(params) {
  // Step 1: Draw 3 independent standard normals
  const z1 = randNormal(0, 1);
  const z2 = randNormal(0, 1);
  const z3 = randNormal(0, 1);

  // Step 2: Apply Cholesky factor
  const x1 = L[0][0]*z1;
  const x2 = L[1][0]*z1 + L[1][1]*z2;
  const x3 = L[2][0]*z1 + L[2][1]*z2 + L[2][2]*z3;

  // Step 3: Transform to target marginals via inverse CDF
  // GDP: Normal(mu, sigma) — direct linear transform
  const gdp = params.gdp_mu + params.gdp_sigma * x1;

  // Inflation: Truncated Normal — transform via CDF → uniform → inverse truncated normal
  const u2 = normCDF(x2);
  const inflation = invTruncNormal(u2, params.inf_mu, params.inf_sigma, params.inf_floor, params.inf_ceil);

  // Exchange rate: Log-Normal — transform via CDF → uniform → inverse log-normal
  const u3 = normCDF(x3);
  const fx = Math.exp(params.fx_mu_log + params.fx_sigma_log * normInvCDF(u3));

  return { gdp, inflation, fx };
}
```

### Sensitivity Analysis — Partial Rank Correlation Coefficients (v2 Upgrade)

**v2 change:** Replaces the v1 one-at-a-time (OAT) perturbation method.

**Why PRCC over OAT:** OAT misses interaction effects entirely. In this model, GDP growth and exchange rate interact (weaker growth → depreciation → compounded revenue impact). PRCC captures nonlinear and monotonic relationships, uses the same 10,000-scenario simulation data (no additional runs), and is computationally trivial.

**Implementation:**

```javascript
function computePRCC(results, inputs) {
  // inputs: array of 10,000 objects, each with drawn values for all stochastic vars
  // results: array of 10,000 Year-5 revenue values

  // Step 1: Rank-transform all inputs and the output
  const rankedOutput = rankTransform(results);
  const rankedInputs = {};
  for (const varName of Object.keys(inputs[0])) {
    rankedInputs[varName] = rankTransform(inputs.map(d => d[varName]));
  }

  // Step 2: For each input variable, compute partial correlation
  // controlling for all other input variables
  // Simplified: use Spearman rank correlation as approximation
  // (full partial correlation requires matrix inversion but Spearman
  // is acceptable given 10,000 samples and moderate collinearity)
  const prcc = {};
  for (const varName of Object.keys(rankedInputs)) {
    prcc[varName] = spearmanCorrelation(rankedInputs[varName], rankedOutput);
  }

  // Step 3: Sort by absolute value
  return Object.entries(prcc)
    .sort((a, b) => Math.abs(b[1]) - Math.abs(a[1]));
}
```

**Note on simplification:** The implementation above uses Spearman rank correlation rather than full partial rank correlation (which would require matrix inversion to control for other variables). At 10,000 samples with moderate collinearity between inputs, Spearman is a sufficiently accurate approximation for the tornado chart ranking. If full PRCC is desired, the implementation requires computing the inverse of the rank-correlation matrix of all inputs, which adds ~50 lines of JavaScript but remains well within the 300ms performance budget.

**Disclosure in output:** The sensitivity chart must include a footnote: "Sensitivity measured via rank correlation between each input variable and Year 5 revenue across 10,000 scenarios. Captures nonlinear and monotonic relationships. Correlated inputs (GDP, inflation, FX) may show shared influence — their individual bars reflect marginal contribution, not independent effect."

## 8. Anti-Hallucination Safeguards for Monte Carlo

### The Core Risk

Monte Carlo simulation can make rough assumptions feel precise. A beautifully rendered probability distribution lends an air of quantitative rigor that can obscure the fact that the input parameters are directional estimates, not measured values. This is the primary anti-hallucination concern.

### Mandatory Safeguards

1. **Every parameter must have a source citation.** The assumptions table in the HTML output must list the distribution type, parameter values, and source for every stochastic variable. Sources must trace to the knowledge files or verifiable public data. **v2 addition:** This includes functional form parameters (sigmoid center, steepness, FX mean-reversion divisor), which must be designated as "Modeling choice" with sensitivity ranges.

2. **The disclosure bar is non-optional.** Every Monte Carlo output — in both Stage 1 and Stage 3 — must include the following disclosure (or equivalent language):

   > *"This simulation models 10,000 scenarios using historical macroeconomic data from World Bank, IMF, Bank of Ghana, GIPC, Ghana Statistical Service, and sector-specific sources. Macroeconomic variables (GDP growth, inflation, exchange rate) are modeled as correlated using a Cholesky decomposition estimated from Ghana's 2020–2025 data — downside scenarios reflect the empirical tendency of adverse macro conditions to compound simultaneously. Input distributions reflect documented market conditions as of March 2026. Results are directional probability estimates — not financial, legal, or investment advice. All projections should be validated with local legal counsel and financial advisors. This analysis was produced as part of the Nexten Summit engagement system; see the document-level disclosure for full context on the dual purpose of this analysis."*

3. **No false precision.** Scenario card values should be displayed to one decimal place ($12.3M), not four ($12.3417M). The precision of the output must not exceed the precision of the inputs.

4. **Insight bar language must be probabilistic.** Use "In X% of scenarios..." or "The median scenario outcome is..." — never "You will earn..." or "Your revenue will be..." or "The market will grow to..."

5. **When sector data is limited, say so.** If the knowledge base lacks a credible addressable market figure for a specific sub-sector, the simulation should either use a conservative parent-sector estimate (with disclosure) or explicitly note that the parameter is a rough directional estimate.

6. **The model must never generate parameters outside documented ranges.** If the knowledge base shows Ghana's GDP growth has ranged from 0.5% (2020, COVID) to 7.2% (Q3 2024), the normal distribution's tails should not regularly produce values of 12% or −5%.

7. **Plausibility back-test (v2 addition).** After simulation completes, check the P75 and P90 Year 5 revenue figures against plausible benchmarks:
   - If P90 Year 5 revenue exceeds $50M for a sub-$10M attendee, or exceeds $500M for any attendee, the output must include a plausibility note.
   - The P90 scenario narrative (Stage 3) must address whether a comparable case of similar market capture exists in the knowledge base. If none is documented: "No comparable case of a new foreign entrant achieving this level of market penetration within 5 years in Ghana or comparable West African markets is documented in the knowledge base."
   - If the model's upper-bound penetration rate exceeds 20% at Year 5, the assumptions table must include the penetration ceiling plausibility note specified in Section 4.

8. **Base rate anchoring (v2 addition).** Where available, the Stage 3 scenario narratives should reference base rates for foreign market entry success in the target sector or comparable African markets. If no base rate data is available, the P50 or P90 narrative should note: "Historical success rates for comparable market entries in this sector are not documented in the knowledge base. The simulation models market-level addressable revenue — actual capture depends on execution factors the model does not assess."

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import truncnorm, norm

def run_monte_carlo(params, n_scenarios=10000, n_years=5):
    results = np.zeros((n_scenarios, n_years + 1))

    # Cholesky decomposition of correlation matrix
    corr_matrix = np.array([
        [1.0, -0.6, -0.5],
        [-0.6, 1.0, 0.7],
        [-0.5, 0.7, 1.0]
    ])
    L = np.linalg.cholesky(corr_matrix)

    for s in range(n_scenarios):
        # Draw correlated standard normals
        z = np.random.standard_normal(3)
        x = L @ z

        # Transform to marginals
        gdp = params['gdp_mu'] + params['gdp_sigma'] * x[0]
        # Inflation: truncated normal via CDF inversion
        u_inf = norm.cdf(x[1])
        a_inf = (params['inf_floor'] - params['inf_mu']) / params['inf_sigma']
        b_inf = (params['inf_ceil'] - params['inf_mu']) / params['inf_sigma']
        inflation = truncnorm.ppf(u_inf, a_inf, b_inf,
                                   loc=params['inf_mu'], scale=params['inf_sigma'])
        # Exchange rate: log-normal via CDF inversion
        u_fx = norm.cdf(x[2])
        fx_base = np.exp(params['fx_mu_log'] + params['fx_sigma_log'] * norm.ppf(u_fx))

        # Stochastic addressable market
        base_market = np.random.triangular(
            params['market_min'], params['market_mode'], params['market_max']
        )

        sector_growth = np.random.triangular(
            params['sg_min'], params['sg_mode'], params['sg_max']
        )
        penetration_y5 = (params['pen_min'] +
                          np.random.beta(params['pen_a'], params['pen_b']) *
                          (params['pen_max'] - params['pen_min']))
        has_delay = np.random.random() < params['delay_prob']
        delay_months = (np.random.uniform(params['delay_min'], params['delay_max'])
                        if has_delay else 0)

        for y in range(1, n_years + 1):
            market = base_market * (1 + sector_growth * (1 + gdp - 0.04)) ** y
            eff_year = max(0, y - delay_months / 12)
            pen = penetration_y5 * (1 / (1 + np.exp(-2.5 * (eff_year - 2.5))))
            # FX with mean-reversion: reduce sigma by sqrt(year)
            fx_yr = np.exp(params['fx_mu_log'] +
                           (params['fx_sigma_log'] / np.sqrt(y)) *
                           norm.ppf(norm.cdf(x[2])))  # correlated draw
            fx_impact = 10.8 / fx_yr
            results[s, y] = max(0, market * pen * fx_impact)

    return results
```

## 13. Addressable Revenue Explainer Callout — Mandatory (v3)

### Problem Statement

Testing revealed that attendees frequently misinterpret the P25/P50/P75 scenario card dollar values in one of two ways:

1. **As total market size** — "That's the market I'm competing for?" (No — the total addressable market is much larger; the cards show the attendee's penetration slice.)
2. **As a revenue projection** — "So I'll earn $4.5M by Year 5?" (No — this is the revenue envelope the market could support, not a projection of what the attendee will earn. It excludes operating costs, margins, staffing, and all execution factors.)

Both misreadings undermine trust. The first makes the opportunity seem smaller than it is. The second creates expectations the model cannot support. This section specifies a mandatory callout that resolves both failure modes.

### Component: "What These Numbers Mean" Callout

#### Placement

Immediately ABOVE the three scenario cards (P25/P50/P75) and immediately BELOW the simulation button + progress indicator. The callout renders after simulation completes, alongside the scenario cards — it is part of the results display, not visible before the simulation runs.

**The attendee reads the explainer BEFORE they read the dollar values.** This sequencing is mandatory.

#### Content Template

The callout dynamically populates based on the attendee's parameters:

> **What These Numbers Mean**
>
> The total addressable market for **[SECTOR]** in Ghana is estimated at **$[ADDRESSABLE_MARKET_MODE]M**. These cards do not show that full market — they show the slice the model estimates a **[REVENUE_TIER]** entrant could realistically capture.
>
> For your revenue tier, the model draws market penetration between **[PEN_MIN]%** and **[PEN_MAX]%** by Year 5. Given your sector's growth trajectory, Ghana's macroeconomic conditions, and the penetration rate that a [REVENUE_TIER] entrant could realistically achieve — **the Median of [P50_VALUE] is the middle-of-the-road estimate for the revenue the market could support for your specific operation by Year 5.**
>
> This is NOT a projection of what you will earn. It is the **revenue envelope** — the size of the opportunity your business could capture if you execute well. It does not account for operating costs, margins, staffing, rent, or other factors that determine actual profit. What you take home depends on execution.

#### Variable Population

| Placeholder | Source | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `[SECTOR]` | Attendee's primary sector from intake | "Education & Workforce" |
| `[ADDRESSABLE_MARKET_MODE]` | Mode value from the Triangular distribution for the attendee's sector | "$200M" |
| `[REVENUE_TIER]` | Attendee's revenue range from Exchange 2 | "sub-$1M" |
| `[PEN_MIN]` | Minimum penetration for the revenue tier | "0.5%" |
| `[PEN_MAX]` | Maximum penetration for the revenue tier | "6%" |
| `[P50_VALUE]` | The computed P50 dollar value from the simulation | "$4.5M" |

#### Visual Specification

- **Background:** Distinct from the scenario cards — use the report's informational callout style (same treatment as the dual-purpose disclosure or insight bar, but with a neutral/educational tone rather than a highlight tone)
- **Border:** Left-border accent in white or light gray — NOT gold, teal, or coral. This is informational infrastructure, not a finding.
- **Icon (optional):** A small "ℹ" info icon to signal "this is context, not a result"
- **Typography:** Body font (Source Sans 3 or equivalent), same size as paragraph text. NOT monospace, NOT display font. It should read as explanation, not data.

#### Rules

1. **Mandatory in every Monte Carlo output.** Stage 1 kiosk, Stage 3 full report, any future output format. No exceptions. If the three scenario cards appear, this callout appears directly above them.

2. **Dynamic, not boilerplate.** The callout must populate with the attendee's actual sector, revenue tier, penetration range, and P50 value. A generic version that says "the model estimates market penetration" without specifying the range is insufficient.

3. **Cannot be buried.** The callout must be visually proximate to the scenario cards — not in a footnote, not in the assumptions table, not at the bottom of the section. The attendee reads the explainer BEFORE the dollar values.

4. **Tone:** Educational, not defensive. This is not a disclaimer — it is context that makes the numbers useful. Write it as if the analyst is leaning over and saying: "Here's how to read this."

5. **Does not replace the assumptions table.** The assumptions table still shows all parameters, distributions, and sources. The callout is a plain-language translation of what the scenario cards mean — it serves a different function.

6. **Does not replace the Monte Carlo disclosure.** The standard disclosure ("This simulation models 10,000 scenarios...") still appears at the bottom of the section. The callout is about comprehension; the disclosure is about provenance and limitations.

7. **Relationship to Enterprise Contextualization ($50M+).** For $50M+ attendees, the Addressable Revenue Explainer Callout appears FIRST (above scenario cards), followed by the Enterprise Contextualization note (which adds the "% of current revenue" comparison). The two are complementary — the callout explains what the number is; the enterprise note puts it in scale context.
