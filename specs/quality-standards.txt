# AI Mirror — World-Class Quality Standards Framework
## Benchmark Reference for Business Opportunity Analysis Reports
### Compiled March 2026 via Deep Research | Revised March 15, 2026 | VERIDEX Calibration Update March 15, 2026

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This document establishes the definitive quality standards framework for evaluating personalized business opportunity analysis reports — specifically, reports that map an executive's competencies, capital profile, or skillset to market opportunities in emerging African economies (primarily Ghana). It is designed to be used by an AI Evaluator Agent that will score reports on a 1–10 scale across multiple dimensions, with clear justification traceable to the standards herein.

The framework draws on established methodologies from top-tier management consultancies (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), economic intelligence firms (EIU, Oxford Economics, S&P Global), multilateral development institutions (IFC, World Bank, AfDB), Big Four advisory practices (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG), and academic research on data visualization (Tufte, Cairo), structured communication (Minto's Pyramid Principle), Monte Carlo simulation best practices (Financial Planning Association, CFA Institute), cognitive load theory (Sweller, Mayer), and AI safety standards (NIST AI RMF, International AI Safety Report 2026). These sources collectively define what "world-class" means in this specific domain.

The Evaluator Agent should apply this framework by scoring each applicable dimension on a 1–10 scale using the detailed rubrics below, applying the specified weights, computing the composite score, and assigning the report to a quality tier. Where two reports are being compared, the same rubric is applied to both, and the scores are presented side by side. Minimum threshold rules (Section 5) ensure that critical failures in integrity or accuracy cannot be masked by strength in other areas. Score calibration anchors (Section 5.4) and anti-inflation instructions (Section 5.5) provide additional safeguards against systematic score inflation. The framework has been calibrated specifically for the AI Mirror context — a personalized, competency-to-opportunity mapping system serving Houston executives exploring the Ghana and West Africa market. Generic advisory quality standards have been adapted to reflect this context throughout.

## 2. Evaluation Dimensions

All evaluation dimensions are defined in Sections 3 (mandatory) and 4 (research-derived additional). Each dimension includes a definition, rationale, weight, detailed scoring rubric, common failure modes, and exemplar references. The 10 mandatory dimensions (Section 3) constitute the core evaluation framework. The 5 additional dimensions (Section 4) were surfaced through deep research across consulting quality standards, emerging market advisory practices, and AI-generated content evaluation methodologies. Together, the 15 dimensions capture the full spectrum of quality factors that distinguish world-class work from adequate work in this specific advisory context.

## 3. Mandatory Evaluation Dimensions

### 3.1 Analytical Rigor & Data Integrity

**Definition:** The degree to which claims, statistics, projections, and recommendations are grounded in verifiable data from credible sources, and the logical soundness of the analytical chain from data to insight to recommendation.

**Why it matters:** This is the foundational credibility layer. Management consultancies like McKinsey and BCG stake their reputations on the verifiability of every number in their deliverables. The IFC's Anticipated Impact Measurement and Monitoring (AIMM) framework requires every investment thesis to trace back to documented development gaps and quantified indicators. A report without analytical rigor is opinion dressed as analysis.

**Weight:** 12%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** At least 75% of quantitative claims cite primary data sources (government statistics bureaus, World Bank WDI, IMF databases, central bank publications, or peer-reviewed research). Every projection includes methodology disclosure. Analytical chain is fully traceable: every conclusion links to specific evidence. No unsourced statistics. Citation density of 3+ sourced data points per output section. Triangulation is used where single-source data is insufficient — multiple independent sources are cross-referenced to validate key figures. Data recency: core macroeconomic figures are from within 18 months of the report date. Logical connectors between sections are explicit — the reader can trace the thread from market data to development gap to competency match to entry strategy to capital requirement without any inferential leaps.
- **7–8 (Strong):** At least 50% of quantitative claims cite primary sources. Most projections include methodology notes. Analytical chain is generally traceable with occasional gaps. Citation density of 2+ sourced data points per section. Some triangulation evident. Cross-section logical links are present but not always explicit.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Mix of sourced and unsourced claims. Some projections lack methodology disclosure. Analytical chain has identifiable gaps where conclusions are not clearly supported. Citation density of 1+ sourced data points per section.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Fewer than 30% of quantitative claims are sourced. Projections appear to be estimates without disclosed methodology. Significant logical leaps between data and conclusions. Occasional statistics appear fabricated or unverifiable.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Pervasive unsourced claims. Statistics appear invented. No methodology disclosure. No traceable analytical chain. The report reads as opinion or speculation rather than analysis.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Citing secondary aggregators (news articles, blog posts) instead of primary data sources
- Using outdated data (pre-2023 macroeconomic figures for a 2026 analysis)
- Presenting ranges without disclosing how they were derived
- "Approximate" figures with no indication of the approximation method
- Mixing GDP figures from different base years or methodologies without adjustment
- Citing "industry reports" without naming the specific report, publisher, or date
- Breaking the analytical chain: jumping from "Ghana's construction sector is growing" to "you should enter this market" without the intermediate evidence steps (addressable market size, competitive density, development gap specificity, regulatory feasibility)

**Exemplar Reference:** The IFC's Country Private Sector Diagnostics (CPSDs) represent the gold standard — every sector analysis traces to specific World Bank or national statistics data, with methodology notes explaining how addressable market figures were derived. The EIU Country Risk Service scores countries across sovereign, currency, banking, political, and economic structure risk — each scored on a 0–100 scale with documented indicator weights and sources.

### 3.2 Strategic Insight Depth

**Definition:** The density and quality of original strategic insight relative to descriptive information. Strategic insight means analysis that reveals non-obvious connections, identifies opportunities the reader would not have seen independently, or reframes the reader's understanding of their own position.

**Why it matters:** The difference between a report that assembles information and one that creates strategic value lies in insight density. McKinsey's internal quality standards distinguish between "so what?" (the insight) and "what is" (the description). Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle, developed at McKinsey, demands that every section culminate in a governing insight that synthesizes the underlying analysis. A competency-to-opportunity mapping report must do more than list facts about Ghana — it must reveal how the specific attendee's skills intersect with specific market gaps in ways the attendee had not previously considered.

**Weight:** 12%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Every output section contains at least one non-obvious strategic insight that connects the attendee's specific profile to a specific market dynamic. The competency mapping reveals connections the attendee likely had not considered. Pivot pathway recommendations are grounded in documented development gaps and supported by market data. The "so what?" is always answered — no section ends with description alone. Insight-to-description ratio exceeds 40% (measured as proportion of text that contains analysis, synthesis, or strategic framing vs. factual description). The vertical/adjacent pivot analysis demonstrates genuine analytical reasoning — not a formulaic application of the three pivot types. The reader's understanding of their own position is observably reframed by the end of the document. Where the report recommends market entry, it references base rates for comparable entries — either the historical success rate of foreign direct investment in the target sector/economy, or comparable case studies that include both successes and failures (not survivorship-biased success-only citations). If base rate data is unavailable, the report explicitly notes this gap rather than proceeding as though every market entry succeeds.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most sections contain strategic insights. Competency mapping shows genuine analytical work. Some sections may present information without fully developing the strategic implications. Insight-to-description ratio of 25–40%.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Strategic insights are present but intermittent. Some competency mappings feel mechanical rather than analytically derived. Several sections read as informational rather than strategic. Insight-to-description ratio of 15–25%.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Report is predominantly descriptive with occasional insights. Competency mapping appears templated — substituting the attendee's industry name into generic recommendations. Most sections read as "here is information about Ghana" rather than "here is what this means for you."
- **1–2 (Poor):** No discernible strategic insight. Report is entirely descriptive or consists of generic information that could apply to any business in any emerging market.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- "Template-swap" analysis: Generic Ghana market overview with the attendee's industry name inserted
- Failing to complete the analytical chain from competency → gap → opportunity → entry strategy
- Restating facts from knowledge files without synthesizing them into actionable intelligence
- Presenting the vertical/adjacent pivot as an afterthought rather than a genuinely analyzed alternative
- Offering insights that are self-evident to anyone with basic knowledge of the market
- Choosing a pivot type (Skills Transfer, Formalization, Industry) based on surface-level pattern matching rather than genuine development gap weighting
- Citing successful market entrants ("Company X entered Ghana and achieved...") without providing the denominator. How many companies attempted similar entries? How many failed or withdrew? Without this context, individual success stories are anecdotes, not evidence. This is survivorship bias — the #1 analytical failure in market entry analyses.

**Exemplar Reference:** BCG's market entry analyses are known for their "insight density" — every chart has an insight headline (not a topic headline), and every section ends with a strategic implication. The pyramid principle demands that the most important insight appears first, with supporting evidence structured beneath it in a MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive) hierarchy.

### 3.3 Actionability & Specificity

**Definition:** The degree to which recommendations, strategies, and next steps are specific enough to act upon — meaning they identify who, what, when, where, how, and at what resource cost, rather than offering generic directional guidance.

**Why it matters:** The gap between insight and action is where most advisory reports fail. Research on consulting quality frameworks consistently identifies specificity as the single most important differentiator between reports that drive decisions and reports that sit on shelves. A recommendation like "explore partnerships in Ghana" is functionally worthless; "establish a joint venture with a GIPC-registered local partner in the Tema Free Zone to access duty-free import privileges for equipment, targeting Q2 2027 operational launch with an estimated $150K–$300K setup cost" is actionable.

**Weight:** 10%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Every recommendation specifies six elements: the action, the mechanism, the target entity or geography, the timeline, the resource implication, and the success indicator. Market entry strategies name specific entry models (JV, greenfield, licensing, franchise) with reasoning for why each model fits or doesn't fit this attendee's profile, scale, and experience level. Timeline items include milestone dates or ranges. Resource requirements include dollar ranges where feasible. Connection map entries reference specific institutional types (not just "government contacts" but "Ghana Investment Promotion Centre — GIPC — for investment registration and incentive qualification"). Quick wins are immediately executable with clear first steps. The attendee could hand this report to an operations team and begin execution without additional research.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most recommendations include 5 of the 6 specificity elements (action, mechanism, target, timeline, resources, success indicator). Entry strategies are differentiated and reasoned. Some items could be more specific but provide enough direction to begin planning.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Recommendations are directional but lack full specificity. Entry strategies are named but not deeply reasoned. Timelines are vague ("medium-term"). Resource estimates are absent or very rough. No success indicators.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Recommendations are generic and could apply to almost any business. Entry strategies are listed without analysis of fit. No meaningful timelines. No resource estimates.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Recommendations are platitudes ("consider entering the market," "build local relationships"). No specificity on any dimension.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Offering a "partnership strategy" without specifying what kind of partner, where to find them, or what the partnership structure should look like
- Recommending "regulatory compliance" without specifying which regulations
- Providing timelines without milestones or phased structure (pilot → entry → scale)
- Suggesting market entry without capital requirement estimates at each phase
- Naming stakeholder categories without explaining why they matter for this specific attendee
- Recommending an entry model (JV, greenfield, etc.) without explaining why it fits this attendee's scale, risk tolerance, and experience level over alternatives

**Exemplar Reference:** Kearney and Roland Berger market entry analyses typically include implementation roadmaps with phased milestones, responsible party designations, and capital deployment schedules. The U.S. Commercial Service "Doing Business In" guides provide specific regulatory steps, registered agent requirements, and fee schedules.

### 3.4 Comprehensiveness & Coverage

**Definition:** The breadth and depth of the analysis across all relevant dimensions of the opportunity — market sizing, regulatory environment, competitive landscape, risk factors, capital requirements, human capital needs, cultural considerations, and infrastructure realities.

**Why it matters:** An incomplete analysis creates blind spots that lead to bad decisions. The IFC's AIMM framework evaluates investments across project outcomes AND market-level impacts, ensuring no dimension is overlooked. The EIU's country risk methodology covers five distinct risk categories (sovereign, currency, banking, political, economic structure) because no single dimension captures the full picture.

**Weight:** 8%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** All 15 output sections (for a full report) or all 6 output sections (for a kiosk report) are fully developed. No section feels thin or rushed. Cross-references between sections demonstrate integrated analysis (e.g., risk factors in the SWOT align with the mitigation strategies; capital requirements reflect the entry strategy chosen; Monte Carlo parameters are consistent with the sector identified in the competency mapping). Regulatory coverage is specific to the attendee's sector. Competitive landscape names actual market participants or characterizes the competitive environment with specificity. Every dimension that would inform an investment decision is addressed: market opportunity, competitive dynamics, regulatory/legal requirements, risk factors, capital needs, talent considerations, infrastructure realities, and timing.
- **7–8 (Strong):** All sections are present and substantively developed. Most cross-references are consistent. One or two sections may be thinner than others but none are perfunctory.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** All sections are present but some feel underdeveloped. Cross-referencing is inconsistent. Some sections read as filler rather than genuine analysis.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Some sections are missing or reduced to a few sentences. Significant gaps in coverage. Sections that are present may contradict each other.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Large sections missing. Analysis covers only 2–3 dimensions of the opportunity. No meaningful cross-referencing.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Developing the opportunity analysis in depth but treating risk assessment as an afterthought
- Omitting regulatory/legal analysis entirely
- Providing a competitive landscape that says "limited competition" without evidence
- Failing to connect the entry strategy to the capital requirements
- Treating the connection map as a generic list rather than an attendee-specific analysis
- Monte Carlo sector parameters that do not match the sector identified in the competency mapping

**Exemplar Reference:** IFC Country Private Sector Diagnostics cover: macroeconomic context, sector analysis (typically 3–5 sectors in depth), regulatory environment, competition/market structure, financial sector access, infrastructure gaps, and recommendations — each section substantively developed and cross-referenced.

### 3.5 Personalization & Relevance

**Definition:** The degree to which the analysis is genuinely tailored to the specific attendee's business, competencies, scale, experience level, and stated intent — as opposed to being a generic market report with the attendee's name inserted.

**Why it matters:** This is the core promise of the AI Mirror system: "Your skills, reflected through Ghana's opportunity." Personalization is what distinguishes this from a downloadable market report. The attendee should read the analysis and feel that someone (or something) genuinely understood their business and mapped it — not just their industry category — to specific opportunities.

**Weight:** 11%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** The competency extraction from the free-text input is precise and reflects the attendee's actual capabilities (not just their industry label). The pivot analysis is clearly derived from the specific competencies described, not from the industry category alone. Revenue scale is reflected in market penetration assumptions, capital requirements, and entry strategy aggressiveness. Africa experience level is reflected in regulatory delay assumptions and entry strategy conservatism/aggressiveness. The attendee would recognize their own business in the analysis. Language and framing reflect the attendee's role (founder vs. investor vs. policy leader). Strategic intent (market expansion vs. capital investment vs. skills transfer vs. partnerships) is visibly integrated into the recommendations. The competency-to-gap mapping grid (Output 1) contains items that could only have been generated for this specific attendee. For attendees at enterprise scale ($250M+ revenue or equivalent institutional resources), the analysis addresses opportunity cost: why this market and this allocation rather than alternatives? This does not require a full comparative market analysis, but it does require acknowledging that the reader has competing claims on capital and attention, and articulating what makes this opportunity distinctively suited to their specific capabilities rather than generically attractive. If the report would be equally compelling addressed to any Fortune 500 technology company, it fails the personalization test regardless of how many company-specific details it contains.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Strong personalization visible in most sections. Competency extraction is accurate. Most recommendations reflect the attendee's scale and experience level. Some sections could be more specifically tailored.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Personalization is visible but intermittent. Some sections feel generically applicable to anyone in the attendee's industry. The competency mapping may be too broad.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Minimal personalization. Analysis reads as an industry report rather than a personal analysis. Competency extraction is superficial. Scale and experience level are not meaningfully reflected.
- **1–2 (Poor):** No meaningful personalization. The same report could have been generated for any attendee who named the same industry.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Mapping the industry label instead of the actual competencies described
- Ignoring revenue scale when sizing opportunity or recommending entry strategy
- Treating "No, this would be new" Africa experience the same as "Yes, actively"
- Using the same entry strategy recommendation regardless of whether the attendee is a $1M founder or a $250M+ executive
- Failing to reflect the attendee's stated strategic intent (market expansion vs. capital investment vs. skills transfer)
- Generic competency-to-gap mapping items (e.g., "business management skills" → "need for professional management") that would apply to any attendee in any industry
- Treating a $5–25M allocation from a $29B company the same as a $5–25M allocation from a $50M company. The strategic calculus is fundamentally different: for the smaller company, this is a bet-the-company investment; for the larger, it is a rounding error that competes with hundreds of similar opportunities globally. A personalized analysis must reflect this difference in framing, risk tolerance, and decision criteria.

**Exemplar Reference:** High-touch wealth management firms (e.g., Goldman Sachs Private Wealth Management) create personalized investment strategy documents that reflect the client's specific risk tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, and values — not just their asset class. The best personalized advisory reports feel like they were written for one person.

### 3.6 Market & Competitive Accuracy

**Definition:** The factual accuracy of all market data, competitive landscape descriptions, regulatory citations, and economic indicators presented in the report.

**Why it matters:** A single inaccurate data point undermines the credibility of the entire analysis. In emerging markets where data is scarcer and harder to verify, accuracy discipline is even more critical. The EIU maintains a team of country analysts who continuously verify and update their assessments. The IFC's performance standards require that every investment decision trace to verified market data.

**Weight:** 8%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** All macroeconomic figures (GDP growth, inflation, exchange rates, population) are accurate and from authoritative sources (Bank of Ghana, Ghana Statistical Service, IMF, World Bank). Sector-specific data aligns with recognized industry sources (IRENA for energy, GSMA for telecoms, Ghana Energy Commission for power). Regulatory citations are accurate and current — specific Acts, regulations, and institutional mandates are correctly named and described. No fabricated organizations, programs, or case studies. Competitive landscape descriptions reflect verifiable market conditions. Government ministries and agencies are correctly named with current mandates.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Core macroeconomic data is accurate. Sector data is mostly accurate with minor imprecisions. Regulatory citations are substantively correct. No fabrications detected.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Most data is accurate but some figures are outdated or slightly off. Some sector data may use estimates where better data exists. Minor regulatory inaccuracies.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Multiple data inaccuracies. Some figures appear to be approximate or estimated without disclosure. Regulatory citations may be wrong or outdated. Some competitive landscape claims are unverifiable.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Widespread inaccuracies. Fabricated data points, organizations, or programs. Regulatory information is substantially wrong.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Citing Ghana's GDP growth rate from 2022 in a 2026 analysis without noting the year
- Inventing a "Ghana Technology Innovation Fund" or similar fictional program
- Describing a regulatory framework that does not exist or has been superseded
- Stating sector CAGR figures that cannot be traced to any published source
- Naming specific companies as competitors without verification
- Conflating Ghanaian government agencies or citing defunct institutional structures
- Incorrectly describing GIPC incentive thresholds or free zone benefits

**Exemplar Reference:** The EIU Country Risk Service maintains accuracy through monthly analyst reviews, cross-referencing government data with IMF Article IV reports and World Bank assessments. Error rates in published EIU data are tracked as a quality metric.

#### 3.6.1 Mandatory Spot-Check Verification Protocol

The Evaluator MUST perform the following verification steps before scoring Dimension 3.6. These are not optional.

**Step 1: Institutional Verification (minimum 5 checks)**
Select at least 5 named institutions, government agencies, programs, or regulatory bodies from the report. Verify each exists, is correctly named, and has the mandate described. Use web search to confirm. Record the result of each check (VERIFIED / UNVERIFIABLE / INCORRECT).

**Step 2: Quantitative Claim Verification (minimum 5 checks)**
Select at least 5 specific quantitative claims (dollar figures, percentages, growth rates, dates) from different sections of the report. Attempt to trace each to its cited source. Record: (a) whether the source exists, (b) whether the figure matches the source, (c) whether the figure is current. Any figure that differs from its cited source by more than 10% or that cites a source that does not contain the claimed figure counts as an error.

**Step 3: Regulatory Verification (minimum 3 checks)**
Select at least 3 regulatory claims (tax rates, incentive provisions, licensing requirements, minimum capital thresholds). Verify each against the cited authority or, if no source is cited, against the most current available regulatory documentation.

**Scoring Impact:**
- 0 errors found across all checks: No deduction
- 1 error found: Maximum score for Dimension 3.6 is 8
- 2 errors found: Maximum score for Dimension 3.6 is 7
- 3+ errors found: Maximum score for Dimension 3.6 is 5
- Any error involving a fabricated institution or program: Triggers Accuracy Floor (Section 5.2, Rule 5)

The Evaluator must disclose all spot-check results in the evaluation document, including which claims were checked and what was found. This creates an audit trail.

**Rationale:** The rubric describes what accuracy looks like at each level but does not by itself force the Evaluator to actually test it. An AI Evaluator Agent assessing an AI-generated report has an inherent tendency to accept plausible-sounding claims rather than verify them — because it was likely trained on the same data distribution that generated the claims. Mandatory spot-checks with recorded results force empirical verification.

### 3.7 Risk & Uncertainty Handling

**Definition:** The quality and honesty with which risks, uncertainties, caveats, and limitations are communicated — including macroeconomic risk, political risk, currency risk, regulatory risk, execution risk, and data uncertainty.

**Why it matters:** Emerging market analyses that present only opportunity without candid risk assessment fail the standard set by every major advisory institution. The EIU's country risk methodology evaluates sovereign, currency, banking sector, political, and economic structure risk separately — because each affects business decisions differently. The IFC requires that every investment appraisal include a risk section with specific mitigation strategies. McKinsey's research on market entry emphasizes that cognitive biases cause executives to systematically underestimate competition and overestimate market size — honest risk treatment corrects for this.

**Weight:** 8%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Risks are presented with the same analytical depth as opportunities. Specific risk factors are identified (not just generic categories). Each risk is paired with a specific mitigation strategy that is feasible and actionable. Currency risk is quantified or bounded (e.g., historical cedi depreciation range, Monte Carlo FX modeling). Political risk is assessed with reference to current governance conditions and upcoming electoral cycles. Regulatory risk cites specific rules that could affect the attendee. Data limitations are disclosed explicitly. Probabilistic language is used correctly ("in X% of scenarios" rather than "you will face"). The SWOT analysis (where present) is balanced — threats and weaknesses receive the same analytical attention as opportunities and strengths. Risk assessment is consistent with the Monte Carlo stochastic variables.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most risks are specific and paired with mitigations. Currency and political risk are addressed. Most data limitations are noted. Some risk areas may lack depth.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Risks are identified but treatment is uneven. Some risks are generic ("political instability"). Mitigations are present but may be generic. Data limitations are partially disclosed.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Risk treatment is perfunctory. Generic risk categories without specificity. Few or no mitigations. Data limitations not disclosed. The report reads as overly optimistic.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Risks are absent or dismissed. The analysis reads as promotional rather than advisory.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Presenting Ghana as uniformly low-risk without acknowledging currency volatility or infrastructure gaps
- Listing risks without mitigations
- Using boilerplate risk language ("as with any emerging market...")
- Failing to distinguish between risks that are manageable and those that are structural
- Omitting data uncertainty disclosures (especially for sector-specific projections)
- Treating the SWOT weaknesses and threats as afterthoughts
- Not connecting risk assessment to the Monte Carlo stochastic variables
- Failing to establish or reference base rates for market entry success/failure in the target economy or sector. A risk section that addresses specific threats but never asks "what percentage of comparable entries succeed?" has a structural gap.

**Exemplar Reference:** The IFC's environmental and social Performance Standards require explicit risk identification and mitigation for every project. Fitch, Moody's, and S&P sovereign risk assessments provide the gold standard for structured risk evaluation in emerging markets.

### 3.8 Quantitative Modeling Quality (Including Monte Carlo)

**Definition:** The rigor, transparency, and intellectual honesty of all quantitative models, projections, simulations, and financial estimates in the report — with specific attention to the Monte Carlo simulation engine where applicable.

**Why it matters:** Quantitative models are where analytical reports most frequently either gain or lose credibility. The Financial Planning Association and CFA Institute both emphasize that Monte Carlo simulations are only as good as their input assumptions. T. Rowe Price's advisory practice recommends that Monte Carlo outputs be framed within a "Confidence Zone" (typically 80–95%) to avoid clients fixating on extreme scenarios. In the advisory context, a Monte Carlo simulation that uses undisclosed parameters, presents false precision, or lacks an assumptions table is worse than no simulation at all — because it creates an illusion of rigor.

**Weight:** 8%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Monte Carlo simulation runs a minimum of 10,000 scenarios and executes client-side in under 200ms. Uses clearly documented distributions (type, parameters, and source for every stochastic variable). Parameters are dynamically selected based on the attendee's sector, scale, and experience — not hardcoded for all attendees. Assumptions table is complete with source citations for every parameter traceable to the knowledge files or verifiable public data. Results use probabilistic language throughout without exception. Percentile bands (P10/P25/P50/P75/P90) are presented rather than single-point estimates. Fan charts and histograms follow Tufte's principles of graphical integrity — no distortion, maximized data-ink ratio, proportional y-axis scales, clear year labels. Sensitivity analysis identifies which variables contribute most to outcome variance (typically via tornado chart in Stage 3). Disclosure bar explains model limitations and what the model does NOT capture (company-level execution, operational costs, competitive dynamics). Addressable market figure is sourced and appropriate to the attendee's recommended opportunity pathway (pivot sector if applicable). Values rounded to one decimal place — no false precision. The revenue model formula (Revenue = AddressableMarket × Penetration × FX_Impact) is documented or inferable. Regulatory delay probability is scaled to the attendee's Africa experience level. The addressable market figure feeding the model is itself treated as uncertain (stochastic or bounded) unless it derives from a source with established methodology — a fixed addressable market from a single unverified source is a modeling failure, not a simplification. Correlations between macroeconomic variables are either modeled (e.g., via copulas, correlation matrices, or regime-switching) or explicitly disclosed as a limitation with an assessment of how independence assumptions affect tail outcomes. All functional forms (adoption curves, penetration ramp functions, growth compounding formulas) have documented parameter sources — hardcoded curve parameters without sourcing are equivalent to undisclosed assumptions. Penetration ceilings are bounded by reference to comparable market entries at similar scale — if no comparables are cited, the ceiling is unsupported.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most parameters are documented with sources. Distribution choices are reasonable and justified. Results use probabilistic language. Assumptions table is mostly complete. Visualization is clean and proportional. Execution is smooth. Minor gaps in parameter sourcing or sensitivity analysis. Correlations between macroeconomic variables are either modeled or explicitly disclosed as a limitation.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Simulation runs and produces results, but parameter documentation is incomplete. Some parameters lack source citations. Results may slip into deterministic language in places. Visualization is functional but not exemplary. Limited or no sensitivity analysis. Independence assumptions are not disclosed.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Parameters appear arbitrary or poorly documented. Assumptions table is incomplete or missing. Results are presented as predictions rather than probability distributions. Visualization may distort or mislead. No sensitivity analysis. Addressable market figure is unsourced. Execution may be slow or buggy.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Simulation is decorative — parameters are not credibly sourced, results are presented as forecasts, no assumptions table, no disclosure. Or simulation is absent when specified.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Using the same parameters for every attendee regardless of sector or scale
- Presenting Monte Carlo P50 as "your expected revenue" instead of "the median scenario"
- Omitting the assumptions table or populating it with vague source citations ("industry data")
- Selecting distribution types (normal, triangular, beta) without justification for the choice
- False precision: reporting "$12.3417M" from a model with uncertainty ranges spanning millions
- Addressable market figure is for the wrong sector (direct translation when pivot was recommended)
- Fan chart y-axis manipulation that exaggerates or compresses the outcome range
- Not disclosing what the model does NOT capture (execution risk, operational costs, competitive dynamics)
- Simulation that runs more than 200ms or fails to render charts on standard devices
- Using hardcoded seed values that produce identical results on every run
- Scenario narratives (P10/P50/P90 prose descriptions) that are generic rather than tied to specific macroeconomic conditions
- Treating the most consequential input (addressable market) as deterministic while modeling less impactful variables stochastically. This creates a model that quantifies the wrong uncertainty.
- Modeling all variables as independent when economic theory and empirical evidence establish correlations (GDP-inflation-exchange rate, sector growth-GDP growth). Independence assumptions artificially narrow tail distributions and underestimate worst-case scenarios.
- Using functional forms (e.g., sigmoid adoption curves) with parameters that appear sourced to nothing. If the S-curve inflection point and steepness are chosen arbitrarily, the model's time-path of revenue is arbitrary.
- Penetration ranges that permit implausible outcomes without flagging them. If the model allows a new entrant to capture 28% of a market in Year 5, and this outcome is presented without noting its implausibility relative to base rates, the model manufactures false optimism in its upper tail.
- Treating macroeconomic variables as statistically independent when economic theory and empirical data establish correlations. At minimum: GDP growth and exchange rate are correlated (weaker growth → capital flight → depreciation); inflation and exchange rate are correlated (depreciation → imported inflation); sector growth and GDP growth are correlated. A model that ignores all correlations will systematically underestimate the probability of scenarios where multiple negative factors compound simultaneously — which is precisely the scenario that destroys market entry investments. The Evaluator should check whether the model documentation discloses independence assumptions and assesses their impact on tail risk.

**Exemplar Reference:** The Financial Planning Association recommends Monte Carlo simulations include 1,000+ scenarios, use documented distribution assumptions, present confidence bands rather than point estimates, and include explicit methodology disclosure. The CFA Institute's standards require that any projection methodology be fully disclosed and that limitations be clearly communicated. The AI Mirror Monte Carlo Integration Spec defines the specific parameter sourcing tables, distribution specifications, and sector mappings that are the authoritative standard for this system.

#### 3.8.1 Monte Carlo Plausibility Back-Test

The Evaluator should perform the following check: examine the P75 and P90 revenue outcomes. Ask: is there a documented case of a comparable firm (similar sector, similar scale, similar market) achieving this level of revenue in a comparable emerging market within the modeled timeframe? If the answer is no, and the model does not flag these outcomes as exceptional, the model's upper tail is unsupported. This should reduce the Dimension 3.8 score by at least 1 point.

#### 3.8.2 Correlation Treatment (Required for Scores Above 7)

Models scoring 8+ on Dimension 3.8 must either:
(a) Implement correlations between at least the three primary macroeconomic variables (GDP, inflation, exchange rate) using a documented correlation matrix or copula structure, OR
(b) Explicitly disclose the independence assumption and include a qualitative assessment of how correlated downside scenarios would affect the P10 outcome (e.g., "If GDP contraction, cedi depreciation, and inflation spike occur simultaneously — as they did in 2022 — the P10 outcome could be X% lower than shown").

Option (b) is acceptable because correlation modeling adds implementation complexity. But pretending correlations don't exist is not acceptable at the 8+ level.

### 3.9 Presentation & Communication Quality

**Definition:** The visual design quality, narrative structure, reading experience, tone calibration, cognitive load management, and overall communicative effectiveness of the report — including typography, color theory, information density, layout, responsiveness, interactive element quality, and executive communication conventions.

**Why it matters:** Fortune 500 executives and successful business owners have been conditioned by McKinsey, BCG, and Bain deliverables to expect a certain standard of visual polish and communicative clarity. Edward Tufte's foundational principle — "above all else show the data" — demands that design serve analysis, not decorate it. Minto's Pyramid Principle demands that communication lead with conclusions and support with evidence. Research on cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988; Mayer, 2001) establishes that executive audiences process information more effectively when it is chunked, hierarchically structured, and visually differentiated by importance. The HTML design must feel professional-grade: consistent typography hierarchy, intentional color usage, responsive layout, and smooth interactions. A brilliant analysis presented poorly will not be taken seriously.

**Weight:** 6%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Design is polished, consistent, and professional-grade. Typography hierarchy is clear and intentional (distinct display and body fonts — Playfair Display for headings, DM Sans for body — with consistent sizing). Color palette is cohesive with semantic meaning (gold = primary accent/base case, teal = skills transfer/upside, coral = industry pivot/downside — consistently applied throughout). Layout is responsive across devices (grid collapses to single column below 720px). Interactive elements (Monte Carlo simulation button, scroll animations) function smoothly with appropriate loading states. Charts follow Tufte's principles: high data-ink ratio, no chartjunk, proportional representation, clear labeling. Narrative structure follows the Pyramid Principle: insights lead, evidence supports. Reading experience flows naturally section to section with visual breathing room. No broken elements, misaligned grids, or inconsistent spacing. Tone is calibrated correctly: warm and inviting, Africa as opportunity not obstacle, confident but grounded, specific but not overwhelming. Cognitive load is managed through progressive disclosure, clear section labeling, and visual hierarchy that guides the eye.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Design is clean and professional. Typography and color are consistent. Layout works across screen sizes. Interactive elements function. Most charts follow visualization best practices. Minor design inconsistencies. Tone is appropriate with occasional lapses.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Design is functional but not polished. Some inconsistencies in typography, spacing, or color usage. Interactive elements mostly work but may have rough edges. Chart quality is adequate. Tone may be uneven — too academic in places, too promotional in others.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Design is unprofessional or inconsistent. Typography is haphazard. Colors clash or lack semantic meaning. Layout breaks on mobile. Interactive elements malfunction. Charts mislead or confuse. Tone is inappropriate for the audience.
- **1–2 (Poor):** No meaningful design. Raw text output. Broken layout. Non-functional interactive elements.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Inconsistent heading sizes or font weights within the same document
- Color palette that lacks semantic structure (using gold for both positive and negative indicators)
- Charts with misleading axes, truncated scales, or excessive decoration (chartjunk)
- Mobile layout that breaks or is unreadable
- Monte Carlo simulation button that doesn't work or takes >1 second to render
- Walls of text without visual breathing room
- Missing or broken scroll animations
- Inaccessible text (low contrast, tiny font size)
- Tone that is too academic for the Houston executive audience (development economics jargon, dense paragraph structure)
- Tone that is too promotional, creating skepticism rather than trust
- Information density that overwhelms rather than guides — no clear visual hierarchy signaling what to read first

**Exemplar Reference:** McKinsey's consulting presentations use a standardized design system with action titles (insight headlines, not topic headlines), one message per slide, and sourced data on every chart. Tufte's principles of graphical integrity (Lie Factor close to 1.0, maximized data-ink ratio, no chartjunk) apply to all charts. The design system specified for the AI Mirror HTML output (Playfair Display + DM Sans, navy/gold/teal palette, surface cards with hover states, semantic badge colors) represents a professional-grade standard.

### 3.10 Ethical Integrity & Anti-Hallucination Compliance

**Definition:** The degree to which the report avoids fabricated information, maintains transparent disclosure about AI involvement, manages the tension between analysis and promotion honestly, uses probabilistic language correctly, and respects the boundary between advisory analysis and financial/legal advice.

**Why it matters:** The 2026 International AI Safety Report (led by Yoshua Bengio, with contributions from 100+ AI experts across 30 countries) identifies hallucinations as a persistent failure mode of language models — and notes that performance declines further in unfamiliar language and cultural contexts, which is directly relevant to Ghana-focused analyses. NIST's AI Risk Management Framework (AI 600-1) establishes that trustworthy AI requires documented safeguards against confabulation. For the AI Mirror specifically, the tension between generating excitement about the Ghana opportunity (a business development objective for Nexten Summit) and providing honest, grounded analysis (an advisory obligation) must be managed transparently. This is a documented conflict of interest — the system is simultaneously an analytical tool and a conversion funnel — and the Evaluator must assess whether analytical integrity is maintained despite this dual purpose.

**Weight:** 10%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Zero fabricated data points, organizations, programs, or case studies. Every statistic traces to a verifiable source. Projections are labeled as projections. Monte Carlo outputs use probabilistic language without exception. The report includes an appropriate disclaimer noting that analysis should be validated with local counsel and does not constitute financial, legal, or investment advice. The promotional function (driving attendance at Nexten Summit) does not compromise analytical honesty — risks are presented candidly even when they might dampen enthusiasm. No invented regulatory programs or government agencies. No fictitious addressable market figures. Data limitations are explicitly disclosed. The distinction between data-grounded analysis and AI-generated synthesis is clear. The CTA section is clearly separated from the analytical sections — it does not masquerade as a finding. The report includes an explicit disclosure that the analysis serves a dual purpose (advisory analysis and event/service business development). This disclosure appears in a visible location, not buried in footer text. The promotional elements are quarantined: they occupy clearly separated sections and do not infiltrate analytical findings. Analytical conclusions include findings that could discourage the recommended action (e.g., "the evidence does not support this timeline" or "this risk may be prohibitive at your scale") — demonstrating that the promotional objective does not suppress negative findings. If the analysis does not contain a single finding that could discourage action, the promotional function has compromised analytical integrity.
- **7–8 (Strong):** No detected fabrications. Most projections properly labeled. Probabilistic language used in Monte Carlo sections. Disclaimer present. Minor instances where promotional tone may slightly outweigh analytical balance. At least 1–2 findings that a reasonable reader could interpret as reasons for caution.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** No major fabrications but some claims are unverifiable. Projections are not consistently labeled. Some Monte Carlo language slips into deterministic framing. Disclaimer may be missing or weak. Promotional and analytical content are not always clearly distinguished.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Some claims appear fabricated or unverifiable. Projections presented as certainties. Monte Carlo results presented as forecasts. Missing disclaimer. Promotional tone overwhelms analytical balance. Some invented organizations or programs suspected.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Multiple fabrications detected. Made-up statistics, organizations, or programs. No disclaimers. Reads as marketing material rather than analysis.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Inventing a "Ghana Digital Innovation Authority" or similar plausible-sounding organization
- Fabricating specific dollar figures for addressable markets without any source
- Presenting Monte Carlo P50 as "your projected revenue" rather than "the median scenario"
- Omitting the disclaimer that analysis should be verified with local counsel
- Making Ghana sound uniformly positive to maximize excitement at the expense of honesty
- Fabricating case studies ("Company X entered Ghana in 2023 and achieved...")
- Using present-tense language for regulatory incentives that may have expired
- Reporting sector CAGR figures that cannot be traced to any published analysis
- Blurring the line between analytical findings and sales messaging — presenting the CTA as if it were a research conclusion rather than a commercial invitation
- Every analytical section reinforcing a predetermined positive conclusion. A genuine analysis will contain at least some findings that complicate or challenge the thesis. If every section points toward "enter this market," the analysis has become advocacy. The test: does the report contain any finding that, taken seriously, would give the reader pause? If not, the promotional function has overridden analytical integrity.

**Exemplar Reference:** The International AI Safety Report 2026 recommends layered safeguards for AI-generated content, including retrieval-augmented generation, source verification, and explicit disclosure of model limitations. NIST AI RMF 600-1 requires that AI systems used for consequential decisions include documentation of known failure modes and mitigation strategies. The AI Mirror's own anti-hallucination safeguards (mandatory source tracing, assumptions tables, probabilistic language requirements, disclosure bars) represent a sector-specific application of these principles.

## 4. Research-Derived Additional Dimensions

### 4.1 Cultural & Operational Contextual Awareness

**Definition:** The degree to which the analysis demonstrates understanding of on-the-ground business realities in Ghana and West Africa — beyond desk research — including informal economy dynamics, cultural business practices, infrastructure constraints, diaspora network dynamics, and practical operational challenges.

**Why it matters:** Research across Big Four advisory practices operating in Africa (Deloitte Africa, PwC Africa, EY Africa, KPMG Africa) consistently emphasizes that the gap between formal regulatory frameworks and actual business practice in African markets is the single biggest source of failed market entries. An analysis that describes Ghana's Free Zones Act without mentioning that power supply reliability in industrial zones varies significantly, or that recommends "hiring local talent" without noting Ghana's specific human capital strengths and gaps, fails the operational context test. For the AI Mirror audience specifically — Houston-based executives, many of whom are members of the African diaspora — understanding the role of diaspora networks as a market entry accelerant is a critical contextual factor that generic emerging market frameworks miss.

**Weight:** 4%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Analysis reflects specific on-the-ground realities: power infrastructure limitations by region, logistics corridor bottlenecks (Tema Port throughput, road conditions between major cities), informal economy dynamics relevant to the sector, practical visa and work permit considerations, banking system specifics (mobile money penetration via MTN MoMo, forex access constraints through Bank of Ghana), cultural business practices (relationship-driven commerce, the role of traditional authorities in land acquisition), and diaspora network dynamics where relevant. These are woven into the analysis organically, not siloed in a separate "cultural considerations" section.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most major operational realities are addressed. Infrastructure constraints are noted. Some cultural and informal economy context is present. Diaspora dynamics acknowledged where relevant.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Some operational context but treatment is surface-level. May reference infrastructure generically without sector-specific or region-specific detail.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Analysis reads as desk research with no operational awareness. Generic statements about "infrastructure challenges" without specificity.
- **1–2 (Poor):** No operational context. Analysis could have been written without any knowledge of Ghanaian business realities.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Recommending manufacturing without discussing power reliability or Tema vs. Takoradi logistics
- Ignoring mobile money (MTN MoMo) as a dominant payment channel
- Treating Ghana's business environment as identical to other West African countries
- Failing to mention the role of traditional authorities in land acquisition
- Not acknowledging the diaspora network effect for Houston-based executives with Ghanaian or West African heritage

### 4.2 Narrative Coherence & Persuasive Architecture

**Definition:** The degree to which the report tells a coherent, compelling story that builds from the attendee's current position to a clearly articulated opportunity — following the SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer) framework or equivalent narrative architecture.

**Why it matters:** McKinsey and BCG both train their consultants in the SCQA framework (developed by Barbara Minto alongside the Pyramid Principle) because executive audiences process information through narrative structure. Situation sets the context the reader already knows; Complication introduces what has changed or what is at stake; Question frames the problem; Answer delivers the insight. Reports that simply list facts without narrative architecture fail to move executives toward decisions.

**Weight:** 4%

The CTA feels like a natural next step in the narrative, not a marketing insert. Transitions between sections are explicit and purposeful.

- **5–6 (Adequate):** Basic structure is present but sections feel independent rather than part of a unified narrative. Transitions are abrupt or absent.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** No discernible narrative arc. Sections are disconnected. The report reads as a collection of independent analyses.
- **1–2 (Poor):** No narrative coherence. Random ordering of information with no connective tissue.

### 4.3 Internal Consistency & Cross-Referencing

**Definition:** The degree to which data points, recommendations, assumptions, and conclusions across different sections of the report are mutually consistent — and the degree to which sections explicitly reference each other to build a cohesive analytical structure.

**Why it matters:** In multi-section advisory reports, internal inconsistency is one of the most common quality failures. McKinsey's quality review process includes a dedicated "consistency check" phase where reviewers verify that numbers, assumptions, and conclusions align across all sections of a deliverable. For the AI Mirror specifically, the risk is amplified because outputs are generated programmatically from multiple knowledge sources — making it easy for the Monte Carlo parameters to reference one sector while the competency mapping references another, or for the risk assessment to highlight a threat that the SWOT analysis omits.

**Weight:** 3%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Every quantitative figure cited in multiple sections is identical across all mentions. The sector identified in the competency mapping is the same sector used for Monte Carlo parameters, addressable market sizing, competitive landscape analysis, and regulatory coverage. Risks discussed in the SWOT appear in the risk mitigation section. Capital estimates in the entry strategy align with capital requirements in the resource section. Timeline milestones in the quick wins section are consistent with the phased timeline in the full report. Cross-references between sections are explicit ("As the Monte Carlo analysis in Section 1B indicates...").
- **7–8 (Strong):** Core figures and sectors are consistent across sections. Most cross-references hold. Minor inconsistencies in secondary details.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Generally consistent but with noticeable discrepancies — a figure cited as "$X" in one section becomes "$Y" in another, or the competitive assessment contradicts the SWOT.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Multiple inconsistencies that undermine credibility. Sections appear to have been generated independently without integration.
- **1–2 (Poor):** Pervasive inconsistencies. The report contradicts itself across sections.

**Common Failure Modes:**
- Monte Carlo using "Technology & AI" sector parameters while the competency mapping recommends an "Education & Workforce" pivot
- GDP growth rate cited as 5.2% in the macroeconomic context but 4.8% in the Monte Carlo assumptions table
- Risk mitigation section addressing risks not mentioned in the SWOT
- Entry strategy recommending a JV while the capital requirements section prices a greenfield operation
- Timeline saying "24 months to pilot" in one section and "12 months to pilot" in another

### 4.4 Decision-Enabling Structure

**Definition:** The degree to which the report is structured to enable executive decision-making — presenting information in a hierarchy that surfaces the most important findings first, provides clear decision points, and manages cognitive load through progressive disclosure.

**Why it matters:** Research on executive communication (Cialdini, 2006; Kahneman, 2011; Minto, 1987) consistently establishes that decision-makers need information structured differently from how analysts produce it. Analysts work bottom-up (data → analysis → conclusion); executives consume top-down (conclusion → evidence → detail). The Pyramid Principle codifies this: lead with the answer, support with key findings, ground in evidence. The AI Mirror must present each section with the decision-enabling insight first, followed by the supporting evidence, rather than building up to the conclusion.

**Weight:** 3%

**Scoring Rubric (1–10):**

- **9–10 (World-Class):** Each section leads with its most important finding or recommendation (insight headline, not topic headline). Supporting evidence follows in priority order. The attendee can read only the first sentence of each section and have a complete picture of the analysis. Data tables and detailed evidence are positioned to support the headline finding, not to precede it. The Monte Carlo section leads with the headline probability finding, not with methodology. Visual hierarchy (card sizing, color weight, positioning) reinforces information priority. A time-pressed executive can scan the document in 2 minutes and extract the key messages.
- **7–8 (Strong):** Most sections lead with findings. Some sections bury the insight under descriptive setup. Visual hierarchy mostly aligns with information priority.
- **5–6 (Adequate):** Structure is present but analytical — builds up to conclusions rather than leading with them. Some sections require full reading to extract the key message.
- **3–4 (Below Standard):** Information is presented in analytical order (data first, conclusion last) with no executive summary treatment. Cognitive load is high.
- **1–2 (Poor):** No discernible information hierarchy. Dense text with no structural guidance.

## 5. Composite Scoring Methodology

### 5.1 Weighted Score Calculation

The overall quality score is computed as:

**Overall Score = Σ (Dimension Score × Dimension Weight)**

Where dimension weights are (summing to exactly 100%):

| # | Dimension | Weight |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 3.1 | Analytical Rigor & Data Integrity | 12% |
| 3.2 | Strategic Insight Depth | 12% |
| 3.3 | Actionability & Specificity | 10% |
| 3.4 | Comprehensiveness & Coverage | 8% |
| 3.5 | Personalization & Relevance | 11% |
| 3.6 | Market & Competitive Accuracy | 8% |
| 3.7 | Risk & Uncertainty Handling | 8% |
| 3.8 | Quantitative Modeling Quality | 8% |
| 3.9 | Presentation & Communication Quality | 6% |
| 3.10 | Ethical Integrity & Anti-Hallucination | 10% |
| 4.1 | Cultural & Operational Context | 4% |
| 4.2 | Narrative Coherence & Persuasive Architecture | 4% |
| 4.3 | Internal Consistency & Cross-Referencing | 3% |
| 4.4 | Decision-Enabling Structure | 3% |
| 4.5 | Competitive Intelligence Specificity | 2% |
| | **Total** | **100%** |

**Formula:**

Overall Score = Σ (Dimension Score × Dimension Weight / 100)

This produces a score on a 1–10 scale. Example: if every dimension scores 7, the overall score is 7.0. The weights reflect relative importance, with Analytical Rigor and Strategic Insight carrying the heaviest weight (12% each), followed by Personalization (11%) and Actionability (10%) — these are the dimensions that most distinguish world-class work from adequate work in this specific context. Ethical Integrity at 10% reflects its critical importance while avoiding double-counting with the floor rule that already prevents catastrophic ethical failures from being masked.

### 5.2 Minimum Threshold Rules

These rules prevent high scores in some dimensions from masking critical failures:

1. **Ethical Integrity Floor:** A report scoring below **6** on Dimension 3.10 cannot receive an overall score above 5.0. A score below 6 indicates that promotional function has compromised analytical integrity, fabrications are present, or disclosure standards are not met — any of which is disqualifying for advisory work.

2. **Analytical Rigor Floor:** A report scoring below **5** on Dimension 3.1 cannot receive an overall score above 5.5. A score below 5 means fewer than half of quantitative claims are sourced — this is not analysis.

3. **Personalization Floor:** A report scoring below **5** on Dimension 3.5 cannot receive an overall score above 6.0. A score below 5 means the report reads as an industry report, not a personalized analysis.

4. **Internal Consistency Floor:** A report scoring below **5** on Dimension 4.3 cannot receive an overall score above 6.5. A score below 5 means multiple contradictions undermine cross-section credibility.

5. **Accuracy Floor:** A report with **2 or more** confirmed factual errors (incorrect figures, fabricated institutions, wrong regulatory citations) detected during the mandatory spot-check (Section 3.6.1) automatically caps Dimension 3.6 at the levels specified in that section and triggers a 0.5-point deduction from the composite score.

### 5.3 Relative Scoring (Comparing Two Reports)

When comparing Report A vs. Report B:

1. Score each report independently using the same rubric
2. Present side-by-side dimension scores in a comparison table
3. Compute overall scores for each
4. Apply minimum threshold rules to each independently
5. Identify which dimensions drive the score difference — rank dimensions by absolute score gap
6. Note any dimensions where the score gap exceeds 3 points — these are the critical differentiators and should be highlighted in the comparison narrative
7. For dimensions where both reports score identically, note this as an area of parity
8. Provide a summary assessment identifying: (a) which report is stronger and by how much, (b) the 2–3 dimensions most responsible for the gap, and (c) what the weaker report would need to change to close the gap

### 5.4 Score Calibration Anchors

To ensure scoring consistency across evaluations, the following anchors define what specific scores require in countable terms. These supplement (not replace) the qualitative rubrics.

**Score of 9 on any dimension requires ALL of the following:**
- Zero identified failures against the dimension's rubric criteria
- The Evaluator can cite at least 3 specific examples from the report demonstrating world-class performance on this dimension
- The Evaluator cannot identify a single specific improvement that would materially strengthen this dimension

**Score of 8 on any dimension requires:**
- No more than 1 identified failure against the dimension's rubric criteria
- The Evaluator can cite at least 2 specific examples demonstrating strong performance
- The Evaluator can identify 1–2 specific improvements, but these are refinements not structural gaps

**Score of 7 on any dimension requires:**
- No more than 2 identified failures
- The Evaluator can cite at least 1 strong example
- Improvements identified are substantive but do not undermine the dimension's overall quality

**Scores of 6 and below** follow the existing qualitative rubrics.

**Mandatory justification for scores of 9 or 10:**
Any dimension scored at 9 or 10 requires the Evaluator to provide explicit written justification citing specific evidence from the report. A score of 9 or 10 without justification is invalid and should be treated as an 8 for composite calculation purposes.

### 5.5 Anti-Inflation Instruction for AI Evaluators

If this framework is being applied by an AI system, the following calibration instruction applies:

**Your default tendency is to score too high.** This is a known property of language models operating as evaluators. To counteract this:

1. **Start from the middle.** For each dimension, begin by asking: "Does this report meet the standard described at level 5–6 (Adequate)?" Only move upward if you can cite specific evidence of performance above that level. Do not start at 8–9 and look for reasons to deduct.

2. **A score of 9 should feel uncomfortable.** If you are not hesitant about assigning a 9, you are probably wrong. A 9 means you cannot identify a single material improvement. For most dimensions on most reports, this is not true.

3. **Check your distribution.** If more than 40% of your dimension scores are 9 or above, your scoring is almost certainly inflated. In a well-calibrated evaluation of a strong report, you should expect most scores in the 7–8 range with a few 9s and a few 6–7s.

4. **Name what's wrong before naming what's right.** For each dimension, identify the weaknesses first, then the strengths. This counteracts the tendency to lead with praise and then struggle to deduct from it.

5. **Apply the "would McKinsey ship this?" test.** For any dimension scored 9 ("World-Class"), ask: would a senior McKinsey engagement manager, reviewing this specific dimension of the deliverable, approve it without requesting changes? If the honest answer is "they'd probably ask for refinements," the score is 8, not 9.

## 6. Quality Tier Definitions

### Tier 1 — World-Class (8.5–10.0)
**Characteristics:** Every dimension scores 7 or above. The analysis is genuinely personalized, factually grounded, strategically insightful, and beautifully presented. Monte Carlo simulation is rigorous with fully documented parameters. A Fortune 500 executive would find this report credible and professionally valuable. This is the quality level produced by senior engagement teams at McKinsey, BCG, or the IFC's best country diagnostics — adapted to the personalized advisory context.

**Who produces work at this level:** Senior McKinsey/BCG engagement teams with dedicated analytical support; IFC investment appraisal teams; EIU's best country analyses; top-tier boutique strategy firms specializing in Africa (e.g., Africa Practice, Dalberg).

**Distinguishing marker:** The attendee reads the analysis and discovers something about their own business they hadn't previously considered. The competency-to-gap mapping feels revelatory, not obvious.

**Tier 1 Boundary Test:** A report cannot be classified as Tier 1 (or described as "near Tier 1" or "at the doorstep of Tier 1") unless ALL of the following are true:
- No dimension scores below 7
- The mandatory spot-check (Section 3.6.1) produces zero errors
- The Monte Carlo either models correlations or explicitly discloses and assesses the independence assumption (Section 3.8.2)
- The promotional function test (Section 3.10) is passed — at least 2–3 findings that could discourage the recommended action
- For enterprise-scale attendees ($250M+), opportunity cost is addressed

A report that fails any of these tests is Tier 2 regardless of its composite score. The Evaluator should not characterize Tier 2 reports as "almost Tier 1" or "at the doorstep of Tier 1" — this language implies that the gap is marginal when the boundary tests indicate structural issues.

### Tier 2 — Professional Grade (7.0–8.4)
**Characteristics:** Strong across most dimensions with no critical failures. Analysis is personalized and data-grounded. Some dimensions may have room for improvement but none are below adequate. Monte Carlo is functional and documented. Would be credible coming from a mid-tier consulting firm or Big Four advisory practice.

**Who produces work at this level:** Big Four advisory practices (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG Africa practices); mid-tier management consultancies; well-resourced investment promotion agencies.

**Distinguishing marker:** The attendee finds the analysis useful and would act on several recommendations. The report is professional but may not surprise the reader with unexpected insight.

### Tier 3 — Competent (5.5–6.9)
**Characteristics:** Analysis is present and mostly correct but lacks depth in several areas. Personalization is visible but intermittent. Some sections feel templated. Monte Carlo may be present but parameters are not fully documented. Risk treatment is superficial. Would be acceptable as a first-pass analysis but would require significant enhancement before being decision-grade.

**Who produces work at this level:** Junior consulting teams; automated market report generators with some customization; standard investment promotion agency reports.

**Distinguishing marker:** The attendee finds some useful information but doesn't feel the analysis was truly written for them. The report could apply to several similar businesses with minimal changes.

### Tier 4 — Below Standard (4.0–5.4)
**Characteristics:** Significant gaps in coverage, depth, or accuracy. Personalization is minimal. Several sections are generic or underdeveloped. Monte Carlo may be missing or decorative. Risk treatment is inadequate. Would not be credible as a deliverable from a professional advisory firm.

**Distinguishing marker:** The attendee notices gaps, generic content, or questionable data. Trust in the analysis is undermined.

### Tier 5 — Inadequate (<4.0)
**Characteristics:** Fundamental failures in accuracy, personalization, or comprehensiveness. May contain fabricated data. Analysis does not meet the minimum standard for professional advisory output. A recipient would lose confidence in the system producing it.

**Distinguishing marker:** The attendee questions whether meaningful analysis was done. Fabricated information or template-level content is evident.

### Technology & AI

- Should note Ghana's Data Protection Act (2012) and its implications for data-intensive businesses

- Addressable market figures for digital infrastructure must be triangulated from at least 2 independent sources. Single-source market sizing for a sector this nascent does not meet the 8+ standard.
- AI/ML talent availability claims must reference specific institutional output data (e.g., annual computer science graduates from named universities, not just "growing tech ecosystem")
- The competitive landscape must address global hyperscaler presence and strategy (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) and assess whether their entry or expansion would compress the addressable opportunity
- Data sovereignty and localization trends must be assessed as both opportunity (drives local infrastructure demand) and risk (may constrain business models that depend on cross-border data flows)

### Energy & Infrastructure
- Must include LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy) benchmarks for the relevant energy type, sourced from IRENA
- Should reference the Ghana Energy Commission, Volta River Authority (VRA), and Electricity Company of Ghana (ECG)
- Must address grid reliability and off-grid opportunity, including the IPP (Independent Power Producer) framework
- IRENA data should be cited for renewable energy projections
- Infrastructure gap data should reference specific corridors or zones (Accra–Tema metropolitan area vs. Northern Region; Tema Port vs. Takoradi Port)
- Should note the Millennium Development Authority (MiDA) compact and power sector reform progress where relevant

### Real Estate & Construction
- Must address land tenure system complexities (allodial title, customary freehold, leasehold, the role of traditional stools and skins)
- Should reference the Lands Commission and registration timelines (which remain lengthy despite reform efforts)
- Construction material supply chain (cement availability via GHACEM/Diamond Cement, import dependencies) should be noted
- Should distinguish between Accra/Tema metro (high demand, premium pricing) and secondary cities (Kumasi, Tamale, Takoradi — lower entry costs, growing demand)
- Housing deficit estimates should be cited with methodology disclosure

### Financial Services & Fintech
- Must address Bank of Ghana regulatory framework for fintech, including the e-money issuer licensing framework
- Mobile money ecosystem (MTN MoMo market dominance, interoperability via Ghana Interbank Payment and Settlement Systems — GhIPSS) must be discussed
- Should reference the Payment Systems and Services Act (2019) and any subsequent amendments
- Should note the regulatory sandbox availability or absence
- Ghana's relatively high banking penetration (compared to West African peers) vs. underserved segments should be noted
- Microfinance and savings/loans sector dynamics relevant to the attendee's opportunity

### Agriculture & Food Systems
- Must distinguish between smallholder and commercial agriculture dynamics (the vast majority of farmers are smallholders)
- Should reference the Planting for Food and Jobs (PFJ) initiative or its successors
- Cold chain and post-harvest loss statistics should be included (estimated at 20–40% for perishables depending on crop)
- Export crop vs. domestic consumption market sizing should be differentiated
- Should note the role of aggregators, cooperatives, and outgrower schemes in the value chain
- Climate variability and irrigation infrastructure gaps should be addressed for crop-specific analyses

### Healthcare
- Should reference the National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS) and its coverage limitations
- Should note the doctor-to-patient ratio (approximately 1:10,000+ nationally, with extreme regional variation) and nurse staffing challenges
- Pharmaceutical import dependency should be quantified (Ghana imports an estimated 70%+ of medicines)
- Medical device regulatory pathway through the Food and Drugs Authority (FDA Ghana) should be addressed
- Should distinguish between private and public healthcare dynamics and the growing medical tourism segment

### Manufacturing & Supply Chain
- Tema Free Zone and related export processing zone incentives must be addressed, including duty-free import provisions, tax holidays, and the specific application process through the Ghana Free Zones Authority
- Should note ECOWAS trade corridor dynamics and Ghana's position as a gateway to the West African market of 400+ million people
- Should reference AfCFTA (African Continental Free Trade Area) implications for cross-border trade and rules of origin
- Local content requirements should be noted where applicable
- Raw material sourcing (local vs. imported) and supply chain reliability should be assessed
- Labor cost comparisons with competing manufacturing destinations should be included where relevant
- Should reference the One District One Factory (1D1F) initiative and its implications for manufacturing incentives

### Education & Workforce Development
- Should reference Ghana's Education Strategic Plan and Free SHS (Senior High School) policy implications for workforce pipeline
- TVET (Technical and Vocational Education and Training) infrastructure gaps should be quantified (the Council for Technical and Vocational Education and Training — COTVET — is the relevant body)
- Youth unemployment rates and demographic dividend dynamics should be noted (Ghana's median age is approximately 21)
- Should distinguish between Accra/Kumasi talent markets and secondary city availability
- Digital skills readiness and English language proficiency (Ghana's advantage as an Anglophone country in West Africa) should be highlighted where relevant
- Should note the growing demand for private education and training services, particularly in technical and digital skills

## 8. Emerging Market-Specific Quality Modifiers

These quality expectations are specific to emerging market analyses and do not apply to developed-market reports.

### Currency Risk Treatment
Reports must address Ghana cedi/USD dynamics explicitly. The cedi has experienced significant depreciation historically (including periods of double-digit annual depreciation), and any revenue projection in USD must account for exchange rate risk. A report that projects revenue only in cedis or only in USD without conversion assumptions fails this standard. The Monte Carlo simulation should include exchange rate as a stochastic variable with parameters sourced from Bank of Ghana data.

### Infrastructure Reality Check
Developed-market assumptions about power reliability, internet connectivity, road quality, port throughput, and water access do not apply. Every recommendation that depends on infrastructure should note the specific infrastructure reality in the proposed operating area. For example, power reliability varies dramatically between Accra (relatively stable with periodic outages) and northern Ghana (frequent and prolonged load-shedding). An analysis that recommends data center operations without addressing backup power requirements, or cold chain logistics without noting the state of rural electricity access, fails this test. The Evaluator should check whether infrastructure is treated as a background assumption (wrong) or as a variable that shapes the strategy (right).

### Informal Economy Awareness
In Ghana, a significant portion of economic activity occurs outside formal statistical capture — estimates range from 70–85% of total employment, depending on the methodology used. Market sizing that relies solely on formal sector data will systematically underestimate addressable markets in retail, food, construction, and services — and overestimate them in sectors where the formal/informal boundary matters for the attendee's business model. A world-class analysis will note this dynamic and, where relevant, adjust addressable market estimates to reflect informal activity. For example, a report sizing Ghana's food processing market using only registered food processing companies would miss the vast majority of the market — most food processing occurs at the micro-enterprise and household level. The Evaluator should look for evidence that the analyst understands and accounts for this dual-economy structure.

### Data Scarcity Protocols
When sector-specific data for Ghana is unavailable, acceptable approaches include: (a) using parent-sector estimates with explicit disclosure ("Addressable market estimated from broader ICT sector due to limited sub-sector data for AI/ML services specifically"); (b) triangulating from multiple proxy indicators (e.g., mobile money transaction volumes as a proxy for financial sector activity, or building permits issued as a proxy for construction market growth); (c) using West African regional data with country-level adjustment factors based on GDP ratios or population shares; (d) bottom-up market sizing from unit economics and known demand signals. Using adjacent-country data (e.g., Nigeria or Kenya) without adjustment is not acceptable — these are different markets with different regulatory environments, consumer behaviors, and competitive landscapes. The Evaluator should verify that any data scarcity is disclosed rather than concealed through invented figures.

### Regulatory Velocity
In emerging markets, regulatory frameworks change faster than in developed economies. Citations should include the year/date of the referenced regulation. Recommendations should note that regulatory verification with local counsel is advisable. Any regulatory incentive cited should be verified as currently active. The Evaluator should flag any regulatory citation that lacks a date or that references a framework more than 3 years old without noting potential changes.

### Political Economy & Governance Context
Reports should acknowledge Ghana's governance context with appropriate nuance. Ghana is widely recognized as one of Africa's most stable democracies with a strong tradition of peaceful power transitions. However, governance indicators (World Bank Worldwide Governance Indicators, Mo Ibrahim Foundation Index of African Governance) fluctuate, and the political party in power influences policy priorities. Upcoming electoral cycles and their potential impact on sector-specific policies should be noted where relevant. The Evaluator should check that governance is treated neither as a blanket positive ("Ghana is stable, no concerns") nor as a blanket negative, but with the specificity that the attendee's sector requires.

### Diaspora Network Effects
For the AI Mirror's specific audience — Houston-based executives, many of whom have African heritage — diaspora networks represent a genuine market entry accelerant that most generic emerging market frameworks fail to capture. Ghanaian and broader West African diaspora communities in Houston and across the U.S. serve as bridge-builders for trust, local knowledge, regulatory navigation, and partnership formation. Reports that acknowledge and leverage this dynamic where relevant to the attendee's profile demonstrate superior contextual awareness. The Evaluator should note whether this factor is addressed when the attendee's profile suggests diaspora relevance.

## 9. AI-Generated Content Quality Modifiers

These additional quality expectations apply specifically to AI-generated or AI-assisted analysis.

### Hallucination Indicators to Watch For
- Organizations, programs, or government agencies that sound plausible but do not exist
- Statistics that are suspiciously round or convenient (e.g., "Ghana's healthcare market is growing at exactly 12% annually")
- Regulatory incentives or tax provisions described in detail but not verifiable
- Named companies described as "operating in Ghana" without verification
- Case studies or success stories that cannot be independently confirmed
- Date-specific claims about recent events that may not have occurred
- Addresses, phone numbers, or URLs that appear specific but are fabricated
- Government ministry names or institutional structures that do not match current Ghanaian governance organization

### Source Verification Depth
For AI-generated analyses, the expected standard is higher than for human-written analyses because AI models are known to confabulate. Every quantitative claim should have an identifiable source path. The Evaluator should spot-check 3–5 data points per output section by attempting to trace them to the knowledge files or verifiable public sources.

### Probabilistic Language Compliance
All Monte Carlo outputs and forward-looking projections should use exclusively probabilistic framing. The Evaluator should flag any instance of deterministic language ("your revenue will be," "you will earn," "the market will grow to") in contexts where uncertainty exists. Acceptable framing: "In 75% of simulated scenarios," "the median projected outcome is," "the P25 (conservative) scenario suggests."

### Disclosure Standards
AI-generated analyses should include: (a) a disclaimer noting that the analysis is AI-generated and should be validated with local legal counsel and financial advisors; (b) source citations for key data points; (c) Monte Carlo methodology disclosure including limitations and what the model does not capture; (d) acknowledgment that the analysis does not constitute financial, legal, or investment advice; (e) list of primary data sources used (World Bank, IMF, Bank of Ghana, GIPC, Ghana Statistical Service, UNCTAD, AfDB, U.S. Commercial Service, and sector-specific sources).

### Conflict of Interest Transparency
The AI Mirror serves a dual function: analytical tool and business development funnel for Nexten Summit Accra 2026. This is a documented conflict of interest. The Evaluator should assess whether this dual purpose is managed transparently. Specifically: (a) the CTA section must be visually and structurally separated from the analytical sections — it must not masquerade as a research finding; (b) risks and challenges must be presented with the same analytical depth as opportunities — the promotional objective must not suppress negative findings; (c) the overall tone should be warm and inviting (Africa as opportunity) without tipping into hype or overselling that a sophisticated executive would perceive as marketing rather than analysis.

The Evaluator should apply a specific test: count the number of substantive analytical findings in the report that could discourage the recommended action. If this count is zero, the report fails the analytical independence test and Dimension 3.10 cannot score above 6, regardless of other ethical compliance factors. A score of 7+ requires at least 2–3 findings that a reasonable reader could interpret as reasons for caution, delay, or alternative action.

### 9.5 Self-Evaluation Bias Disclosure

When the Evaluator Agent is operated by the same entity that produced the report being evaluated, or when the Evaluator Agent uses the same underlying AI model that generated the report, this constitutes a conflict of interest that must be disclosed in the evaluation document.

**Required disclosure language (to appear at the top of any evaluation):**

> "This evaluation was produced by [system name], which is operated by [entity name]. The report under evaluation was also produced by / produced for [same entity]. This self-evaluation relationship may introduce systematic bias toward score inflation. The evaluation should be treated as an internal quality check, not an independent assessment."

**Scoring impact:** An evaluation that does not include this disclosure when the self-evaluation condition applies has its composite score automatically marked as "UNAUDITED — self-evaluation bias not disclosed." This does not change the numerical score but flags it for any reader as potentially inflated.

**Rationale:** No amount of rubric sophistication eliminates the fundamental problem of self-evaluation. The most honest response is disclosure. This is standard practice in financial auditing (auditors disclose relationships with audit clients), academic peer review (reviewers disclose conflicts), and consulting (firms disclose when they evaluate their own prior work). The framework should apply the same standard.
