## GIPC Registration Requirements

All foreign investment projects must register with the **Ghana Investment Promotion Centre (GIPC)** after incorporation and before commencing business. Registration is governed by the **GIPC Act 2013 (Act 865)**. Per the GIPC Act, all foreign companies are required to register with GIPC after incorporation with the Office of the Registrar of Companies (ORC).

**Current Minimum Capital Requirements (as of early 2026):**

| Business Type | Minimum Foreign Capital |
|---|---|
| Joint venture with Ghanaian partner (Ghanaian holds ≥10% equity) | US$200,000 |
| Wholly foreign-owned enterprise | US$500,000 |
| Trading enterprise (buying/selling imported goods/services) — wholly foreign | US$1,000,000 |
| External company (incorporated abroad, branch in Ghana) | No capital requirement (higher registration fees ~US$11,000) |
| Free Zone enterprise | No minimum capital requirement |

Capital requirement may be met in **cash or capital goods** (machinery, equipment) relevant to the investment, or a combination of both, transferred through the banking system. The local authorized bank must confirm the import of capital with the Bank of Ghana, which then confirms the transaction to GIPC for investment registration purposes. Trading companies are also required to **employ at least 20 skilled Ghanaian nationals**.

**CRITICAL PENDING REFORM — Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Bill, 2025:**

Ghana's Parliament is considering the **Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Bill, 2025**, a comprehensive new investment law that would:

- **Rename GIPC** to the "Ghana Investment Promotion Authority" with enhanced regulatory powers
- **Abolish minimum capital requirements** for joint ventures ($200K) and wholly foreign-owned enterprises ($500K)
- **Reduce the trading enterprise threshold** from US$1,000,000 to **US$500,000** (Lexology, October 2025)
- **Remove sector restrictions** currently barring foreign participation in 8 reserved business activities
- **Replace biennial registration renewal** with annual renewal
- **Introduce a risk-based screening system** to assess investor credibility, project viability, and compliance with environmental and labor standards
- **Limit Technology Transfer Agreements** to initial durations of 5 years (down from 10 years)
- **Criminalize "fronting"** (using a Ghanaian as a proxy for an unauthorized foreign business) with penalties up to GHS 48,000

The Bill was introduced to Parliament's **Trade, Industry, and Tourism Committee** in late 2025. President Mahama announced the reform at TICAD-9 in Japan in August 2025. FDI inflows rose 382% in H1 2025, and policymakers believe removing capital barriers will further accelerate investment. However, local business groups (GUTA, AGI) have cautioned about potential displacement of Ghanaian small traders. The Association of Ghana Industries stated that "the liberalisation of capital requirements must be balanced with robust local content and partnership policies" (High Street Journal, October 2025). This is the **most significant overhaul of Ghana's investment policy in over a decade.**

**Status as of April 12, 2026:** ✅ **PASSED BY PARLIAMENT on March 26, 2026** — during the 31st Sitting of the 1st Meeting of the 2nd Session of the 9th Parliament (GhanaMPs.com; Ghanaian Times; MyJoyOnline; GNBCC; GhanaWeb). The Bill was one of 10 bills passed during the February 3 – March 27, 2026 parliamentary session. **⚠️ PRESIDENTIAL ASSENT PENDING.** As of April 12, 2026, President Mahama has assented to 5 of the 10 bills passed during this session (Security and Intelligence Agencies Act; University of Engineering and Agricultural Sciences Act; Ghana Deposit Protection Amendment Act; Growth and Sustainability Levy Amendment Act; Education Regulatory Bodies Amendment Act) — the GIPA Bill is **not among them** (Graphic Online, April 1, 2026; Ghanaian Times; GBC Ghana Online). The GIPA Bill awaits presidential assent and gazette publication before it enters into force. **All existing GIPC Act 2013 (Act 865) requirements — including the $200K/$500K/$1M capital thresholds and the 8 reserved sector restrictions — remain in force until the GIPA Act is formally enacted.** However, given that the Bill has passed Parliament with no reported opposition sufficient to block it, investors should plan for the new framework to take effect in the near term. Consult local legal counsel for the latest status.

**Automatic Immigration Quotas (work/residence permits):** Under GIPC Act 865, Section 35, companies with paid-up capital of $50,000–<$250,000 receive 1 automatic immigrant quota; $250,000–<$500,000 receive 2; $500,000–<$700,000 receive 3; >$700,000 receive 4. Additional quotas may be applied for based on demonstrated need.

## Sectors Reserved for Ghanaians (Excluded from Foreign Investment)

Under the GIPC Act 2013, non-Ghanaians **cannot** engage in:
1. Petty trading, hawking, or selling goods in a market stall
2. Taxi or car hire service with fewer than 25 vehicles
3. Beauty salons or barber shops
4. Printing of telecom recharge scratch cards
5. Production of exercise books and basic stationery
6. Retail of finished pharmaceutical products
7. Production, supply, and retail of sachet water
8. Pool betting and lotteries (except football pools)

**Reform Status — GIPA Bill PASSED Parliament March 26, 2026:** The Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Bill, 2025, which passed Parliament on March 26, 2026 (awaiting presidential assent), proposes to **remove all eight sector restrictions** and permit foreign entities to freely invest in all sectors of the Ghanaian economy. The Bill's memorandum states the existing reservation conflicts with relevant legislation and the mandate of several sector-specific regulators. However, **strong and vocal opposition exists** from local business associations — GUTA (Ghana Union of Traders Associations) and AGI (Association of Ghana Industries) — who fear displacement of Ghanaian small traders and entrepreneurs. AGI stated that "the liberalisation of capital requirements must be balanced with robust local content and partnership policies" (High Street Journal, October 2025). **⚠️ The Bill has passed Parliament, but presidential assent has not yet been granted as of April 12, 2026. All 8 sector restrictions remain in force under the GIPC Act 2013 until the GIPA Act is formally enacted. Investors should not assume the reserved sectors are open until the enacted text is confirmed and gazetted. Given the political sensitivity of opening petty trading, pharmaceutical retail, and other small-enterprise sectors to foreign competition, investors should also monitor whether implementing regulations modify or phase in these changes.**

## Foreign Ownership Rules

- **100% foreign ownership** is permitted in most sectors under the GIPC Act. United States investors are treated the same as other foreign investors (U.S. State Dept Investment Climate Statement 2025).
- **Free Zones:** 100% foreign ownership explicitly allowed with no restrictions on profit repatriation.
- **Sector-specific restrictions** exist in multiple regulated sectors, each with their own governing bodies and requirements:

| Sector | Regulator | Key Restrictions |
|---|---|---|
| **Banking** | Bank of Ghana | Minimum capital: GHS 400 million for universal banking license. BoG approval required. |
| **Mining** | Minerals Commission | Local content requirements apply. Mining lease requires government equity participation (up to 10% free carried interest). |
| **Petroleum (upstream)** | Petroleum Commission | Local content requirements under Petroleum (Local Content and Local Participation) Regulations, 2013 (LI 2204). GNPC carried interest. |
| **Fishing** | Fisheries Commission | Industrial fishing licenses have local partnership requirements. |
| **Telecommunications** | National Communications Authority (NCA) | License required. Spectrum allocation regulated. |
| **Insurance** | National Insurance Commission | Minimum capital requirements. |
| **Securities** | Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) | Licensing and capital requirements. |
| **Energy** | Energy Commission / PURC | Licensing and tariff regulation. |
| **Real Estate** | Lands Commission | Foreigners cannot own freehold land (see Land section below). |

## Business Registration Process

Registration is handled through the **Office of the Registrar of Companies (ORC)**, which was inaugurated in July 2022 and took over registration functions from the Registrar General's Department. The ORC operates under the **Companies Act 2019 (Act 992)**.

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Reserve company name** — ORC (online at rgdeservices.com): 1–2 business days
2. **Incorporate company** — ORC: 5–10 business days. Requirements:
   - Minimum 2 shareholders
   - Minimum 2 directors (at least 1 must be resident in Ghana)
   - Company secretary with background in corporate law and administration, or trained under a company secretary for at least 3 years (Companies Act 2019 requirement)
   - No longer required to state business objectives or obtain a certificate to commence business (simplification under Act 992)
3. **Obtain Tax Identification Number (TIN)** — Ghana Revenue Authority (GRA): 1–3 business days
4. **Register with GIPC** — Submit incorporation documents, proof of capital transfer, project description: 5–10 business days. Registration is currently renewed biennially.
5. **Register with SSNIT** (Social Security and National Insurance Trust): within 30 days of hiring employees
6. **Obtain Business Operating Permit** from Metropolitan/Municipal/District Assembly
7. **Environmental Permit** — Required for certain sectors from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)
8. **Open corporate bank account** — requires TIN, incorporation documents, GIPC certificate

**Total timeline:** 2–6 weeks for basic setup. Sector-specific licenses (banking, mining, telecom, etc.) require additional approvals and significantly longer timelines.

### Compliance Requirements

| Requirement | Filing Body | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Annual returns | ORC | Annual |
| Tax returns (corporate) | GRA | Annual (self-assessment); quarterly provisional tax payments |
| VAT returns | GRA | Monthly |
| SSNIT contributions | SSNIT | Monthly (employer 13% + employee 5.5%) |
| GIPC registration renewal | GIPC | Biennial (will become annual under proposed Bill) |
| Transfer pricing return | GRA | Within 4 months of year-end (if applicable) |
| Withholding tax returns | GRA | Monthly |

### Growth and Sustainability Levy (GSL)

Mining companies pay a **Growth and Sustainability Levy** on gross production. The levy was increased to 3% in the 2025 budget, but the **Growth and Sustainability Levy (Amendment) Act, 2026** — signed into law by President Mahama on April 1, 2026 — **reduced it back to 1%** following the introduction of a sliding-scale royalty structure (Graphic Online, April 1, 2026; Ghanaian Times). The sunset clause extends to **2028**. This is in addition to the 10% mining royalty and 35% corporate income tax. The GSL is payable quarterly and is **not deductible** for CIT purposes.

### Financial Sector Recovery Levy (FSRL)

A **5% levy on profit before tax** applies to banks (other than rural/community banks), enacted to support financial sector reforms following the 2017–2019 banking crisis. The levy is not an allowable deduction.

### Emissions Levy — REPEALED

The Emissions Levy Act, 2023 (Act 1112) — which imposed GHS 100 per tonne of CO₂-equivalent emissions per month on construction, manufacturing, mining, oil and gas, and electricity/heating sectors — was **repealed** by the Emissions Levy (Repeal) Bill, 2025, passed by Parliament on March 26, 2025 and signed into law by President Mahama on **April 2, 2025** (Ghana Presidency; Ghana News Agency; Graphic Online). The GRA was directed to halt collection immediately. This levy is **no longer in force.**

**2025–2026 Tax Rationalization:** On April 2, 2025, President Mahama signed into law the repeal of the E-Levy (Electronic Transfer Levy Act 2022, Act 1075), the Emissions Levy (Emissions Levy Act 2023, Act 1112), and the Betting Tax (via Income Tax Amendment Bill 2025) — all on the same day (Ghana Presidency; GNA; Graphic Online, April 3, 2025). The COVID Health Recovery Levy was also abolished. These repeals fulfilled key campaign commitments and materially reduced the tax burden on businesses and individuals. In the **2026 Budget**, the effective VAT rate was further reduced to **~20%** (modification to levies structure), and the VAT registration threshold was raised from GHS 200,000 to **GHS 750,000** — easing compliance for small businesses.

### Sector-Specific Tax Incentives

- **Agriculture/agro-processing:** 5-year tax holiday. Tax rebates for livestock, fish, poultry farming. Imported fishing gear certified by Minister of Fisheries exempt from customs duties and taxes.
- **Manufacturing:** Reduced corporate tax rates based on location. Duty exemptions on raw materials/machinery.
- **Tourism/Hotels:** 22% CIT rate (vs. 25% standard). Tax holidays and customs duty exemptions.
- **Rental income (new construction):** Exempt from tax for first 5 years.
- **Companies selling/leasing premises:** Income tax exempt for first 5 years of operation.
- **Export enterprises:** 8% CIT rate for non-traditional exports. Various duty exemptions and reduced rates.
- **GIPC-registered enterprises:** Entitled to import exemptions for plant inputs, machinery, and parts (Chapters 82, 84, 85, 89 of Customs Code zero-rated).
- **Textiles:** Zero-rate concession for supply of textiles by a local manufacturer was extended to 31 December 2025. **⚠️ Status as of March 2026 unknown — verify whether renewed for 2026 with GRA or qualified tax adviser.**
- **Vehicle assembly:** Zero-rate concession for locally assembled vehicles under the Ghana Automotive Development Programme was extended to 31 December 2025. **⚠️ Status as of March 2026 unknown — verify whether renewed for 2026 with GRA or qualified tax adviser.**
- **Electric vehicles:** Import VAT waiver on EVs for public transportation.
- **Solar/renewable energy equipment:** Exemptions Act 2022 waives import duties and VAT on solar equipment, reducing costs 15–20%.
- **Locally manufactured sanitary towels:** Zero-rated for VAT.

## Transfer Pricing Regulations

Ghana has a well-developed transfer pricing regime, governed by the **Income Tax Act, 2015 (Act 896)** and the **Transfer Pricing Regulations, 2020 (L.I. 2412)**, which replaced the original 2012 regulations. The regime aligns with the OECD's BEPS initiative and Transfer Pricing Guidelines.

### Key Requirements

- **Arm's Length Standard:** All transactions between persons in a controlled relationship must be conducted at arm's length. The Commissioner-General of GRA may adjust chargeable income where transactions do not comply.
- **Documentation:** Contemporaneous documentation required, including a **Master File** (group-level) and **Local File** (entity-level), filed electronically within 4 months of year-end.
- **Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR):** Required for multinational enterprise groups with consolidated revenue exceeding **GHS 2.9 billion** in the prior year. Due within 12 months of reporting year-end.
- **Transfer Pricing Return:** Must be filed separately from the tax return, within 4 months of year-end.
- **Safe Harbor Exemption:** Arrangements below **US$200,000** in value are exempt from full documentation requirements (though GRA may aggregate transactions if avoidance is suspected). Low value-adding intra-group services with a mark-up of 3% or less also qualify for simplified treatment.
- **Technology Transfer Agreements:** Controlled transactions under a GIPC-registered Technology Transfer Agreement with charges ≤2% of net profit qualify for simplified documentation.
- **Approved Methods:** Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP), Resale Price, Cost Plus, Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM), Transactional Profit Split.
- **Enforcement:** The GRA's Transfer Pricing Unit (TPU) within the Large Taxpayer Office is increasingly active. Multinational companies in mining, oil, telecoms, banking, and FMCG face heightened scrutiny. Failure to comply can result in adjustments, back taxes, penalties, and interest.

**Practical Note:** Under IMF programmes and global best practices, Ghana is actively aligning its tax enforcement with OECD transfer pricing principles. Companies should maintain robust documentation proactively.

Applications are submitted through the GRA's online portal.

## Free Zones Program

**Ghana Free Zones Authority (GFZA)** — established August 31, 1995 under the **Free Zone Act 1995 (Act 504)**. Governed by a 9-member Board chaired by the Minister of Trade and Industry. Current CEO: Dr. Mary Awusi.

### Locations & Zones

The GFZA oversees **four primary zones spanning over 7,000 acres** with additional enclaves under development:

| Zone | Location | Size | Focus Areas |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tema Export Processing Zone (TEPZ)** | Tema (24 km from Kotoka Airport, adjacent to Tema Port) | 1,200 acres | Manufacturing, assembly, logistics, agro-processing, textiles, pharmaceuticals. Hosts 50+ companies. |
| **Ashanti Technology Park (Boankra)** | Ashanti Region (20 km from Kumasi) | 1,099 acres | ICT/cyber village, cocoa processing, light/heavy manufacturing, warehousing, biotechnology, BPO |
| **Sekondi Industrial Park** | Sekondi-Takoradi (port city) | 2,200 acres | Heavy industry, oil & gas services |
| **Additional enclaves** | Accra, Kumasi | Various | Export-oriented manufacturing, services |

### Key Incentives

- **100% exemption** from income tax on profits for **10 years**
- Corporate tax capped at **15%** after the 10-year holiday (Free Zone Act 1995, Section 31(2); some GFZA promotional materials and investment guides cite 8% — confirm applicable rate with GFZA for your specific license)
- **100% exemption** from import duties and levies on all imports for production/export
- **Total exemption** from withholding tax on dividends
- Relief from double taxation (where DTTs exist)
- **100% foreign ownership** permitted
- **No restrictions** on repatriation of dividends, profits, loan servicing, fees, or proceeds from sale of interest
- No minimum capital requirement (must show evidence of funding)
- Dedicated **fast-track cargo facilitation** at ports and airports (one-stop regulatory shop for customs, immigration, environmental clearances)
- Temporary waivers allowing up to **10% domestic sales** during economic downturns

### Requirements

- Free Zone enterprises must **export at least 70%** of production.
- Registration required through GFZA.
- Compliance with environmental, labor, and safety standards.

### Non-Traditional Exports Performance

The GFZA CEO reported non-traditional exports reached **$2.25 billion in 2024**, with **$1 billion in Q1 2025 alone** (GhanaWeb, September 2025).

### Pending: Special Economic Zones Bill, 2025

A draft **Special Economic Zones Bill, 2025** is under public consultation by the Business Regulatory Reform unit of the Ministry of Trade, Agribusiness and Industry. The Bill aims to:
- Consolidate and modernize the free zones/industrial parks framework
- Establish a formal SEZ regulatory structure with a one-stop shop
- Expand zones beyond traditional manufacturing into green technology and digital services
- Align with AfCFTA objectives
- Ensure local SMEs, workers, and communities benefit from SEZs

The GFZA publicly endorsed the Bill's passage in September 2025. The AfCFTA Policy Network urged the government to fast-track the Bill. It is currently **pending cabinet approval** before proceeding to Parliament. Ghana does not currently have formal "Special Economic Zones" under law — existing industrial parks are loosely referred to as SEZs.

## Land Ownership for Foreigners

- **Foreigners cannot own freehold land** in Ghana. The 1992 Constitution (Article 266) vests all land in the people of Ghana through their traditional authorities.
- Foreigners can obtain **leasehold interests** for a maximum of **50 years**, renewable.
- Ghanaians can hold leases up to **99 years**.
- Joint ventures with Ghanaian partners can provide structural solutions for land access, with Ghanaian partners holding the freehold and granting long-term leases to the JV entity.

### Land Tenure Systems

| System | Coverage | Manager | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Customary** | ~80% of all land | Chiefs, stools, skins, families, clans | Dominant system. Allocation rights held by traditional leaders. Documentation often informal. |
| **Statutory/Public** | ~20% of all land | Lands Commission | Acquired by government for public purposes. Better documented but registration still incomplete. |

### Key Challenges

- **Multiple titling systems** and documentation gaps create uncertainty. Multiple claims to the same land parcel are common, particularly in Greater Accra and peri-urban areas.
- **The most cited structural obstacle** for real estate investment by foreign investors (U.S. State Dept, SECO, World Bank).
- Transaction costs are high due to extensive verification requirements.
- **Land Act 2020** aimed to consolidate and modernize land laws, but implementation remains incomplete.
- Land registration coverage is limited — the Land Title Registration Law applies in Greater Accra and parts of Ashanti but not nationwide.
- Informal land transactions remain widespread, creating exposure to fraud and disputes.

### Practical Guidance for Foreign Investors

- Engage a **reputable local law firm** with land transaction experience before any land acquisition.
- Conduct thorough due diligence including: search at Lands Commission, consultation with relevant stool/skin/family heads, independent survey, and community inquiries.
- Ensure all transactions are properly documented and registered with the Lands Commission.
- Budget for land verification and registration costs (typically 3–8% of transaction value).
- Consider leasehold structures through a locally incorporated company for operational simplicity.

## Labor Law Essentials

### Wages and Compensation

- **Minimum wage (2026):** GHS 21.77/day (effective January 1, 2026; 9% increase from GHS 19.97 in 2025), set by the National Tripartite Committee. This equates to approximately **US$2.00/day** at current exchange rates (~GHS 10.8/USD) and approximately **GHS 653/month** (based on 30 working days). The minimum wage applies across all sectors.
- **Average gross monthly salary (formal sector):** Approximately GHS 3,800–4,200 (~US$260–290) as of early 2026. Actual wages vary significantly by industry, location, and experience.
- **Public sector:** The government and Organized Labour agreed on a **9% base pay increase** for all public sector workers under the Single Spine Salary Structure (SSSS) for 2026.

### Employment Framework

- **Governing law:** Labour Act 2003 (Act 651).
- **Employment contracts:** Must be in writing for contracts exceeding 6 months. Contracts must specify terms and conditions, job description, remuneration, and notice periods.
- **Working hours:** Standard 8 hours/day, 40 hours/week. Maximum 48 hours/week including overtime.
- **Overtime:** Compensated at **1.5x** the normal hourly rate.
- **Probation:** Terms specified in the employment contract or collective bargaining agreement.
- **Weekly rest:** Employees must receive at least one weekly rest day.

### Work Permits and Immigration

- **Work permits required** for all non-Ghanaians. Issued by Ghana Immigration Service. Processing time: 4–8 weeks.
- Tied to **GIPC immigration quotas** for registered enterprises (1 permit at $50K, 2 at $250K, 3 at $500K, 4 at $700K+).
- Additional quotas may be applied for based on demonstrated operational need.
- Work permits are typically valid for 1–2 years and must be renewed.

### Social Security

- **SSNIT (Social Security and National Insurance Trust):** Mandatory for all employees under the National Pensions Act, 2008 (Act 766).
- **Employer contribution:** 13% of basic salary.
- **Employee contribution:** 5.5% of basic salary.
- **Total:** 18.5% of basic salary.
- **Allocation:** The combined 18.5% is distributed as follows: **13.5% to Tier 1** (the mandatory Basic National Social Security Scheme managed by SSNIT — of which SSNIT retains 11% for pensions and remits 2.5% to the National Health Insurance Authority as the NHIA contribution); and **5% to Tier 2** (the mandatory Occupational Pension Scheme, a defined-contribution scheme managed by privately licensed trustees). The employer remits 13.5% to SSNIT within 14 days of month-end (SSNIT FAQs; PwC Tax Summaries; KPMG Ghana).
- **Tier 3** (voluntary provident fund/personal pension) is optional; contributions up to 16.5% of basic salary are tax-exempt.

### Termination and Severance

- Notice periods range from **2 weeks to 3 months** depending on length of service and terms of employment contract.
- Severance pay regulated under the Labour Act.
- Non-compliance with termination procedures may result in **fines up to GHS 12,000** or potential imprisonment.
- Employees can file complaints with the **National Labour Commission** for unfair dismissal.

### Trade Unions

- Active labor movement. **Ghana Trades Union Congress (TUC)** is the main federation.
- **Collective bargaining** recognized and protected under the Labour Act.
- Employers must not interfere with union activities. Anti-union discrimination is prohibited.
- Note: The 2024 nurses' strike (Ghana Registered Nurses and Midwives Association) over delayed implementation of the 2024 Collective Agreement demonstrates the active role of organized labor in Ghana's economy.

### Practical IP Considerations

- IP enforcement capacity is limited — both administrative and judicial resources are constrained.
- **Budget for brand protection measures:** Companies should proactively monitor for infringement and be prepared to pursue enforcement through the courts or customs seizure mechanisms.
- The Ghana FDA plays a role in pharmaceutical IP enforcement but capacity for comprehensive market surveillance is constrained.

## Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs)

Ghana has active BITs with multiple countries providing investment protection, including:

**Notable BITs:** United Kingdom, Germany, Netherlands, France, Switzerland, China, India, Malaysia, Denmark, Burkina Faso, Serbia, Egypt, Guinea, Romania, and others.

**Key protections typically include:**
- Fair and equitable treatment
- Protection against expropriation without fair, prompt, and adequate compensation
- Free transfer of investment returns, capital, and loan repayments
- National treatment and most-favored-nation treatment
- Access to international arbitration (ICSID, UNCITRAL)

**International Arbitration Conventions:**
- Ghana is a signatory to the **International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID)** Convention
- Ghana is a signatory to the **New York Convention** on Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958)
- Ghana is a member of the **Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA)** — part of the World Bank Group, providing political risk insurance

**Note on AfCFTA:** The AfCFTA Investment Protocol (under negotiation) aims to create a harmonized pan-African investment framework. This may eventually phase out or supersede many intra-African BITs. The timeline for this is still uncertain.

**Note for U.S. Investors:** There is **no bilateral investment treaty between the United States and Ghana.** U.S. investors rely on the protections under the GIPC Act, which provides for non-discrimination, protection against expropriation, and guarantees on profit/capital repatriation. The U.S.-Ghana Trade and Investment Framework Agreement (TIFA) exists but does not provide the same protections as a BIT.

## AfCFTA Implications

The **African Continental Free Trade Area Secretariat** is headquartered in **Accra, Ghana** — a major strategic advantage giving Ghana institutional influence over continental trade rules.

**Market Access:** AfCFTA creates a single market of **1.4 billion people** with a combined GDP of approximately **$3.4 trillion**. Ghana-based businesses gain preferential access to this market.

**Key Benefits for Ghana-Based Companies:**
- **Tariff elimination** on 90%+ of intra-African goods trade (phased implementation — 90% of tariff lines liberalized immediately, remaining 7% as sensitive products, 3% as excluded)
- Simplified and harmonized **rules of origin**
- **Services trade liberalization** (5 priority sectors identified: business services, communications, financial services, tourism, and transport)
- **Investment protocol** (under negotiation — may create common investment rules across Africa)
- **IP protocol** (under negotiation)
- **Competition protocol** (under negotiation)
- Cross-border payment infrastructure: **Pan-African Payment and Settlement System (PAPSS)** enables instant cross-border payments in local currencies, reducing need for dollar intermediation. Ghana is an early adopter.

**Practical Impact:** Companies manufacturing in or exporting from Ghana can access three overlapping markets from a single production base:
1. **ECOWAS (400M+ people)** — duty-free for qualifying goods
2. **AfCFTA (1.4 billion people)** — preferential access as tariff elimination progresses
3. **EU and other global markets** — via GSP preferences and bilateral trade arrangements

Ghana's positioning as AfCFTA host gives it advantages in shaping implementation and attracting companies seeking a continental gateway.

## ECOWAS Trade Benefits

Ghana is a founding member of **ECOWAS** (Economic Community of West African States — 15 member states, 400M+ people).

**ECOWAS Trade Liberalization Scheme (ETLS):**
- Qualifying industrial products from Ghana can be exported to other ECOWAS states **duty-free**
- Agricultural and artisanal products also qualify
- Requires ECOWAS origin certification
- Ghana's ports (Tema, Takoradi) serve as **key transit points** for landlocked ECOWAS states (Burkina Faso, Niger, Mali), generating significant transit trade volume
- The Accra-Kumasi-Tamale-Ouagadougou road corridor is a strategic trade route

**ECOWAS Common External Tariff (CET):** Applied at Ghana's borders for goods from outside ECOWAS. Five bands: 0%, 5%, 10%, 20%, 35%.

**Recent Developments:** Political instability in Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger (military coups since 2020) has created uncertainty in ECOWAS trade flows, but Ghana's ports remain critical infrastructure for the region. The suspension/withdrawal of some Sahel states from ECOWAS may affect future trade dynamics, but the immediate impact on Ghana's transit trade has been limited.

## Dispute Resolution

### Courts

- **Commercial Division of the High Court** handles business disputes. Specialized **Commercial Court** in Accra.
- **Financial and Economic Crimes Court** handles financial fraud and white-collar crimes.
- **Challenges:** Court backlogs can delay commercial dispute resolution significantly. Enforcement of judgments can be slow. Legal costs are relatively high for SMEs.

### Arbitration

- **Ghana Arbitration Centre** provides domestic arbitration services.
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution Act 2010 (Act 798):** Provides comprehensive framework for mediation and arbitration in Ghana.
- International arbitration available through **ICSID, ICC, or LCIA** for foreign investors under BITs.
- **New York Convention:** Foreign arbitral awards are enforceable in Ghana. However, enforcement can be slow due to judicial system capacity constraints.
- **UNCITRAL Model Law:** Ghana's arbitration framework draws on UNCITRAL principles.

### Mediation

- The ADR Act encourages mediation as a first step in commercial disputes.
- Court-annexed mediation programs exist but usage is still developing.

## Currency Controls & Repatriation

- **Foreign exchange regime:** Ghana operates a managed floating exchange rate system. The Bank of Ghana intervenes in the FX market to manage volatility.
- **Current exchange rate (March 2026):** Approximately GHS 10.8/USD.
- **Profit repatriation:** Foreign investors registered with GIPC are **guaranteed the right** to repatriate profits, dividends, loan servicing payments, fees, royalties, and proceeds from the sale of investments through authorized dealer banks.
- **Capital repatriation:** Guaranteed for GIPC-registered investments upon liquidation or sale.
- **Foreign currency accounts:** Permitted for both residents and non-residents, subject to BoG regulations.
- **No surrender requirements:** Foreign exchange earned by exporters does not need to be surrendered to the central bank (though retention requirements may apply in certain sectors).
- **PAPSS:** Pan-African Payment and Settlement System enables cross-border payments in local currencies across participating African countries.

**Risk Note:** Despite the cedi's remarkable ~40% appreciation in 2025, the currency has a long-term depreciation trend. The cedi depreciated from ~GHS 1.5/USD in 2013 to ~GHS 16.0/USD in November 2024 before recovering. All foreign-currency-denominated investments carry exchange rate risk. Hedging options are limited in the Ghanaian FX market.

## Current Regulatory Risks & Pending Changes

### Active Legislative Pipeline

1. **Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Bill, 2025:** Comprehensive overhaul of GIPC Act — removes minimum capital requirements, opens reserved sectors, introduces risk-based screening. **PASSED by Parliament on March 26, 2026.** Awaiting presidential assent as of April 12, 2026. Once enacted, this will be the most significant change to Ghana's investment framework in over a decade.

2. **Special Economic Zones Bill, 2025:** Modernizes free zones framework, aligns with AfCFTA objectives. Under public consultation, pending cabinet approval before Parliament.

3. **Virtual Asset Service Providers Act (2025):** Passed — regulates cryptocurrency and digital asset businesses. Bank of Ghana developing implementation framework. eCedi (CBDC) under development.

4. **Occupational Safety and Health Bill:** Under public consultation on Ghana Business Regulatory Reforms Portal.

5. **Business Regulatory Reform Commission Bill, 2024:** Aims to establish a permanent body overseeing regulatory quality.

6. **Electronic Transactions Bill, 2025:** Comprehensive revision of digital governance legislation by the Ministry of Communication, Digital Technology and Innovations.

### Ongoing Regulatory Risks

7. **Energy Sector Reform:** Electricity tariff adjustments ongoing under IMF program. Energy sector debt ($3.1B; total cleanup requirement $3.7B) creates risk of further tariff increases. ECG distribution losses at 30% in 2024 — a chronic institutional challenge.

8. **Tax Policy Uncertainty:** E-Levy, Emissions Levy, and Betting Tax all abolished on April 2, 2025 — a significant positive signal of tax rationalization. However, government revenue needs are ongoing (the E-Levy alone generated ~GHS 4.9 billion in 2023, and the combined revenue loss from all three repeals creates a fiscal gap), and new levies/taxes could emerge. The effective indirect tax burden (~20% including all levies) is still relatively high. Growth and Sustainability Levy was increased from 1% to 3% for mining companies and extended to 2028.

9. **Local Content Requirements:** Sector-specific local content rules (especially oil & gas, mining) continue to evolve and can create compliance complexity. The GIPA Bill (passed Parliament March 26, 2026; awaiting assent) may further modify local content expectations once enacted.

10. **Land Law Reform:** Ongoing but slow. The Land Act 2020 aimed to consolidate land laws but implementation remains incomplete. This remains the most significant structural barrier for real estate investors.

11. **Environmental Regulation:** EPA enforcement capacity limited but expectations rising, particularly around mining (galamsey crackdown) and industrial operations. Environmental impact assessments required but compliance monitoring weak.

12. **Fiscal Responsibility Framework:** Enacted under the IMF program — targets **primary surplus of 1.5% GDP** and **expenditure growth below 10% annually**. Constrains government spending but improves macroeconomic predictability. The 2025 outturn (primary surplus of 2.6% of GDP) exceeded the target, signaling strong fiscal discipline.

13. **IMF Program Conditionality:** The $3B Extended Credit Facility (ECF) program has one remaining review (6th). Program extension to August 2026. Structural benchmarks include Treasury Single Account integration (target end-May 2026), taxpayer registry cleansing, public procurement reform, and statutory funds reform. Compliance with IMF benchmarks provides fiscal predictability but constrains government policy flexibility.

14. **Galamsey (Illegal Mining) Enforcement:** Government crackdowns on illegal mining are periodic and can create uncertainty for legitimate mining operations. Environmental regulations around water body protection are tightening. Estimated $2.4B annual loss to illegal mining activities.

15. **Lithium Mining Regulatory Uncertainty:** The original Ewoyaa lithium mining lease was laid before Parliament in November 2025 with a reduced 5% royalty. The Minister of Lands and Natural Resources withdrew it on December 10, 2025 following civil society criticism over royalty concessions. A revised lease with a sliding-scale royalty structure (5–12% based on spodumene prices) was resubmitted December 22, 2025. Parliament's Committee on Lands and Natural Resources met February 12, 2026 to review, but **as of March 16, 2026, after 25+ parliamentary sittings, the lease remains unratified** (Radio Tamale, March 16, 2026). Mining investors are expressing growing concern about delays, with some reporting declining confidence in Ghana's mining sector governance. This signals increased Parliamentary scrutiny of extractive industry concessions and has broader implications for investor confidence in strategic minerals development.

## Summary: Regulatory Environment Assessment (March 2026)

**Ghana's regulatory environment is in a period of significant reform.** The GIPA Bill (passed Parliament March 26, 2026; awaiting presidential assent), Special Economic Zones Bill, and multiple modernization initiatives signal a government committed to improving the investment climate. The GIPA Bill's passage removes the most significant legislative uncertainty — capital thresholds and reserved sectors — though **implementation timeline and any modifications via implementing regulations remain to be confirmed** once presidential assent is granted and the Act is gazetted.

**For foreign investors, the practical regulatory calculus is:**

- **The legal framework for investment is well-established** — GIPC Act, Companies Act 2019, Free Zone Act, and sector-specific laws provide a functional (if complex) framework for market entry.
- **Reforms are directionally positive** — removal of capital thresholds, opening of reserved sectors, and modernization of SEZ framework would materially improve the investment climate.
- **Compliance requirements are real** — transfer pricing, tax filings, GIPC registration, work permits, and sector-specific licensing all require careful attention. "Uneven enforcement of rules" is the primary complaint from foreign companies (SECO Economic Report 2025).
- **Local legal counsel is essential** — the complexity of Ghana's regulatory environment, particularly around land, taxation, and sector-specific requirements, demands experienced in-country legal guidance.
- **No U.S.-Ghana tax treaty or BIT** — American investors should structure investments with awareness of the higher withholding tax rates and reliance on GIPC Act protections rather than treaty-level guarantees.
- **Post-crisis macro stability improves regulatory predictability** — the IMF program, fiscal responsibility framework, and central bank independence provide a more predictable macroeconomic policy environment than Ghana has had in years.
