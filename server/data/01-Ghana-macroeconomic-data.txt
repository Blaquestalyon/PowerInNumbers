# Ghana macroeconomic data: comprehensive verification and expansion

**Bottom line: Of 50+ data points examined, roughly 80–85% are confirmed accurate against Tier 1 sovereign and multilateral sources, with several high-stakes figures now corrected.** The document's GDP PPP figure was overstated by ~$100 billion (using a non-standard source), the Q1 2025 GDP growth figure was outdated (revised upward to 6.3% from 5.3%), the debt-to-GDP ratio depends critically on which definition is used, and the IMF program size should read "approximately US$3 billion" rather than US$3.2 billion. Meanwhile, Ghana's 2025 macroeconomic performance dramatically outpaced all institutional forecasts — Q1–Q3 GDP growth averaged 6.1%, the cedi appreciated ~40%, debt-to-GDP fell to 45.3%, and reserves surged to $13.8 billion.

**⚠️ CRITICAL CONCENTRATION RISK:** Ghana's 2025 macroeconomic outperformance is heavily dependent on gold. Gold exports approximately doubled to ~$20 billion in 2025, representing roughly 55–60% of total goods exports. The current account surplus, reserves surge, cedi appreciation, and debt-to-GDP compression are all significantly linked to sustained gold prices above $2,400/oz. A 20% gold price correction would erase approximately $4 billion in annual export revenue, severely stressing the current account surplus, reserves accumulation, and cedi stability. This single-commodity exposure is the most material risk to the macroeconomic narrative presented below, and readers should weigh all positive indicators against it.

## 1. GDP and growth: strong data but one critical error

### Nominal GDP 2024
**Document claims US$82.3 billion — CLOSE BUT SLIGHTLY LOW.**
The World Bank Macro Poverty Outlook (October 2025) records **US$82.9 billion**. The IMF WEO October 2025 implies a similar figure (~US$82.8 billion derived from per capita of $2,406 × population). The FRED/World Bank GDP series (updated December 2025) records US$82.31 billion. The Ghana Statistical Service reports ₵1.2 trillion in local currency (Q4 2024 GDP Bulletin, March 10, 2025). The document's $82.3 billion likely reflects an earlier data vintage. **Recommended correction: US$82.9 billion (World Bank MPO, October 2025).**

### GDP PPP 2024
**Document claims ~$380 billion — ⚠️ MAJOR ERROR.** The official IMF WEO October 2024 figure is **US$269.1 billion**; the World Bank WDI records **US$276.4 billion**. The $380 billion figure matches only World Economics (worldeconomics.com), which applies non-standard adjustments for informal economy size and outdated GDP base years — World Economics itself states its figure is 62% higher than the World Bank's. This is **~$100 billion higher** than every official source. **World Economics figures should never appear in analytical documents without a prominent methodological disclaimer, as they are routinely mistaken for official data.** The document must correct to the IMF/World Bank figure (~$269–276 billion). GDP per capita PPP is **$8,027** (World Bank WDI, 2024).

### GDP growth rate 2024
**Document claims 5.7% — ✅ CONFIRMED across all sources.** GSS Q4 2024 GDP Bulletin (March 10, 2025), World Bank MPO, AfDB, and IMF all report 5.7%. Quarterly breakdown (GSS quarterly GDP bulletins): Q1 4.8%, Q2 7.0%, Q3 7.2%, Q4 3.6%. GDP per capita nominal: **US$2,406.6** (World Bank/IMF, October 2025).

### GDP composition by sector 2024
**Document claims services ~47%, industry ~31%, agriculture ~22% — ✅ CONFIRMED.** GSS annual 2024 data: Services 47.0%, Industry 30.8%, Agriculture 22.2%.

### Q1 2025 GDP growth
**Document claims 5.3% — ⚠️ OUTDATED.** The initial GSS release (June 11, 2025) reported 5.3%, but the Q2 2025 GDP Bulletin (September 10, 2025) **revised this upward to 6.3%**. The document must update to 6.3%.

### Subsequent 2025 quarterly data
GSS has published through Q3 2025: **Q2 2025: 6.5% (revised)**, **Q3 2025: 5.5%**. Q4 2025 is not yet released (expected late March 2026). The Government Statistician indicated Q4 may slow to ~4.2% based on MIEG data, implying full-year 2025 of approximately **5.5–5.7%** — far above all institutional forecasts. Non-oil GDP grew by 7.5% during Q1–Q3 2025, compared with 5.8% in the corresponding period of 2024 (BoG 128th MPC Press Release, January 2026).

## 2. Inflation has fallen below the central bank's own target floor

### Headline inflation readings
**All three figures confirmed ✅.** February 2026: **3.3%** (GSS CPI Release, March 4, 2026). January 2026: **3.8%** (GSS CPI Release, February 5, 2026). December 2025: **5.4%** (GSS CPI Release, January 7, 2026).

### Monthly disinflation trajectory through 2025
Inflation fell from **23.8%** in December 2024 to **5.4%** in December 2025. Key monthly readings (year-on-year, GSS monthly CPI releases): January 23.5% (GSS, February 12, 2025) → February 23.1% → May 18.4% → July 12.1% → August 11.5% → September 9.4% → October 8.0% → November 6.3% → December 5.4% (GSS, January 7, 2026). Food inflation has been the primary driver, plunging from 28.3% in January 2025 to just **2.4%** in February 2026 (GSS CPI Release, March 4, 2026) — now below non-food inflation (4.0%).

### Consecutive months of decline
As of the February 2026 GSS release, the count is **14 consecutive months** of year-on-year decline. The decline began in January 2025. No March 2026 CPI data is yet available.

### Bank of Ghana monetary policy rate
**Current rate: 15.50%** (effective January 28, 2026), cut by 250 basis points from 18.0% at the 128th MPC meeting (BoG MPC Press Release, January 28, 2026). The BoG has cut rates aggressively — **1,250 basis points cumulative** from peak to current (from 28% to 15.5%).

**Corrected rate sequence:** Governor Asiama hiked the rate from 27% to 28% in **March 2025**; the rate was **held at 28%** in May 2025. The cutting cycle then began: **July 2025: 28% → 25%** (300bps cut, 125th MPC meeting — BoG Governor press briefing, July 30, 2025; confirmed by Ghana Business News, CNBC Africa, Citi Newsroom), **September 2025: 25% → 21.5%** (350bps cut), **November 2025: 21.5% → 18%** (350bps cut), **January 2026: 18% → 15.5%** (250bps cut, 128th MPC meeting). The July 2025 cut was the largest single rate cut in BoG history.

**Note:** The 129th MPC meeting is scheduled for **March 16–18, 2026** (BoG MPC Press Release, January 2026). The decision will be announced on March 18. Given February 2026 inflation at 3.3% — well below the 6–10% target band — the direction of the next rate decision is closely watched, though the Iran conflict and surging global oil prices complicate the picture (Business & Financial Times, March 2026).

### Inflation target band
**8% ± 2% (i.e., 6%–10%).** At 3.3%, inflation is now **below the lower bound** of the target range — a historically unusual position that triggered the January 2026 rate cut.

## 3. The cedi's remarkable 40% appreciation and reserves surge

### Exchange rate verification
**Current rate March 2026: ~GHS 10.8/USD — ✅ CONFIRMED** (Trading Economics: 10.82; Xe.com: 10.86; exchange-rates.org: 10.78 as of March 9–12, 2026). **November 2024 peak: GHS 16.0 — ✅ CONFIRMED** (TheGlobalEconomy.com: monthly max 16.04; ModernGhana: 15.99). **December 31, 2025: GHS 10.45 — ✅ CONFIRMED** (GhanaWeb interbank rate on December 31). The cedi appreciated approximately **40.7% against the USD in 2025** (BoG 125th MPC Press Release; MoF), making it one of the world's best-performing currencies.

**⚠️ Sustainability note:** A 40.7% appreciation in a single year is historically extraordinary for any currency, let alone one emerging from a debt crisis. The cedi rally is heavily supported by record gold export receipts channeled through the GoldBod program. The exchange rate moved from ~GHS 10.47/USD at the start of January 2026 to ~GHS 10.81 by March 10, representing a modest 3.2% depreciation year-to-date in 2026 (African Markets, March 11, 2026). Monitoring of the sustainability of this appreciation is essential.

### Cedi trajectory through 2025
After depreciating to ~GHS 15.5/USD in early April 2025, the cedi surged to ~GHS 10.28 by June, weakened slightly to ~GHS 12.6 in October, then strengthened again to close 2025 at GHS 10.45. IC Group research (September 2025) estimated a fair value of ~GHS 12.2/USD, noting a dual FX market with interbank at 10.5/USD and retail near 12.0/USD.

### GoldBod program
**⚠️ The document's claim of "$5 billion in H1 2025" requires clarification.** The Lands and Natural Resources Minister stated that Ghana earned **$5.2 billion from total gold exports** in January–April 2025 — but GoldBod itself was only established in March 2025 (under the Ghana Gold Board Act 2025, Act 1140). The $5.2 billion reflects total sector gold exports, not exclusively GoldBod-generated foreign exchange. The Finance Minister cited **$10 billion** in GoldBod-related FX generation for full-year 2025 (Parliament, February 25, 2026). The IMF's 5th ECF Review Staff Report flagged **$214 million in trading losses** from ASM doré gold transactions through Q3 2025. In the context of claimed $10 billion in FX generation, this represents a 2.1% loss ratio — the materiality of which depends on the risk profile of the operations, but the IMF's explicit transparency concerns warrant close monitoring. The document should distinguish between total gold export earnings and GoldBod-specific FX generation.

### Gross international reserves
**End-2024: US$8.98 billion — ✅ CONFIRMED** (World Bank/CEIC; BoG; 4.0 months of import cover). **End-2025: US$13.83 billion** (BoG Governor, January 7, 2026; Finance Minister, February 25, 2026), providing **5.7 months of import cover** — exceeding the IMF's end-2025 benchmark of $8.37 billion by **$5.4 billion**. The BoG liquidated approximately 18.5 tonnes of gold to support the local currency (BoG 128th MPC Press Release, January 2026).

**⚠️ Usable vs. gross reserves:** The BoG conducted approximately $10 billion in total FX interventions during 2025 (Finance Minister, Parliament, February 2026). Usable reserves — excluding encumbered assets, petroleum fund holdings, and pledged collateral — are substantially lower than the gross figure. The IMF 5th Review Staff Report assesses net usable reserves, but the specific figure could not be independently verified from publicly available sources. For any investment decision, the gross reserves figure alone overstates the available buffer. This gap should be addressed using the full IMF 5th Review Staff Report data.

## 5. Public debt figures depend heavily on definition used

### Debt-to-GDP ratio 2024
**⚠️ MAJOR CONFLICT — Document claims 70.5%.** The Ministry of Finance Annual Public Debt Report 2024 (presented to Parliament March 28, 2025) records **61.8%** (central government + government-guaranteed, provisional). S&P uses a "net of liquid assets" measure of **71.4%**. The IMF WEO general government gross debt measure is **~82.9%**. KPMG Ghana cited **70.6%** as of June 2024 (before Eurobond restructuring). The document's 70.5% likely reflects either the S&P methodology or mid-year pre-restructuring data. **The document must specify which definition it uses.** The official MoF year-end figure is 61.8%.

### 2025 actual debt-to-GDP
The Finance Minister reported in Parliament (February 25, 2026) that public debt fell to **GH¢641 billion = 45.3% of GDP** at end-December 2025. BoG 128th MPC data (January 2026) shows 45.5% as of end-November 2025, declining from 63.1% a year earlier.

**⚠️ ESSENTIAL CONTEXT — Currency translation effect:** A significant portion of this decline is mechanical rather than structural. The cedi's 40.7% appreciation compressed the local-currency value of dollar-denominated external debt. In dollar terms, public debt actually rose — BoG data indicate an increase from approximately $49.5 billion to $59.4 billion between early and mid-2025. If the cedi reverses even partially, the debt-to-GDP ratio re-inflates. The 45.3% figure is real and confirmed, but presenting it as "collapsing" without noting the currency translation mechanism overstates the degree of structural fiscal improvement.

### Debt trajectory projections
**S&P projects 47.4% by 2028 — ✅ CONFIRMED** (S&P May 2025 upgrade report). Given that the actual 2025 outturn (45.3%) already undercut the 2028 projection, further downward revisions are likely — provided the cedi does not depreciate materially.

### Eurobond restructuring
**All figures confirmed ✅.** Total exchanged: **$13,103.9 million** ($13.1 billion) — MoF APDR 2024 and press conference October 4, 2024. Participation rate: **~98.6%** — MoF results announcement October 3, 2024. Nominal reduction: **37%** (~$4.87 billion upfront plus past-due interest) — MoF. Average coupon reduced from ~8% to ~4%. Exchange completed October 9–10, 2024. Post-restructuring Eurobond stock: $9,236.1 million. Ghana met all 2025 Eurobond coupon payments ($1.4 billion across three tranches), including a $709 million payment on December 30 — ahead of schedule.

### Bilateral debt restructuring
MoU signed January 28, 2025. Bilateral debt quantum: $5,173.9 million (at end-2022). **Bilateral agreements signed with 7 countries**: Czech Republic, UK, Spain, France, China, Finland, Germany. The OCC was co-chaired by France and China. Debt service relief: ~$2,845 million during ECF period.

### Fiscal deficit 2024
**5.2% of GDP on cash basis — ✅ CONFIRMED** (MoF APDR 2024). On commitment basis, the deficit was higher at ~7.9%. The 2025 full-year outturn was extraordinary: overall deficit fell to just **1.0% of GDP** (commitment basis) versus a 2.8% revised target. Primary surplus reached **2.6% of GDP** (commitment) versus a 1.5% target. BoG 128th MPC data reports primary balance on commitment basis at a surplus of 2.8% of GDP as of late 2025.

### Interest expenditure as % of revenue
Post-restructuring, this ratio fell to approximately **25–28%** (S&P: ~25% in 2024; EGP Tracker: 25.1% as of June 2025), down dramatically from ~47–48% in 2021–22. Government revenue as % of GDP 2024: approximately **15.3–15.9%** (MoF). Tax-to-GDP ratio: **13–14.5%** by international standards (OECD Revenue Statistics Africa 2025: 14.5% for 2023).

## 6. Current account surplus and record trade performance

**Current account 2024 surplus of 1.8% of GDP — ✅ CONFIRMED** (Bank of Ghana, January 2026 Summary of Economic and Financial Data). Some sources report higher figures (Trading Economics: 4.4%; World Bank: 2.0%) due to different BOP methodologies. The BoG's 1.8% is the directly attributable official figure.

**2025 current account:** The actual outturn was a **US$9.1 billion surplus** (Finance Minister, Parliament, February 2026; confirmed in BoG 128th MPC Press Release) — massively exceeding S&P's May 2025 projection of 4.6% of GDP. Balance of payments surplus was $3.98 billion (provisional, BoG 128th MPC). This was driven by record gold exports, increased remittances, and moderation in services and income payments.

**⚠️ GOLD CONCENTRATION:** The $9.1 billion current account surplus is overwhelmingly driven by gold exports, which approximately doubled to ~$20 billion in 2025. Stripping out the gold surge, the current account picture would be materially different. This concentration risk means the surplus figure should not be extrapolated forward without explicit gold price and production volume assumptions.

**Trade data 2024:** Total goods exports approximately **US$19.1 billion** (BoG). Total goods imports approximately **$15.3 billion** (oil imports $4.6 billion; non-oil $10.7 billion). Trade surplus approximately **$3.8 billion**. Top export commodities: gold **$10.3 billion** (55.3% of exports), crude oil **$3.8 billion** (17.8%), cocoa **$1.9 billion** (8.4–9.7%).

**2025 commodity performance:** Gold exports doubled to approximately **$20 billion** (driven by record production of ~4.8 million ounces and surging prices). Cocoa exports doubled to ~$3.8 billion. Oil exports declined to ~$2.6 billion.

## 12. Gold concentration risk assessment

**This section was absent from the original document and is required for analytical completeness.**

Ghana's 2025 macroeconomic outperformance is structurally dependent on gold to a degree that creates material concentration risk across multiple indicators:

**Export dependence:** Gold exports approximately doubled from ~$10.3 billion in 2024 to ~$20 billion in 2025, representing roughly 55–60% of total goods exports. This makes Ghana's external accounts more commodity-concentrated than at any point in recent history.

**Current account sensitivity:** The $9.1 billion current account surplus is overwhelmingly gold-driven. A 20% decline in gold prices (from ~$2,400/oz to ~$1,920/oz) would reduce gold export revenue by approximately $4 billion, likely eliminating or severely compressing the current account surplus.

**Reserves linkage:** The BoG's reserve accumulation strategy explicitly relies on gold. The 128th MPC Press Release disclosed that the BoG liquidated approximately 18.5 tonnes of gold. Sustained gold prices are essential to maintaining the reserves buffer.

**Cedi stability linkage:** The cedi's 40.7% appreciation was supported by gold-driven FX inflows. A reversal in gold prices would reduce FX supply and could trigger cedi depreciation, which would in turn re-inflate the debt-to-GDP ratio (see Section 5).

**GoldBod operational transparency:** The IMF 5th Review flagged $214 million in trading losses from ASM doré gold transactions through Q3 2025. The IMF raised explicit concerns about operational transparency. These losses, in the context of claimed $10 billion in FX generation, represent a 2.1% loss ratio. Whether this is within acceptable risk parameters depends on information about the trading operations that is not fully public.

**What this means for analysis:** Any forward-looking use of the macroeconomic data in this document must incorporate explicit assumptions about gold prices and production volumes. The 2025 data cannot be linearly extrapolated without addressing this concentration.
