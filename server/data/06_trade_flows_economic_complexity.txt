## Ghana's Trade Profile — Aggregate Data

### Total Trade (2024)
- **Total exports (goods):** Approximately **US$19.1 billion** (Bank of Ghana); GH₵294.9 billion (GSS)
- **Total imports (goods):** Approximately **US$15.3 billion** (Bank of Ghana); oil imports $4.6 billion, non-oil $10.7 billion. GSS reports GH₵250.2 billion.
- **Merchandise trade surplus:** Approximately **US$3.8 billion** (BoG), or GH₵44.7 billion (GSS)
- **Total trade (goods + services):** Goods trade as % of GDP approximately **38.7%** (2023, World Bank). Services exports ~$8.7 billion; services imports ~$11.2 billion (WITS, 2023 data).
- **Current account surplus (2024):** **1.8% of GDP** (Bank of Ghana, January 2026)
- **Current account surplus (2025):** **US$9.1 billion** (Finance Minister, Parliament, February 2026) — massively exceeding all projections, driven by record gold exports.

### Trade Balance Trajectory
Ghana historically runs merchandise trade deficits, but this reversed dramatically in 2023–2025:
- 2022: Trade deficit (pre-restructuring, high imports)
- 2023: Shift toward surplus as gold and cocoa export values rose
- 2024: Trade surplus of ~$3.8 billion (BoG)
- 2025: Record surplus driven by gold exports doubling to ~$20 billion

### Trade Openness
- Trade (goods + services) as % of GDP: ~**38.7%** (2023) — a decline from previous years reflecting both cedi depreciation effects and shifting trade composition.
- Ghana is more open than many Sub-Saharan African peers but less trade-dependent than Asian emerging markets.

## Ghana's Top Exports (2024)

Ghana's economy remains heavily concentrated on primary commodity exports, with just three product categories (gold, oil, cocoa) accounting for the vast majority of export revenue.

### Top Export Products (2024, Bank of Ghana / OEC / GSS)
1. **Gold** — **$10.3 billion** (55.3% of total goods exports). Africa's #1 gold producer. Production reached a historic high of **4.8 million ounces** in 2024 (19.3% growth). Artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) output surged 70%.
2. **Crude Oil** — **$3.8 billion** (17.8%). Jubilee, TEN, Sankofa fields. Production ~132,000 bpd average in 2024, rising to ~180,000 bpd by October 2025.
3. **Cocoa (beans + processed products)** — **$1.9–3.37 billion** depending on timeframe (BoG 2024 calendar year: ~$1.9B at 8.4–9.7% of exports; USDA MY 2024/25 exports including processed: $3.37B).
4. **Oil Products** — ~7.5% of H1 2024 exports (OEC/GTAIC)
5. **Manganese ore** — significant (Ghana is a major global producer)
6. **Aluminum** — VALCO smelter output
7. **Shea butter/nuts** — growing cosmetics and food-grade export market
8. **Rubber**
9. **Cassava products**
10. **Timber and wood products**

### 2025 Commodity Export Performance
- **Gold exports doubled** to approximately **$20 billion** in 2025, driven by record production (~4.8M oz), surging global gold prices, and GoldBod program FX generation. The Finance Minister cited $10 billion in GoldBod-related FX generation for full-year 2025.
- **Cocoa exports doubled** to approximately **$3.8 billion** in 2025, driven by the global cocoa price spike.
- **Oil exports declined** to approximately **$2.6 billion** in 2025 due to production issues (oil/gas contracted −22.5% in Q2 and −18.2% in Q3 2025 by GSS measure).

### Export Concentration Risk
Ghana's export basket is among the most concentrated in West Africa. Key metrics:
- **Top 3 products (gold, oil, cocoa)** accounted for approximately **81.5% of total goods exports** in 2024 (BoG data).
- Gems and precious metals alone: ~36.2% of export value (GTAIC/Seair analysis).
- Mineral fuels: ~26.2%; Cocoa: ~18.1%.
- This concentration creates vulnerability to commodity price shocks, Dutch disease dynamics, and limited value-capture from global supply chains.

### Top Export Destinations (2024, GSS/BoG)
1. **Switzerland** — gold and cocoa
2. **United Arab Emirates** — gold
3. **South Africa** — gold, oil, rubber (60.5% of Ghana's exports within Africa go to South Africa)
4. **China** — oil, manganese, aluminum
5. **India** — gold
6. **Canada** — oil and cocoa (received 58.6% of Ghana's exports to North America per GSS)
7. **Netherlands** — cocoa, shea
8. **Burkina Faso** — manufactured goods (12.4% of intra-African exports)
9. **Brazil** — oil and cocoa
10. **United States** — crude oil, cocoa, apparel (~$1.2 billion total exports)

### Cocoa Export Detail (MY 2024/25, USDA FAS)
- Cocoa bean exports projected at **520,000 MT** (up 55% YoY)
- Total cocoa exports (beans + processed) surpassed **$3.37 billion** — up 119% from MY 2023/24's $1.54 billion
- Cocoa exports to US: **$581.5M** (Jan–Jul 2025), up 318% YoY — a record
- Domestic grinding capacity: **504,780 MT** — operating at **<50%**
- 2024/25 production estimate: **650,000–700,000 MT**, a ~32% increase over the prior season but still well below the 2021/22 peak of 1.04 million MT
- **Value addition opportunity:** Significant room to increase processed cocoa exports vs. raw bean exports. Chocolate manufacturing for domestic and regional markets barely exists — virtually all chocolate consumed in Ghana is imported.

## Import Substitution Opportunities

**Import gaps are opportunity signals.** The following categories represent products Ghana imports heavily that could feasibly be produced or processed domestically:

| Product Category | Import Value (est.) | Domestic Potential | Feasibility | Government Policy Support |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rice** | ~$280–560M/year | High — arable land available, irrigation expansion underway (Afram Plains) | Medium-term (5–10 years to scale) | Planting for Food & Jobs; irrigation investments |
| **Poultry** | ~$300–400M/year | High — local demand massive, feed supply chain needs development | Medium-term | 1D1F incentives |
| **Pharmaceuticals** | ~$370M+ (~70% imported) | High — government priority; some local manufacturers exist but at small scale | Medium-term | National Pharmaceutical Policy; FDA incentives |
| **Processed food** | Several hundred $M | High — raw materials abundant (cassava, plantain, cocoa, shea, fruits) | Near-term | 1D1F; Planting for Food & Jobs |
| **Fertilizer** | Significant | Medium — building on Moroccan phosphate processing; limited domestic raw materials | Medium-term | |
| **Construction materials** | Significant | Medium — cement production exists (Dangote investing); steel capacity growing (B5 Plus) | Near-term for some products | 24-hour economy initiative |
| **Packaged water/beverages** | Large domestic market | High — local production active but quality standards variable | Already active | |
| **Textiles/garments** | Significant | Low-Medium — sector severely contracted; revival requires major investment | Long-term | AGOA preferences (now extended 1 year) |
| **Vehicles/assembly** | Very large | Medium — VW, Toyota, Nissan, Suzuki assembly announced or planned; Kantanka local manufacturer | Medium-term | Automotive Industry Policy |

**Government signals:** 1D1F (One District One Factory) program, 24-hour economy initiative, GIPC investment push, and Made-in-Ghana Procurement Policy (2024–2025) all prioritize import substitution in manufacturing and agro-processing. The Ministry of Finance and PPA are developing a "buy national" approach through a forthcoming Made-in-Ghana Procurement List requiring presidential approval before importing goods available locally.

### AGOA Status (Critical — February 2026 Update)
The African Growth and Opportunity Act has been central to the U.S.–Ghana trade relationship:

- **AGOA expiration:** The original Act expired **September 30, 2025**.
- **One-year extension:** Signed by the Trump administration on **February 3, 2026**, providing temporary continuation of duty-free, quota-free market access for eligible African countries including Ghana.
- **Pre-AGOA value:** AGOA provided duty-free access for Ghanaian exports valued at approximately **$340 million** in 2024, including textiles, cocoa derivatives, and manufactured goods. Ghana's total utilized AGOA exports excluding oil were approximately $86 million in 2022.
- **Post-expiration tariff impact:** Before the one-year extension, Ghanaian exporters faced tariffs estimated at up to **15%** on goods that previously entered U.S. markets duty-free. The Importers and Exporters Association of Ghana (IEAG) reported a **45% decline** in AGOA-related exports during early 2025 compared to 2024.
- **U.S. tariff regime:** The Trump administration imposed a **blanket 10% tariff** on imports from several countries including Ghana (effective April 5, 2025), followed by an **additional 15% tariff** on Ghanaian exports on August 7, 2025.
- **Uncertainty persists:** The U.S. House Ways and Means Committee approved a 3-year AGOA Extension Act in December 2025, but the Trump administration indicated support for only a one-year extension. The one-year extension signed in February 2026 provides temporary relief but leaves long-term uncertainty.
- **Ghana's response:** Ghana is negotiating a comprehensive trade cooperation framework with the U.S. to move beyond AGOA dependence. Trade Minister Elizabeth Ofosu-Adjare has emphasized the Accelerated Export Development Programme to diversify exports to the U.S.

## Trade Agreements & Preferential Market Access

Ghana has a uniquely favorable network of trade agreements that allows a Ghana-based manufacturer to access multiple large markets with preferential or zero-tariff treatment. This is a central pillar of Ghana's investment value proposition.

### EU–Ghana Interim Economic Partnership Agreement (iEPA)
- **Signed:** October 2016. **Effective implementation by Ghana:** July 1, 2021.
- **EU market access:** Duty-free, quota-free access for **all** Ghanaian exports (agricultural and manufactured) to the EU market.
- **Ghana's market opening:** Ghana will eliminate tariffs on EU products covering **78% of tariff lines** by 2029. Of these, 22% were immediately eliminated in 2021; another 50% reached zero by end-2024; the remaining 6% reach zero by 2029. **22% of tariff lines are excluded** (including cotton, textiles, olive oil, meat, crustaceans, milk, coffee, cocoa, tobacco, fruits, cement, beauty products, rubber, automobiles).
- **EU–Ghana trade growth:** The EU's total trade with Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana increased by **73% since 2016** to reach **€19 billion in 2024** (EU DG Trade).
- **West Africa context:** The EU is West Africa's biggest trading partner, with total trade of **€64 billion in 2024**. The EU is the main export market for West African transformed products (fisheries, agribusiness, textiles).
- **182 Free Zone companies** export to the EU market under the EPA framework.

### UK–Ghana Interim Trade Partnership Agreement
- **Signed:** March 2021. **Effective:** March 5, 2021.
- Replicates the EU–Ghana EPA tariff treatment, giving UK exports the same reduced tariff or duty-free treatment as EU products.
- Post-Brexit continuity agreement.

### ECOWAS Common External Tariff (CET)
- Ghana applies the ECOWAS CET to imports from non-preferential trading partners (including the U.S.):
  - **0%** on essential social goods
  - **5%** on essential commodities, raw materials, and capital goods
  - **10%** on intermediate goods and parts
  - **20%** on final consumer goods
  - **35%** on specific goods (protection band)
- **ECOWAS Trade Liberalization Scheme (ETLS):** Qualifying goods of community origin can move duty-free across ECOWAS member states.
- **ECOWAS market:** ~**400 million people** across 15 countries (though 3 member states — Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger — announced exit from ECOWAS in 2023–2024, creating significant uncertainty).

### Ghana–China Trade Negotiations
- As of 2025–2026, Ghana and China are negotiating a **zero-tariff trade agreement**. Under this proposed deal, Ghanaian exports would gain tariff-free access to the Chinese market. The arrangement is still in negotiation; no legal text published or ratified.

### WTO Membership
- Ghana is a founding WTO member. All 16 West African countries are WTO members.
- Ghana applies MFN (Most Favored Nation) weighted average tariff of **13.43%** and effectively applied weighted average tariff of **13%** (WITS).
- The WTO Agreement on Fisheries Subsidies (effective September 2025) creates new compliance obligations for Ghana.

### Generalized System of Preferences (GSP)
- Ghana benefits from GSP preferences offered by several developed countries, providing reduced-tariff or duty-free access for qualifying products.
- With the EU EPA and AGOA, GSP is less critical but remains a fallback for markets where specific bilateral agreements don't exist.

### Market Access Summary — From a Ghana Manufacturing Base

| Destination Market | Population | Access Terms | Agreement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **European Union** | 450M | Duty-free, quota-free (all products) | EU–Ghana iEPA |
| **United Kingdom** | 67M | Duty-free, quota-free (replicates EPA) | UK–Ghana ITPA |
| **ECOWAS** | ~400M | Duty-free (qualifying community origin goods) | ECOWAS ETLS |
| **AfCFTA** | 1.4 billion | 90% of tariff lines liberalized (implementation ongoing) | AfCFTA |
| **United States** | 340M | AGOA preferences (1-year extension, uncertain future) | AGOA + 10% baseline tariff |
| **China** | 1.4 billion | Under negotiation (zero-tariff proposed) | Pending |

**This is Ghana's gateway proposition:** A U.S. company manufacturing in Ghana can potentially access the EU (450M), UK (67M), ECOWAS (400M), and AfCFTA (1.4B) markets at preferential rates — while a U.S. company exporting directly from the United States faces ECOWAS CET rates and no special access to the EU or broader Africa.

## AfCFTA — The Continental Free Trade Area

### Overview
The African Continental Free Trade Area is the world's largest free trade area by number of participating countries, covering **54 of 55 AU members** (all except Eritrea), a population of **1.4 billion**, and combined GDP exceeding **$3.4 trillion**.

### Implementation Status (March 2026)
- **Ratification:** **49 countries** have ratified (up from 48 in 2024). 54 have signed.
- **Tariff concessions:** 37+ member states have submitted tariff schedules. Countries aim to eliminate tariffs on **90% of goods** over 5–10 years, with 7% "sensitive" products liberalized over 10–13 years, and 3% excluded.
- **Guided Trade Initiative (GTI):** Launched October 2022 as a pilot to test AfCFTA operational, institutional, and legal frameworks. **39 states participated.** The GTI was **formally concluded in May 2025** at the 16th Council of Ministers Meeting, affirming that AfCFTA legal instruments for trade in goods are operational.
- **e-Tariff Book:** Online tool providing businesses with tariff and rules of origin information — operational.
- **AfCFTA Adjustment Fund:** Established to promote commercially viable investments that enable trade and diversification; made its first significant investment.
- **Phase II Protocols adopted (2023–2024):** Investment, Intellectual Property Rights, Competition Policy, Digital Trade, and Women and Youth in Trade. These expand AfCFTA's scope but require member ratification.
- **Outstanding issues:** Rules of origin for textiles/clothing and autos still being finalized. Non-tariff barriers remain significant.

### Ghana's Strategic Position as AfCFTA Host
Ghana hosts the **AfCFTA Secretariat** in Accra, providing institutional advantages:
- Influence over shaping rules of origin, services trade protocols, and investment frameworks
- Early access to implementation intelligence and compliance requirements
- Positioning as the "trade capital" of Africa, attracting trade-adjacent investment (legal services, logistics, trade finance, consulting)
- Symbolic and practical significance for investor confidence

### Intra-African Trade Data (2024–2025)
- Intra-African trade reached approximately **$220 billion** in 2025, representing roughly **15–18%** of Africa's total trade — compared to 59% in Asia and 68% in Europe.
- Intra-African trade experienced a **12.4% rebound in 2024** following a contraction in 2023.
- The ECOWAS sub-region remains at just **11.5%** intra-regional trade, significantly below the continental average.
- AfCFTA-linked trade added an estimated **0.5 percentage points** to Africa's GDP growth in 2022.

### Projected Impact (UNECA / World Bank)
- Intra-African exports could increase by **52% by 2030** (UNECA) and by **45% by 2045** (World Bank).
- GDP increase: Cumulative gain of **$450 billion by 2035** (UNECA).
- Employment: **17.9 million new jobs** and income growth reaching $450 billion by 2035.
- Poverty reduction: Up to **50 million people** lifted out of extreme poverty.
- Real income increase: **7% Africa-wide** by 2035 (World Bank).
- FDI inflows into AfCFTA member states rose by **17% between 2021 and 2023**.

### Customs Clearance Improvements
- Customs clearance times along key corridors (e.g., Tema–Abidjan) have dropped from **12 hours to 9.5 hours**.
- More than half of 220 non-tariff barrier complaints logged through AfCFTA's platform were resolved, with average resolution time of **39 days**.
- Logistics costs fell in select corridors: **−9% for road freight, −5% for maritime**.

### AfCFTA Risks and Challenges
- Slow ratification of Phase II protocols
- Non-tariff barriers persist: administrative delays, complex customs procedures, regulatory inconsistencies
- Weak transport, energy, and logistics infrastructure increases trade costs
- Uneven implementation: some countries progressing rapidly while others lag
- Limited private sector awareness and use of AfCFTA benefits
- Average customs dwell time across Africa: **126 hours** (pre-AfCFTA baseline) — still high in many corridors

## Pan-African Payment and Settlement System (PAPSS)

### Overview
PAPSS is a centralized real-time gross settlement infrastructure enabling instant cross-border payments in local African currencies. It was developed by Afreximbank in collaboration with the African Union and AfCFTA Secretariat.

### Current Scale (March 2026)
- **160+ commercial banks** connected across **19 countries** in 4 regions (Afreximbank, February 2026)
- **14 payment switches** connected
- **15 central banks** signed on
- Live commercial banks in **12 countries** with 4 more (Tunisia, Comoros, Uganda, Egypt) in onboarding
- Pilot savings estimated at **$5–8 million** in foreign exchange conversion fees in two years

### Products (2025 launches)
1. **PAPSS Instant Payment System (IPS)** — real-time cross-border payments in local currencies
2. **PAPSSCARD** — Africa's first continental card scheme, launched June 2025, designed to challenge dominance of foreign card networks and keep processing, fees, and data within Africa
3. **PAPSS African Currency Marketplace (PACM)** — launched July 2025 for direct peer-to-peer exchange of African currencies. During pilot phase, 80+ African corporates transacted across 12 currency pairs, all settled in local currencies.

### Ghana-Specific Implementation
- Ghana is an early PAPSS adopter (part of the original WAMZ pilot: Nigeria, Ghana, Gambia, Sierra Leone, Liberia, Guinea)
- In Ghana, banks and non-bank financial institutions — including savings and loans companies, fintechs, and mobile money platforms — can access PAPSS through the **Ghana Interbank Payment and Settlement Systems Limited (GhIPSS)** platform
- **BrijX** — a B2B currency swap platform allowing direct Cedi–Naira exchanges — launched as a Bank of Ghana regulatory sandbox pilot in February 2025

### Strategic Significance
- Aspires to save businesses across Africa **$5 billion annually** in transaction costs
- Eliminates need for dollar or euro intermediation for intra-African payments
- Supports AfCFTA implementation by reducing the cost of cross-border commerce
- MoU signed with **BUNA** (Arab Monetary Fund's cross-border payment system) for eventual interoperability
- Average cost of sending $200 across African borders: **7–8%** (World Bank 2023), well above the global average of 6–7% and the SDG target of 3%. PAPSS aims to dramatically reduce this.

## Regional Trade Corridors

### Ghana–Nigeria Corridor
- **Nigeria (220M population)** is the single largest market opportunity for Ghana-based manufacturers under AfCFTA
- Nigeria is Ghana's third-largest FDI source by project count (2025)
- Significant Nigerian business community in Ghana
- GUTA-NUTAG tensions over trading rights reflect competitive dynamics between Ghanaian and Nigerian traders
- Cross-border mobile money payments growing: MTN MoMo Nigeria-Ghana link; BrijX Cedi-Naira swap platform
- **Nigeria commenced AfCFTA Guided Trade Initiative** on July 16, 2024 at Lagos Port Complex Apapa — initial exports to 5 African countries
- Abidjan–Lagos corridor described as "the backbone of the regional economy" but remains incomplete

### Ghana–Côte d'Ivoire Corridor
- Both are top cocoa producers; compete and collaborate on cocoa pricing
- **ARS-1000 standard** jointly adopted October 2024 for cocoa pricing
- Trade in manufactured goods, agricultural products. Significant border trade.
- Compete for FDI in manufacturing and processing
- EU total trade with Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire combined: **€19 billion in 2024** (up 73% since 2016)

### Ghana–Burkina Faso / Landlocked States
- Ghana's ports (Tema, Takoradi) serve as critical transit points for Burkina Faso, Niger, Mali
- **Accra–Kumasi–Tamale–Ouagadougou corridor** is a strategic trade route
- Transit trade is significant volume for port operations and logistics services
- Burkina Faso accounts for **12.4% of Ghana's intra-African exports** (2024, GSS)
- **ECOWAS exit risk:** Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger announced departure from ECOWAS. If formalized, this could disrupt transit trade arrangements, though practical trade flows are likely to continue given economic interdependence.

### Ghana as Regional Hub
- **Port access:** Tema and Takoradi ports serve as gateways for landlocked Sahelian states
- **Air connectivity:** Kotoka International Airport with direct U.S. flights (United Airlines Accra–Washington Dulles)
- **Political stability anchor:** Ghana's democratic stability stands out against regional coups (Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, Guinea since 2020)
- **AfCFTA Secretariat host:** Institutional prestige and access
- **English-speaking:** Facilitates business with global markets (alongside Francophone neighbors)

## Export Processing & Value-Addition Opportunities

### Cocoa Value Chain
- Ghana exports majority of cocoa as raw beans despite installed grinding capacity of **504,780 MT** operating at **<50%**
- **Value-addition gap:** Cocoa paste, butter, and powder command significantly higher prices than raw beans
- Chocolate manufacturing for domestic and regional markets barely exists — virtually all chocolate consumed in Ghana is imported
- MY 2024/25 cocoa exports hit $3.37B — demonstrates the revenue scale of even partially-processed exports
- COCOBOD implementing anti-smuggling reward scheme (1/3 of confiscated cocoa value) to combat illicit cross-border cocoa trade

### Gold Beneficiation
- GoldBod program generated ~$10 billion in FX for full-year 2025 (Finance Minister; note: IMF flagged $214 million in trading losses through Q3 2025)
- Government exploring gold refinery development — currently most gold exported as doré bars for refining abroad
- Jewelry manufacturing for export is nascent
- **Africa's #1 gold producer** with 4.8 million ounces in 2024

### Shea Butter Processing
- Ghana is a major shea nut producer. Bunge processes shea in-country.
- High-value cosmetic/food-grade shea butter processing for export is an opportunity
- Global shea butter market growing driven by clean beauty and plant-based food trends

### Cashew & Other Agricultural Products
- Cashew processing capacity growing but most nuts still exported raw
- Fruits (mangoes, pineapples) — processing for juice, dried fruit in demand but cold chain limitations constrain quality
- **30–40% post-harvest losses** across agriculture sector reflect infrastructure and skills gaps

### Oil & Gas Processing
- **Tema Oil Refinery** — limited processing capacity
- Opportunity for petrochemicals, fertilizer from gas, and refined product exports
- LPG and natural gas for domestic industrial use still underdeveloped

### Free Zone Enterprise Benefits

- **Unrestricted profit repatriation**
- Duty-free imports of raw materials, machinery, equipment

## Economic Complexity

### Economic Complexity Index (ECI) — Ghana's Position
Ghana's ECI ranking is **low and declining**, reflecting heavy dependence on primary commodities (gold, cocoa, oil) with limited product diversification:

- **ECI ranking (2022, latest available):** Approximately **121st** globally (Harvard Growth Lab Atlas of Economic Complexity / OEC — data typically lags 2–3 years due to trade statistics compilation), placing Ghana among the lowest-ranked economies worldwide.
- **Trend:** Declining. Harvard Growth Lab identified Ghana as one of the countries showing "the fastest declines in the complexity rankings in the past decade," alongside Panama, Cuba, and Brazil — all having "failed to diversify their exports."
- **ECI score:** Negative (indicating exports are dominated by ubiquitous products of low sophistication)
- **African comparisons:** South Africa, Morocco, Tunisia, and Kenya all rank significantly higher. Even within West Africa, Senegal and Côte d'Ivoire have historically ranked higher in complexity.

### What ECI Means for Ghana
The Economic Complexity Index measures the diversity and sophistication of a country's productive capabilities as revealed by its export basket. Ghana's low ranking reflects several structural features:

1. **Extreme export concentration:** Top 3 products (gold, cocoa, oil) = ~81.5% of exports. These are all primary commodities with low product complexity scores.
2. **Low product diversification:** Ghana exports relatively few distinct products compared to its income level.
3. **High ubiquity of exports:** The products Ghana exports (gold, cocoa beans, crude oil) are also exported by many other countries — they don't signal unique productive capabilities.
4. **Limited manufacturing base:** Manufacturing grew at only ~3.1% in 2024 (GSS). The manufacturing sector contributes a small share of exports.

### ECI vs. Income — The Growth Prediction
The Harvard Growth Lab's central finding is that countries whose economic complexity exceeds what would be expected for their income level grow faster in subsequent decades. Ghana's situation is the inverse — the economy is "growing without significant structural transformation" (GTAIC analysis):

- Ghana's income level has risen (GDP per capita ~$2,407 in 2024), but its economic complexity has stagnated or declined.
- The Atlas of Economic Complexity specifically compares Ghana to Thailand: both had similar levels of schooling in 1970, but Thailand underwent a massive increase in economic complexity (one standard deviation in ECI) between 1970 and 1985, causing sustained economic boom. Ghana's complexity and income stagnated as it remained an exporter of cocoa, aluminum, fish, and forest products.
- **Implication:** Ghana's current GDP growth (5.7% in 2024) is largely commodity-driven. Without structural transformation toward more complex products, long-term growth potential is constrained.

### Complexity Outlook — Where Ghana Can Move
The Complexity Outlook Index (COI) measures how many complex products are "nearby" in a country's product space — i.e., how easily a country can diversify into new complex products given its current capabilities. For Ghana, high-potential adjacencies include:

- **Processed agricultural products:** Moving from raw cocoa to chocolate, from raw shea to cosmetics, from raw cashews to processed snacks
- **Light manufacturing:** Garments, textiles (leveraging AGOA/EPA access), packaged food products
- **Construction materials:** Cement, steel products, tiles — leveraging domestic demand from the 1.8M housing deficit
- **Petrochemicals:** Leveraging oil/gas production into downstream chemicals and fertilizer
- **Agro-industrial inputs:** Fertilizer, animal feed, agricultural chemicals

### Diversification Imperative
The gap between raw commodity export and processed/manufactured goods is Ghana's central economic development challenge. Every dollar of cocoa bean that becomes chocolate, every ounce of gold refined domestically, every kilogram of shea processed into cosmetics represents a step up the value chain — and a higher-margin opportunity for investors who bring:
- Processing capability and technology
- Quality management systems (ISO, HACCP, etc.)
- Market access and distribution networks
- Training infrastructure and skills transfer

This is precisely where the AI Mirror's **Skills Transfer Pivot** and **Formalization Pivot** frameworks become most relevant: Ghana's economy needs foreign competencies not just for direct business operations but to build the productive capabilities that increase economic complexity.

## Made-in-Ghana & Industrial Policy Signals

### Made-in-Ghana Procurement Policy (2024–2025)
- Ministry of Finance and Public Procurement Authority developing a "buy national" approach
- Forthcoming **Made-in-Ghana Procurement List** will require presidential approval before government agencies can import goods available locally

- **Geographical Indications Act, 2003 (Act 659)** protects Ghana-origin products

- Government push to extend economic activity around the clock
- Targets manufacturing, logistics, and services sectors

- Policy to establish at least one factory or enterprise in each of Ghana's 260+ districts
- Focus on agro-processing, manufacturing, and import substitution
- Tax incentives and concessionary financing available

## Data Quality Notes & Anti-Hallucination Safeguards

### Data Source Hierarchy
1. **Bank of Ghana (BoG) / GSS** — official government statistics; primary source for trade balance, export values, import values
2. **USTR / U.S. Census Bureau** — authoritative for U.S.–Ghana bilateral trade data
3. **OEC / Harvard Growth Lab** — authoritative for Economic Complexity Index and product space analysis
4. **UNCTAD / World Bank / IMF** — authoritative for aggregate trade and FDI flow data
5. **USDA FAS** — authoritative for cocoa and agricultural trade data
6. **EU DG Trade** — authoritative for EU–Ghana trade data
7. **Afreximbank / AfCFTA Secretariat** — authoritative for PAPSS and AfCFTA implementation data
8. **Industry reports and news sources** — supplementary; verified against primary sources where possible

### Known Data Conflicts
- **GSS vs. BoG trade data:** Different methodological bases can produce different aggregate trade figures. GSS data is in Ghana cedis; BoG data in U.S. dollars. Exchange rate conversion introduces discrepancies.
- **WITS data vs. BoG data:** WITS (World Bank) shows total exports of $16.87 billion and total imports of $16.39 billion — lower than BoG figures of $19.1B and $15.3B respectively. Likely reflects different data vintages and BOP vs. customs methodology.
- **Gold export figures:** The Finance Minister cited $10 billion in GoldBod-related FX generation vs. $5.2 billion in total gold exports in January–April 2025. These reflect different scopes (total sector vs. GoldBod-specific).
- **China import share:** Ranges from 23% (U.S. Commercial Service) to 50.4% (GTAIC/OEC) depending on whether re-exports and indirect trade are included.
- **Rice import values:** UN COMTRADE/WITS data shows $286M (2023); local-currency conversion methods yield higher estimates (~$560M). The commonly cited $800M figure likely conflates import volume (~800,000 MT) with dollar value. This document uses the verified $280–560M range.
- **Pharmaceutical import share:** Commonly cited at 85% in Ghanaian policy discourse, but peer-reviewed studies and the Indian High Commission market survey consistently report ~70% imported / ~30% locally manufactured. This document uses the verified ~70% figure.

### Projection Caveats
- All trade projections (AfCFTA impact, intra-African trade growth, GDP gains) are modeled estimates based on full implementation scenarios that have not yet been achieved
- AGOA status is fluid; any renewal beyond the current 1-year extension is subject to U.S. Congressional action and executive policy
- Ghana–China zero-tariff agreement is under negotiation with no confirmed timeline
- Cocoa export values are highly sensitive to global commodity prices; the 2024–2025 price spike may not be sustained
- Gold export values depend on both production volumes and global prices; the IMF has flagged concerns about GoldBod trading losses and transparency
