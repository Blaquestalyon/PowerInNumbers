## Skills & Workforce Gaps

### Overview

Ghana's labor market is characterized by a structural mismatch between a youthful, rapidly growing working-age population and the economy's capacity to absorb it into productive employment.

- **Informal sector:** Accounts for **75–89% of the workforce** (UNDP HDR 2023; GSS). The vast majority of workers operate outside formal employment structures, without contracts, social protection, or access to institutional training.
- **Total labor force:** ~**14 million** (GSS Labour Statistics Bulletin, July 2025).
- **Labor force participation rate:** Averaged **70.2%** in 2024 — peaked at 73.0% in Q3 2023 (GSS).
- **Unemployment rate (annual average, 2024):** **13.6%**, down from 14.6% in 2023 (GSS). Q4 2024 rate: 13.1%.
- **Youth unemployment (15–24):** **32%** (GSS 2024 average).
- **Broader youth unemployment (15–35):** **22.5%** (GSS).
- **NEET rate (not in employment, education, or training), ages 15–24:** **25.8%** (GSS Q4 2024).
- **Urban-rural divide:** Urban unemployment averaged **15.9%** in 2024, versus 10.4% rural — a 5.5 percentage point gap (GSS). However, rural employment is predominantly informal and subsistence-level.
- **Estimated unemployed youth population:** ~**3.4 million** (cited by President Mahama at the Adwumawura Programme launch, July 2025).
- **Employment by sector (2024, GSS):** Services ~44%, Agriculture ~39%, Industry ~17%.
- **Female employment:** Consistently exceeds male employment; gender gap widened from 632,000 in Q1 2022 to 1.12 million in Q4 2024 (GSS).
- Seven out of ten unemployed Ghanaians are under 35. The World Bank's 2025 Economic Update identifies absorbing the growing working-age population into productive employment as Ghana's **paramount economic challenge**.

**Critical ILO vs. GSS discrepancy:** ILO modeled estimates show total unemployment at just 3.0% and youth unemployment at 5.4% — dramatically lower than GSS survey-based figures. The GSS figures, derived from actual quarterly household surveys (AHIES), are more reflective of Ghana's labor market realities. All figures in this document use GSS data unless otherwise stated.

### Education-Employment Mismatch

The gap between what educational institutions produce and what the labor market demands is a defining structural challenge:

- More than **half of Ghana's workers** experience an education mismatch with their roles (Nexford University/labor market analysis).
- Only **24%** of surveyed vocational training institutions offer full competency-based training aligned with current job market demands (Ghana TVET Service/UNICEF study).
- In manufacturing, approximately **87%** of the workforce has at most basic education; only 2.1% have university/polytechnic training.
- In agriculture, approximately **95%** have no formal education or at best basic education — a key factor in the sector's lower productivity.
- **PwC Africa Workforce Survey 2025:** While 64% of African workers (including Ghana) reported using AI at work — 10 percentage points above the global average — only **35%** believe their current skills will remain relevant over the next three years.
- **85% of organizations** surveyed identified AI development skills as a priority; **86%** ranked cybersecurity capabilities as critical (SAP-commissioned study, 2025).

### Vocational Training Infrastructure

- Ghana has a **Commission for Technical and Vocational Education and Training (CTVET)**, but institutions face **severe infrastructure deficits** limiting capacity (NDPC 2024 Annual Progress Report).
- Skills training is **not aligned with job market demands**. Graduates often lack practical/applied competencies. Only 24% of vocational institutions offer full competency-based training (Ghana TVET Service/UNICEF study).
- Inadequate infrastructure, lack of qualified trainers, and high implementation costs make meaningful TVET reform difficult.
- Government investing **GHS 564.4M (~$53M)** in youth skills development programs in 2025.
- **GHS 410M** allocated for combined Adwumawura and National Apprenticeship Programme rollout.
- **GHS 100M** specifically allocated for the Adwumawura Programme from the 2025 Budget.
- Apprenticeship programs received over **230,000 applications** (120,000 for Adwumawura, 115,000 for NAP) as of August 2025, indicating massive demand for structured skills development.

### Digital Skills Gap

- **Acute shortage** of IT professionals across all levels. Ghana ranks **101st/139** on the Global Innovation Index 2025 (WIPO).
- Only ~**7.95 million** active social media users out of 35 million population. Digital literacy low in rural areas.
- **Internet penetration:** 69.9% (24.3 million users, DataReportal January 2025) — but quality and affordability remain barriers outside urban areas.
- **Smartphone penetration:** ~58% (Market Data Forecast, 2025).
- Fixed broadband penetration remains extremely low at ~**2–3%**. No 5G licenses approved yet.
- **Key skills initiatives (early-stage responses):**
  - **One Million Coders Program** (April 2025) — partnership with MTN for digital skills training in AI, data governance, cybersecurity.
  - **Huawei LEAP Programme** (launched 2022) — aims to train 100,000 people in ICT technologies by 2025.
  - **MTN Ghana Foundation Skills Academy** — targets 100,000+ young Ghanaians with digital and financial skills.
  - **eSkills4Jobs** — government/World Bank/Ghana-India Kofi Annan Centre collaboration targeting 5,000+ youth.
  - **IT For Youth Ghana** — NGO-led workshops on coding, data analytics, web development for underserved youth.
  - **Hacklab Foundation** — hosts Africa's largest annual hackathon (1,000+ tech professionals) in collaboration with IBM, KNUST, Standard Bank.

### Management & Business Administration

- Limited supply of experienced **C-suite and senior management talent**, particularly in sectors like healthcare administration, industrial management, and technology leadership.
- MBA programs exist (University of Ghana Business School, GIMPA, Ashesi University) but output insufficient for demand.
- **Diaspora returnees** often fill senior management roles in multinational operations — indicating a structural gap in domestic management pipeline.
- Particular shortages in: healthcare administration, industrial management, technology leadership, large-scale project management, supply chain management for multi-country operations.

### Mental Health Workforce Crisis

The mental health workforce gap deserves special attention given its severity:

- Only ~**80 psychiatrists** serve Ghana's 35 million population (Mental Health Authority, March 2025) — a ratio of approximately **1 psychiatrist per 437,500 people**. WHO recommends 1 per 100,000.
- An earlier WHO assessment cited as few as **39 psychiatrists** in active clinical practice (0.13 per 100,000 persons).
- An estimated **2.2–2.3 million Ghanaians** live with mental health conditions. One in five Ghanaians will experience a mental health condition in their lifetime.
- Only approximately **2–2.8%** of those suffering from mental illnesses have access to treatment (WHO/Global Health: Science and Practice).
- **Clinical psychologists and psychiatric nurses** are in critically short supply, particularly outside Greater Accra.
- Ghana has only **3 specialized psychiatric hospitals**, all located in the southern half of the country. Since the 2012 Mental Health Act, psychiatric units have been established in all 5 teaching hospitals and every district hospital, but staffing remains inadequate.
- Mental health receives less than **1.4%** of total government health expenditure, compared to WHO's recommended minimum of $2 per capita for low-income countries (Ghana allocates <$0.50).
- Psychotropic drug supply chains are unreliable. Medications frequently arrive near expiry. Mental health service users often must purchase medications from outside sources.

### Healthcare Workforce — The Employment-Deployment Paradox

Ghana faces a paradoxical healthcare workforce situation:

- **Over 70,000–100,000+ trained health professionals remain unemployed** while acute shortages persist at service delivery points. The figure has evolved through 2025: MoH spokesperson cited over **100,000** as of May 2025 (Graphic Online); Health Minister Kwabena Mintah Akandoh cited **over 70,000** in his July 2025 parliamentary statement (including over 900 unemployed medical doctors), later revised to **74,000** in October 2025 (Citi Newsroom, Ghanamma). The Minister warned the backlog could reach **180,000 by end-2028** without intervention. The discrepancy reflects different counting methodologies (some include 15,000 nurses given financial clearance in December 2024 but not yet on payroll). Regardless of the precise count, the paradox is clear: Ghana has both a massive healthcare workforce surplus *and* a critical healthcare delivery shortage simultaneously — a fiscal deployment failure, not a training failure.
- **Doctor-to-patient ratio:** ~1:8,000 — significantly below WHO's recommended 1:1,000.
- **Projected needs-based shortage by 2035:** 161,502 additional health professionals across 11 categories (PMC/modeling study).
- Ghana's 2020 supply satisfied only **67%** of aggregate needs-based requirements for primary healthcare.
- Key shortages: Doctors (56% deficit), pharmacists (73.7% deficit), medical laboratory scientists (70.4% deficit), community health nurses (41.4% deficit).
- Investment of ~**US$480 million** required in health professions education alone to correct supply-demand mismatches.
- **Brain drain:** Over 13 countries have formally expressed interest in recruiting Ghanaian nurses and health professionals (Minister of Health, 2025). Countries including UK, Canada, and Germany are actively recruiting from Ghana, compounding domestic shortages.
- **Managed migration** is being explored as a temporary strategy — exporting surplus trained professionals (particularly nurses) while deploying remaining professionals more efficiently domestically.

## Infrastructure Gaps

### Overview: Scale of the Infrastructure Challenge

Ghana's infrastructure needs are enormous. Government estimates suggest the country needs **US$37 billion annually for the next 30 years** to meet its development goals, with an additional **US$8 billion annually** required just to maintain existing infrastructure (Deputy Minister, GBC September 2025). **⚠️ This is a government aspiration figure, not an independently modeled or costed estimate.** At $37B/year for 30 years, it implies $1.11 trillion in cumulative infrastructure investment for an $82B economy — roughly 45% of GDP annually. No financing pathway for this scale of investment has been articulated. The figure is useful as an indicator of the scale of the perceived gap, not as a realistic investment target. The 2025 budget earmarked **GHS 13.85 billion** (~$1.3B) for the "Big Push" infrastructure programme — significant but approximately 3.5% of the stated annual need.

The NDPC 2024 Annual Progress Report provides a "sobering assessment" of development challenges, with infrastructure bottlenecks — especially in transport and energy — **slowing industrial growth and employment generation**.

**Government's "Big Push" priorities (2025 budget):**
1. Roads and Transport
2. Energy and Power Generation
3. Digital Infrastructure
4. Urban and Rural Development (housing, water, sanitation)

### Power Generation & Energy

- **Installed generation capacity (November 2024):** 5,260 MW excluding embedded generation (dependable: 4,856 MW). Including embedded: 5,507 MW (dependable: 5,060 MW). Reserve margin: 18%. (Ghana Energy Commission)
- **Generation mix (2024):** Thermal **66%**, Hydro **33%**, Renewables (solar PV, biogas) **<1%**.
- **Projected 2025 electricity consumption:** 25,836 GWh (+4.7% YoY).
- **Critical gas supply gap:** Energy Commission's 2025 Outlook projects that combined domestic and Nigerian gas imports will be **insufficient to meet projected demand throughout 2025**. This structural imbalance is the driver behind recurring "dumsor" (load-shedding), which returned in 2024.
- **Renewable energy deficit:** Solar at only **246 MW** (<1% of generation) despite massive potential (irradiance above 5 kWh/m²/day in key regions). Government target: **1,514 MW solar by 2030**; long-term ETIP target: 70% renewable electricity by 2060.
- **Energy sector debt:** ~**$3.1 billion** (Finance Minister, Parliament, March 2025). Total cleanup requirement: $3.7 billion. Government paid $1.47 billion in 2025 and fully restored the $597 million World Bank Partial Risk Guarantee.
- **ECG (Electricity Company of Ghana) distribution losses:** **30% in 2024** — chronically underfunded, a major source of system inefficiency. Government created a committee to evaluate private sector participation in ECG revitalization.
- **Electrification:** **89.03%** access rate (2024) — but last-mile quality unreliable. Off-grid communities still underserved. Government connected 276 rural communities to the National Grid in 2024. Target: 90% by end of 2025.
- **Battery storage:** **Virtually nonexistent** in Ghana — a critical gap as solar deployment scales.
- **EV infrastructure:** 2024 budget introduced duty waivers on EVs. Renewable Energy and Green Transition Fund includes EV charging stations. Infrastructure is at near-zero.

### Transportation

- **Roads carry ~95% of passenger traffic and ~97% of all movable freight** — making the road network the backbone of the economy.
- **Road network:** Approximately 65,725 km (2022, World Bank). Major highways (Accra-Kumasi, Accra-Cape Coast, Eastern Corridor N2) improved but secondary/feeder roads in poor condition, especially in northern regions.
- **Notable recent projects:** Pokuase four-tier interchange (reduced journey times from 2 hours to 30 minutes), Tema Motorway expansion, Eastern Corridor Road (695.6 km N2 — 74% improved as of late 2022).
- **Rail:** **Virtually non-existent** for freight/passenger at scale. The **Tema-Mpakadan Railway Line** (97.6 km standard gauge) was inaugurated in late 2024 but remained **non-operational as of mid-2025** due to theft, vandalism, and absence of a clear operational model. Ongoing plans for Accra-Kumasi railway and Western Railway Line rehabilitation — progress slow.
- **Ports:**
  - **Tema Port** (largest): Expanded with new MPS Terminal 3 (Bolloré/APM Terminals/GPHA). Increased container handling capacity. Ghana National Single Window deployed for customs streamlining.
  - **Takoradi Port:** Serves oil/mining sector and Western Region. Expansion projects underway.
  - Port efficiency improving but customs clearance times still lag regional competitors. Average import time: **~8–12 days** for documentary compliance; physical inspection adds time.
  - Tema and Takoradi serve as **key transit points** for landlocked ECOWAS states (Burkina Faso, Niger, Mali).
- **Air:** Kotoka International Airport (Accra) — daily direct flights to US (United Airlines Washington/Newark), UK, Europe, Middle East. Limited domestic air service. Limited cold chain infrastructure at airport.
- **Logistics costs:** Remain high relative to regional competitors. Trucking costs elevated by fuel prices, road conditions, and checkpoints.

### Digital Infrastructure

- **Submarine cable landings:** 5, with ~**2,905 Gbps** aggregate capacity. March 2024 cable severing exposed vulnerability and redundancy gaps.
- **Mobile penetration:** >135% (38.95 million connections; 113% of population).
- **3G/4G coverage:** Near-universal but quality/speed inconsistent outside urban areas. **5G licenses not yet approved.**
- **Fixed broadband penetration:** Extremely low (~**2–3%**). Median fixed download speed: **46.16 Mbps** (up 37% YoY, Ookla January 2025).
- **Data centers:** Only **one Tier IV data center** in West Africa — Onix Accra 1 (acquired by AIIM). Massive data center supply gap. Africa's data center market projected to grow at 14.46% CAGR through 2031, with demand expected to exceed supply by **300%** (IFC/AIIM).
- **Starlink:** Launched August 2024 — beginning to address rural connectivity.
- **Broadband wireless market shares:** Busy (81%), Surflin (11%), Telesol (6%), Broadband Home (2%) (U.S. Commercial Service).
- **Public institutions** struggle with outdated systems and limited connectivity, affecting service delivery and digital inclusion (NDPC 2024 APR).

### Water & Sanitation

- **Urban water supply coverage:** ~**77%**. Ghana Water Company (GWC) produces 871,496 m³/day vs. demand of 1.13 million m³ — a daily shortfall of approximately **258,500 m³**.
- **Rural water access:** Significantly lower than urban. Access to improved sanitation facilities is still below national targets.
- **Sanitation infrastructure:** "Inadequate across many regions" (NDPC 2024 APR). Limited sewerage systems. Poor waste disposal practices, particularly in urban slums and peri-urban communities.
- **Open defecation** persists in some areas — a public health and economic productivity issue.
- **WASH infrastructure gap** is cross-cutting — affecting health, education, and productivity outcomes. Waterborne diseases remain a significant burden.
- **Keta Water Supply Rehabilitation and Expansion Project:** Under construction — includes new water treatment facility (35,000 m³/day capacity) to serve 400,000+ residents in the Volta Region (Deutsche Bank/development finance).

### Housing

- **Deficit: 1.8–2.0 million units** (GSS 2021 census; World Bank). Some sources cite ~2 million when accounting for quality deficiency.
- **Annual housing need:** 70,000–133,000 units (World Bank). Demand increasing by ~100,000 homes per year. Government target: 200,000 units annually.
- **Current delivery:** Only ~**33%** of annual housing need met. Government has initiated construction of 10,720+ houses at various stages — only 5% of the 200,000 annual target (CAHF).
- **Structural mismatch:** Approximately **1.3 million dwelling units remain vacant** — revealing a mismatch between what is built (largely luxury) and what people can afford.
- **Mortgage penetration:** <**5%** of population. Average lending rate >30% (2024). Fewer than 12 of 23 banks offer mortgages.
- **Mid-market sweet spot:** GHS 200K–400K ($18K–$37K). Accra prime residential: $150K–$450K+.
- **Urban slums:** Estimated **33%** of Ghanaian urban residents live in slums (CAHF).
- Over **40%** of Accra's middle-income families rented homes in 2024.

### Cold Chain & Agricultural Logistics

- **30–40% post-harvest losses** in fruits and vegetables due to lack of cold storage and refrigerated transport.
- Cold chain revenue opportunity: **up to $900 million annually** (U.S. Commercial Service) **[commercial estimate — not a verified market size; see Report 02 addressable market decomposition]**.
- Limited cold storage at ports, airports, and distribution centers. Critical bottleneck for agricultural export competitiveness.
- **Irrigation:** Only ~**11,000 hectares** under irrigation out of 58,000 km² cultivated — minimal by international standards.
- Ghana imports **~$2.2 billion in food annually** — a significant portion of which could be substituted with better logistics and processing infrastructure.

### Construction Materials & Building Supply Chain

- **Heavy import dependency** for construction materials — cement components, steel, tiles, glass.
- Building materials subsector (excluding cement) valued at approximately **$3.8 billion** at end-2021 (GCB Bank). Ceramics and roofing sheets contributed $1.3 billion.
- Annual domestic spending on building materials: ~**$800 million**, with $280 million on imported raw materials (GCB Bank).
- **Construction sector market size:** Estimated $9–13.2 billion (ConstructAfrica, 2023), with CAGR of 13.8% from 2013–2021.
- Construction cost inflation eased to **9.7%** by September 2025 — significant retreat from double-digit pressures.

## Institutional & Regulatory Gaps

### Building Codes & Construction Standards

- National Building Regulations exist but enforcement is **inconsistent**. Unauthorized construction common, especially in peri-urban areas.
- **No mandatory energy efficiency standards** for buildings (though Energy Commission developing these).
- No standardized green building certification system widely adopted.
- Building permit processes vary significantly by municipal authority.
- **15% YoY increase** in residential building permits issued (GSS) — indicating growing formal construction but enforcement of compliance remains a challenge.

### Quality Assurance & Certification

- **Ghana Standards Authority (GSA)** operates but testing/certification infrastructure limited. Insufficient accredited testing laboratories for the breadth of products in the market.
- **Food and Drugs Authority (FDA)** regulates pharma and medical devices but capacity for market surveillance constrained.
- **Counterfeit products widespread** in pharmaceuticals, agro-chemicals, electronics — a major market integrity problem.
- **ISO certification:** Limited number of ISO-certified companies. Quality management training infrastructure underdeveloped. No widespread culture of ISO compliance in domestic manufacturing.
- **Metrology:** National measurement infrastructure inadequate for precision manufacturing and export-grade quality assurance.

### Financial Regulation

- **Bank of Ghana** has strengthened supervision post-2017 banking crisis (9 banks collapsed). Capital adequacy ratio improving to **17.5%** by April 2025 (with regulatory reliefs).
- **Fintech regulation** relatively advanced — Ghana ranked **#1 globally for mobile money regulation** (GSMA Regulatory Index 2024, score: 95.06%).
- **Insurance regulation** underdeveloped; penetration at just **1.0% of GDP** (BoG 2024 Financial Stability Review; **0.63%** under IFRS 17 methodology). Actuarial profession virtually nonexistent locally.
- **Securities market:** Ghana Stock Exchange small and historically illiquid, though GSE Composite returned **79.4% in 2025** and crossed 15,000 points for first time in March 2026.
- **Virtual Asset Service Providers Act (2025):** Passed — regulates cryptocurrency and digital asset businesses. eCedi (CBDC) under development.
- **NPL ratio:** 21.8% at end-December 2024, declining to 19.5% by October 2025. Excluding fully provisioned loss-category loans, adjusted NPL was 6.8%.

### Healthcare Regulation

- **Ghana FDA** handles medical device and pharmaceutical regulation but capacity for thorough pre-market assessment and post-market surveillance is limited.
- **No comprehensive telemedicine regulatory framework** yet (eHealth Policy in development).
- NHIS accreditation system exists but standards enforcement uneven.
- **Mental Health Act 2012 (Act 846):** Progressive legislation but implementation severely constrained by funding (less than 1.4% of health budget allocated to mental health) and personnel shortages.
- **Counterfeit pharmaceuticals** remain widespread — requires sustained market surveillance capacity building.
- **No comprehensive clinical trial regulation** — limiting pharmaceutical R&D investment.

### Environmental Regulation

- **Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)** exists but enforcement capacity **severely limited**.
- **Galamsey (illegal mining)** demonstrates the enforcement gap — estimated **$2.4 billion annual loss** despite government crackdowns. 70–80% of artisanal and small-scale mining is unlicensed/unregistered.
- Environmental impact assessments required but **compliance monitoring weak**.
- Water body contamination from illegal mining remains a major environmental and public health concern.
- **Climate vulnerability:** Ghana's infrastructure is identified as particularly vulnerable to climate change impacts — adaptation planning for energy, water, and transport sectors is in early stages.

### Professional Licensing

- Medical, legal, engineering, and accounting professions have licensing bodies. However, **capacity to evaluate and recognize foreign credentials varies** significantly.
- Mutual recognition of professional qualifications under AfCFTA still in early stages.
- **Professional bodies:** Ghana Medical and Dental Council, Ghana Bar Association, Ghana Institution of Engineers, Institute of Chartered Accountants Ghana — each with different processes for foreign credential assessment.

### Land Administration

- **Most cited structural obstacle** for real estate and infrastructure investment (U.S. State Dept, SECO, World Bank).
- **Dual system:** Customary (~80% of all land, managed by chiefs/stools/skins/families) and Statutory (~20%, managed by Lands Commission).
- Multiple claims to the same land parcel common, particularly in Greater Accra and peri-urban areas.
- **Land Act 2020** aimed to consolidate and modernize land laws but implementation remains incomplete.
- Land registration coverage limited — the Land Title Registration Law applies in Greater Accra and parts of Ashanti but not nationwide.
- Transaction costs are high due to extensive verification requirements — typically 3–8% of transaction value.

### Public Procurement & Project Execution

- Macroeconomic instability and **delayed project execution** have constrained expansion of job-intensive sectors (NDPC 2024 APR).
- Infrastructure projects frequently start in the last year of a government's term and may stop when a new party gains power — creating a cycle of incomplete projects.
- Public procurement reform is a structural benchmark under the IMF program.
- **PPP framework:** Recognized as essential ("indispensable" — Deputy Minister) given fiscal space limitations, but PPP legal and institutional framework still maturing.
- **Ghana Infrastructure Investment Fund (GIIF)** creating Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) to attract private capital, blended finance, and international development funding.

## Where Skills Transfer > Direct Service Delivery

In many domains, Ghana's greatest need is not for foreign companies to deliver services directly, but for **skills transfer and training infrastructure**:

- **Medical education and residency training** programs matter more than importing individual doctors. Ghana trains 500–600 doctors annually but needs to approximately double this rate, plus dramatically expand specialist training. Investment of ~$480M in health professions education could correct workforce gaps.
- **Vocational training systems** (German dual-education model, Swiss apprenticeship model, etc.) are more impactful than importing individual technicians. The National Apprenticeship Programme's scaling from 10,000 to 100,000 demonstrates government appetite for this model.
- **Agricultural extension services** and farmer training create more lasting value than foreign-operated farms. 95% of cultivated land is smallholder farms <10 hectares — transformation happens through farmer capacity, not land consolidation.
- **Software development bootcamps and apprenticeships** build permanent local capacity. The One Million Coders Program and Hacklab Foundation represent government and private sector buy-in for this approach.
- **Quality management training** and ISO certification support create self-sustaining systems — more valuable than one-off quality audits.
- **Mental health workforce training** — task-shifting programs (training primary care workers to deliver basic mental health services) have been adopted as national policy. International expertise in designing and running these programs is needed.
- **Construction skills certification** — apprenticeship programs for artisans (plumbing, electrical, welding) with internationally recognized certification would address both the housing deficit and youth unemployment.
- **Creative industry business skills** — over 70% of creative sector youth lack formal training. Business management, IP, and digital production skills training has outsized impact.

**The most valued foreign partners combine direct business operations with structured knowledge transfer.** This is both a **policy expectation** (under GIPC and sector-specific regulations) and a **practical competitive advantage** — companies that invest in local talent development build deeper relationships, stronger supply chains, and more sustainable market positions.

**Note:** In some domains, **direct foreign service delivery remains the appropriate model** alongside or ahead of skills transfer — particularly where specialized infrastructure or rare expertise is involved. Examples include: Tier IV data center design and operations management, specialized surgical procedures (oncology, cardiac), gold refinery engineering, battery storage system design, and rail signaling systems. In these cases, the skills transfer pathway takes years to produce competent practitioners, and immediate operational capacity requires direct foreign involvement. The skills transfer framing applies most strongly where Ghana has a training pipeline that can be accelerated (healthcare, vocational trades, software development, agricultural extension) rather than where capabilities must be built from near-zero.

**Government signal strength:** The creation of a dedicated **Ministry of Youth Development and Empowerment** under the Mahama administration, with combined 2025 program allocations exceeding **GHS 974 million** ($90M+) for youth skills development, Adwumawura, and National Apprenticeship Programme, signals that skills transfer partners will find receptive institutional counterparts and potential co-investment opportunities.
