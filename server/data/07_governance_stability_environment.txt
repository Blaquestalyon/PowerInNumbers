## Political Stability & Democratic Track Record

Ghana is widely regarded as one of **Africa's most stable democracies** and has been a model of democratic governance in West Africa for over three decades.

### Electoral History
- **Consecutive peaceful transitions:** Ghana has conducted peaceful, democratic transfers of power in **1992, 2000, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, and 2024** — seven consecutive democratic elections under the Fourth Republic, with no party holding power for more than two consecutive terms.
- **2024 election:** Former President **John Dramani Mahama (NDC)** defeated incumbent Vice President Mahamudu Bawumia (NPP) with **56.42% of the vote** vs. 41.75% (updated EC results from 275 of 276 constituencies; initial declaration from 267 constituencies showed 56.55%/41.61%) — the largest margin in 24 years. **Voter turnout: 60.9%** (initial declaration from 267 constituencies); updated to **63.97%** from 275 constituencies (EC, January 2025). Bawumia conceded defeat on December 8, the day after the election.
- **Parliamentary landslide:** NDC won **184 out of 276 seats** (EC-reported figure; IPU records 183, IDEA records 186 — reflecting different stages of result adjudication), giving approximately a two-thirds majority, with NPP securing 88 seats and independents winning 4. This gives Mahama an exceptionally strong mandate for legislative action.
- **Historic milestone:** **Jane Naana Opoku-Agyemang** became Ghana's first female Vice President, sworn in with Mahama on January 7, 2025.
- **Multi-party system:** Dominated by NPP (center-right) and NDC (center-left) in a competitive two-party democracy. 12 presidential candidates contested the 2024 election; 801 candidates contested parliamentary seats.
- **Military coups:** Last military government ended in 1992 with the transition to the Fourth Republic. Ghana has not experienced a successful coup in over **33 years** — exceptional in the West African context.
- **Constitutional framework:** 1992 Constitution provides for separation of powers, independent judiciary, and term-limited presidency (2 terms of 4 years). Amended via referendum.
- **Electoral Commission:** Independent body (chaired by Jean Mensa for 2024 election) that manages elections. Electronic voter register and biometric verification system deployed at all 40,648 polling stations.

### Regional Context
Ghana's democratic stability stands out sharply against neighboring instability:
- **Burkina Faso** — military coup September 2022 (second coup in 8 months)
- **Mali** — military coup May 2021
- **Niger** — military coup July 2023
- **Guinea** — military coup September 2021

Ghana serves as a regional anchor of stability and is frequently cited by the U.S. State Department, World Bank, and African Union as the model for democratic governance in West Africa. The country hosts several international organizations and diplomatic missions specifically because of its stability profile.

### Post-Election Policy Direction (Mahama Administration, 2025–)
The Mahama administration entered office with a comprehensive reform agenda centered on economic reset:

- **National Economic Dialogue** (March 3–4, 2025): Chaired by Dr. Ishmael Yamson, bringing together industry experts and academia. Produced recommendations on governance reform, SOE restructuring (particularly COCOBOD), agro-processing, and technological innovation.
- **2025 Budget** (March 2025): Themed "Resetting Ghana – Building the Economy We Want Together." Key measures include abolition of the E-Levy, betting tax, and emissions tax (all three signed into law by President Mahama on **April 2, 2025**); fiscal reset targeting primary surplus; reoriented spending toward human development, infrastructure, and production.
- **Mid-Year Fiscal Policy Review** (July 24, 2025): Finance Minister Dr. Cassiel Ato Forson reported primary budget surplus of 1.1% of GDP (vs. 0.4% IMF target) and overall deficit of just 0.7% (vs. 1.8% target).
- **24-Hour Economy Policy**: Signature initiative to stimulate round-the-clock economic activity in manufacturing and services using three-shift system.
- **Chief Executive Summit** for state-owned enterprises: Aligning SOE operations with government policy priorities.
- **120-Day Social Contract**: Administration committed to specific deliverables within first 120 days.

### Policy Continuity Risks
- **Government-party dynamics:** NDC's two-thirds parliamentary majority enables ambitious legislation but could also reduce checks on executive power.
- **Post-election tensions:** Some localized violence reported after election results (destruction of EC office in Damongo; clashes in several constituencies). Both candidates condemned violence. USIP noted "lower voter turnout in the 2024 election is just as indicative of the national mood as the outcome."
- **Anti-LGBTQ legislation:** Parliament passed Human Sexual Rights and Family Values Bill in February 2024; Akufo-Addo did not sign before leaving office. New version reintroduced February 2025 under Mahama administration. International criticism from Western governments and human rights organizations.
- **Fiscal discipline test:** IMF program conditionality constrains policy flexibility. Whether fiscal discipline survives political pressures through the election cycle (next election 2028) is the key sustainability question.

## Rule of Law

### Judicial System
- **Constitutional guarantee:** Independent judiciary guaranteed under the 1992 Constitution. Supreme Court is the highest court.
- **Court structure:** Supreme Court → Court of Appeal → High Court (with specialized divisions) → Circuit Courts → District Courts.
- **Commercial Division of the High Court:** Handles business disputes. **Specialized Commercial Court in Accra** provides dedicated commercial dispute resolution.
- **Challenges:** Court backlogs can delay commercial dispute resolution. Enforcement of judgments can be slow. Legal costs relatively high for SMEs. Freedom House (2025) notes "some weaknesses in judicial independence."
- **Corruption in judiciary:** Occasional scandals have affected public confidence; however, the judiciary is generally regarded as functional compared to regional peers.

### Arbitration & Dispute Resolution
- **ICSID:** Ghana is a signatory to the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes convention.
- **New York Convention:** Foreign arbitral awards are enforceable in Ghana, though enforcement can be slow due to judicial system capacity constraints.
- **Ghana Arbitration Centre:** Provides domestic arbitration services.
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution Act 2010 (Act 798):** Comprehensive framework for mediation and arbitration in Ghana.
- **International arbitration:** Available through ICSID, ICC, or LCIA for foreign investors under bilateral investment treaties.
- **UNCITRAL Model Law:** Ghana's arbitration framework draws on UNCITRAL principles.

### Property Rights
- **Constitutional Protection:** The Constitution protects against expropriation without fair compensation. GIPC Act provides additional investor protections.
- **Land Tenure Complexity:** Dual system — customary (~80%) and statutory (~20%) — creates title uncertainty. Multiple claims to the same land parcel are common, particularly in Greater Accra and peri-urban areas. The most cited structural obstacle for real estate investment by foreign investors (U.S. State Dept, SECO, World Bank).
- **Land Act 2020:** Aimed to consolidate and modernize land laws but implementation remains incomplete. Land Title Registration Law applies in Greater Accra and parts of Ashanti but not nationwide.
- **Foreigners cannot own freehold land** — maximum leasehold of 50 years, renewable. Joint ventures with Ghanaian partners provide structural solutions.
- **Intellectual Property:** Ghana is a WIPO member with patent (20 years), trademark (10 years renewable), and copyright (life + 70 years) protections. Enforcement weak — counterfeit goods widespread.
- **BIT Protections:** Active bilateral investment treaties with major investor countries provide international arbitration recourse. **Note: No U.S.–Ghana BIT exists** — negotiations opened in 2008 but never concluded.

## Security Environment

### Overall Assessment
Ghana is one of the **safest countries in West Africa** for business operations and personal security. It does not face the insurgency, terrorism, or large-scale conflict affecting Sahel neighbors.

### Internal Security
- **General safety:** Safe for business operations and travel. Foreign executives routinely work and live in Accra, Kumasi, and other major cities without security incidents.
- **Urban crime:** Petty crime (theft, pickpocketing) exists in major cities (Accra, Kumasi) but violent crime rates are low by regional standards. Armed robbery incidents occur but are not systematically targeting foreign businesses.
- **Police capacity:** Ghana Police Service is generally functional but resource-constrained. Private security services widely available and commonly used by businesses.
- **Cybercrime:** Rising concern — particularly online fraud (sakawa). Cyber Security Authority established with licensing and compliance requirements.

### Northern Border Risk
- **Sahel spillover:** Some concern about instability spillover from Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger. Government has increased security presence in northern regions.
- **Bawku conflict:** Protracted chieftaincy dispute in the Bawku area (Upper East Region) causes periodic violence. Curfew in place. This is a localized issue that does not affect business centers.
- **IMF 5th Review (December 2025):** Noted the need to accommodate "security needs" in the 2026 budget — signaling government awareness of rising security expenditure requirements.

### Galamsey (Illegal Mining)
- **Significant security/environmental issue** in mining regions. Estimated **$2.4 billion annually** lost to illegal mining activities.
- Government enforcement campaigns ongoing but inconsistent. President Mahama inaugurated an anti-illegal-gold-trade task force (July 8, 2025).
- Galamsey destroys water bodies, cocoa farms, and forest reserves — Ghana registered the largest deterioration on the continent in the IIAG Sustainable Environment sub-category.
- Anti-galamsey protests in September 2024 led to police clashes and over 50 arrests.
- **Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)** exists but enforcement capacity severely limited.

### Terrorism Risk
- **Low.** Ghana has not experienced significant terrorist attacks. No active insurgency.
- **Regional monitoring:** Ghana is part of the Accra Initiative (with Côte d'Ivoire, Togo, Benin, Burkina Faso) for cooperation on preventing terrorist spillover from the Sahel.
- **U.S. assessment:** The U.S. has not designated any terrorist threats specific to Ghana. Travel advisories for Ghana are among the lowest-risk in West Africa.

## Press Freedom & Information Access

- **Press Freedom:** Ghana has a vibrant and diverse media landscape. Multiple private TV/radio stations, newspapers, and online news platforms. Media operates freely and is often critical of government.
- **Freedom House Rating:** **"Free"** — one of the few African countries to achieve this classification consistently.
- **Regional comparison:** Ghana is routinely ranked among the **top 3–5 African countries** for press freedom by both Freedom House and Reporters Without Borders.
- **Media landscape:** Over 500 radio stations, dozens of TV channels (including Joy News, Citi TV, GTV, TV3, UTV, Metro TV), and active online media (GhanaWeb, MyJoyOnline, Citi Newsroom, Graphic Online, Modern Ghana).
- **Internet Freedom:** Generally unrestricted. No systematic internet censorship or social media shutdowns during elections (unlike some regional peers).
- **Right to Information Act 2019 (Act 989):** Provides framework for public access to government information, though implementation and awareness remain limited.
- **Concerns (Freedom House 2025):**
  - Blogger sentenced to one month in prison for false news causing a bank run (August 2024)
  - Opposition activist arrested and charged with publishing false news (May 2025) related to social media posts about the chief justice
  - AI-generated disinformation during the 2024 election campaign (deepfake audios and fabricated news cards)
  - Anti-LGBTQ legislation (if signed) could criminalize online content positively portraying LGBTQ relationships

## Government Digital Transformation

### Platforms and Systems
- **Ghana.Gov Platform:** Integrated digital government services platform. Over **80% of government-to-person payments now digital** (via mobile money). Enables government service delivery, tax payments, and permit applications.
- **Ghana Card (National ID):** Biometric national identification system with growing coverage. Simplifies financial services onboarding, KYC processes, and government service access. Increasingly used as primary identity document.
- **Tax Administration:** Ghana Revenue Authority digitizing tax filing and collection. TIN system modernized. Taxpayer registry cleansing underway (IMF 5th Review structural benchmark — target: end-May 2026 for Treasury Single Account integration).
- **Business Registration:** Registrar General's Department/Office of the Registrar of Companies (ORC) offering online incorporation. Company registration can be completed in 1–5 business days.
- **GIFMIS:** Government Integrated Financial Management Information System being linked to Treasury Single Account for better public financial management.
- **Ghana Business Regulatory Reforms Portal (brr.gov.gh):** Active public consultation platform for pending legislation including GIPC Bill, Special Economic Zones Bill, and Occupational Safety and Health Bill.
- **Ghana National Single Window:** Digital customs clearance platform reducing processing times for imports and exports.

### Digital Economy Infrastructure
- **Mobile money:** 74.1 million registered accounts (February 2025); GH₵3.01 trillion in transaction value in 2024 (+57% YoY). Average monthly transaction value increased from GH₵13 billion in 2017 to GH₵270 billion by 2024.
- **Internet penetration:** ~70% (24.3 million users, DataReportal January 2025).
- **E-Levy abolished** in early 2025 — removed friction from mobile transactions, boosting digital economy activity.
- **eCedi (CBDC):** Under development by Bank of Ghana.
- **Virtual Asset Service Providers Act (2025):** Regulatory framework for cryptocurrency and digital asset businesses enacted.

- **Next actions:** Market participants expect potential upgrade reviews following the 6th and final IMF ECF review (expected ~April 2026) and continued fiscal discipline.

## Major Policy Initiatives Affecting Business Environment

### Enacted/Implemented (2024–2025)
1. **E-Levy Abolition** — Electronic transaction levy scrapped in early 2025, removing friction from mobile money and digital transactions.
2. **Betting tax and emissions tax abolition** — Both repealed alongside the E-Levy; all three signed into law on **April 2, 2025** (Emissions Levy (Repeal) Bill, 2025; Income Tax (Amendment) Bill, 2025 — Ghana Presidency; GNA).
3. **Fiscal Responsibility Framework** — Enacted under IMF program. Targets primary surplus of 1.5% GDP and expenditure growth <10% annually. 2025 outturn exceeded targets significantly.
4. **24-Hour Economy Policy** — Government initiative to create flexible, round-the-clock economic activity to boost employment and production.
5. **Gold-for-Reserves (GoldBod) Program** — Purchasing artisanal/small-scale gold for central bank reserves. Generated ~$10B in FX for full-year 2025. Strengthened reserves and stabilized cedi.
6. **National AI Strategy** (May 2025) — Positions Ghana as "Africa's AI Hub" within 4 years. Four pillars: Data as National Asset, Compute Power, Talent Development (One Million Coders Program), AI Innovation.
7. **Virtual Asset Service Providers Act** (2025) — Regulatory framework for cryptocurrency/digital assets.
8. **Affirmative Action Act** (September 2024) — Targets gender equity in political posts, corporate leadership by 2030.
9. **Energy Tariff Adjustments** — Electricity prices adjusted under IMF program to reduce energy sector debt ($3.1B; total cleanup $3.7B).
10. **State-Owned Bank Recapitalization** — Budget resources allocated to recapitalize state banks post-domestic debt exchange. Ghana Financial Stability Fund ($750M) deployed ~$450M to 8 institutions.
11. **Revenue Assurance Strategy** — Launched to tighten tax compliance and widen the tax net.
12. **Planting for Food and Jobs 3.0** — Revised agricultural support program with enhanced irrigation, seed/fertilizer distribution, and value-addition focus.

### Pending/In Progress (2025–2026)
1. **Ghana Investment Promotion Authority Bill, 2025** — Comprehensive GIPC Act overhaul removing minimum capital requirements, opening 8 reserved sectors, introducing risk-based screening. Before Parliament's Trade, Industry, and Tourism Committee. If passed, the most significant investment policy overhaul in over a decade.
2. **Special Economic Zones Bill, 2025** — Modernizes free zones framework; aligns with AfCFTA objectives.
3. **Treasury Single Account integration** (target: end-May 2026) — Critical IMF structural benchmark.
4. **Public procurement reform** — Ongoing under IMF conditionality.
5. **Statutory funds reform** — Reducing earmarked transfers to improve fiscal flexibility.
6. **Independent Emoluments Commission** — Being established to reform public sector pay.
7. **Made-in-Ghana Procurement List** — Forthcoming; requires presidential approval before importing goods available locally.
8. **Electronic Transactions Bill, 2025** — Comprehensive digital governance legislation revision.
9. **Business Regulatory Reform Commission Bill, 2024** — Establishing permanent regulatory quality oversight body.
10. **AfCFTA investment protocol implementation** — Ghana-specific implementation of continental investment framework.

### Ghana's Competitive Advantages vs. Peers
- **Democratic stability** — unmatched in West Africa; 7 consecutive peaceful elections
- **English-speaking** — reduces entry barrier for US/UK investors
- **AfCFTA Secretariat host** — institutional influence over continental trade rules
- **Manageable size** — 35M population is large enough for market scale, small enough for relationship-based business
- **Direct US flights** — United Airlines Accra–Washington Dulles/Newark; improves connectivity for US-based investors
- **Improving macro fundamentals** — fastest post-default credit recovery in recent African history

- **Freedom House "Free" status** — one of very few in Africa

### Ghana's Competitive Disadvantages vs. Peers
- **Higher operating costs** than some competitors (Nigeria offers cheaper labor; Rwanda offers simpler regulation)
- **Energy reliability** remains inferior to Kenya and Rwanda; ECG distribution losses at 30% in 2024
- **Smaller market** than Nigeria (230M) for purely domestic-focused businesses
- **Land administration** more complex than Rwanda's digitized system; most cited structural obstacle for real estate investors
- **Declining peace index** — GPI fell from 38th (2021) to 61st (2025); Bawku conflict and galamsey tensions
- **Lingering post-crisis caution** — despite upgrades, credit ratings still speculative grade (B-)
- **No U.S.–Ghana BIT or tax treaty** — American investors face higher withholding taxes and rely on GIPC Act protections rather than treaty-level guarantees
- **AGOA uncertainty** — one-year extension only; long-term U.S. market access uncertain

## Investor Perception & Business Environment Signals

### Positive Signals (2025–2026)
- **FDI surge:** GIPC-registered FDI in H1 2025 reached $862.96 million — up **381.91%** from H1 2024. 76 projects registered.
- **Stock market boom:** GSE Composite Index returned **79.4% in 2025** (highest annual gain since 2004). GSE-CI crossed 15,000 points for the first time in March 2026.
- **Bond market re-entry:** Government exploring cautious return to international capital markets post-restructuring.
- **Business confidence:** World Bank 9th Ghana Economic Update (June 2025) noted government's "strong mandate offers renewed momentum for reforms."
- **IMF endorsement:** "Generally satisfactory" performance assessment at 5th review.
- **Diaspora investment:** Over 1,500 Black Americans relocated to Ghana since Year of Return (2019). Growing international interest in Accra as business and lifestyle hub.

### Concern Areas
- **"Uneven enforcement of rules"** remains the primary complaint from foreign companies (SECO Economic Report Ghana, 2025)
- **Energy sector debt** ($3.1B; total cleanup $3.7B) creates risk of further tariff increases
- **Tax policy uncertainty** — E-Levy abolished (positive), but government revenue needs ongoing; new levies/taxes could emerge. Growth and Sustainability Levy increased from 1% to 3% for mining companies.
- **Local content requirements** continue to evolve, particularly in oil & gas and mining
- **Intellectual property enforcement** weak — counterfeit goods widespread
- **Bureaucratic processes** can be slow; "patience and relationships" required (consistently cited by experienced investors)
- **Currency risk** — despite remarkable 2025 appreciation (~40% per Ministry of Finance methodology; the precise figure varies from ~32.5% to ~48% depending on whether measured in exchange-rate terms or cedi purchasing-power terms), the cedi has a long-term depreciation trend (GHS 1.5/USD in 2013 → GHS 16.0/USD in November 2024)

## Summary: Business Environment Assessment (March 2026)

**Ghana in March 2026 is at a pivotal inflection point.** The country has emerged from a severe debt crisis (2022–2024) with remarkable speed — inflation from 54% to 3.3%, cedi recovered ~40% (per MoF methodology; varies by measurement approach), all three rating agencies upgraded, IMF program nearing completion with exceptional fiscal outperformance, reserves at record $13.8 billion, and the stock market at all-time highs.

The new Mahama administration entered office with the largest electoral mandate in 24 years and a two-thirds parliamentary majority, pursuing ambitious reform: removing investment barriers, abolishing transaction levies, launching the National AI Strategy, restructuring SOEs, and targeting a 24-hour economy. The 2025 fiscal outturn (primary surplus of 2.6% GDP vs. 1.5% target, overall deficit just 1.0% vs. 2.8% target) demonstrates unusual fiscal discipline for a first-year administration.

**For foreign investors, the calculus is:**

- **Macro risk is declining rapidly** but not eliminated. Fiscal discipline must survive political cycles. Heavy dependence on gold exports for the 2025 outperformance creates commodity vulnerability. IMF has flagged $214M in GoldBod trading losses through Q3 2025 alone (IMF Fifth Review, Country Report No. 25/343; full-year losses likely higher). GoldBod disputes the "loss" characterization, attributing the figure to inherent costs of purchasing doré gold at a 3–5% discount to refined spot prices.
- **Regulatory reform is directionally positive** but execution matters more than announcement. The pending GIPC overhaul, SEZ modernization, and digital economy legislation would materially improve the investment climate if enacted and implemented.
- **Market opportunity is genuine** — 35M people, $83B economy, gateway to 1.4B AfCFTA market, duty-free access to the EU (450M) and UK (67M), massive infrastructure and housing deficits to fill, and deep development gaps across healthcare, education, technology, and agriculture.
- **Governance quality is above-average for Sub-Saharan Africa** across virtually every major index — but the declining trends in the Global Peace Index and Mo Ibrahim "Warning Signs" classification warrant monitoring.
- **Competitive positioning requires patience, relationships, and local partnership.** Ghana rewards investors who commit for the long term and invest in local capacity. Skills transfer and formalization of systems are often more valued than direct service delivery.
- **The window is open.** Post-crisis valuations, reform momentum, recovering investor confidence, and a government with an unusually strong legislative mandate create favorable entry conditions. These conditions will not last indefinitely — as macro fundamentals normalize, first-mover advantages diminish.
